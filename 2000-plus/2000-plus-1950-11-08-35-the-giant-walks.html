Picture this: November 8th, 1950. Across America, families huddled around their radios as an unsettling sound effect—the thunderous, deliberate footsteps of something impossibly massive—echoed from their speakers. In "The Giant Walks," listeners were transported to a world where the impossible had become terrifyingly real. This episode presents a chilling meditation on scale, power, and human helplessness as our civilization confronts an entity beyond comprehension. The dramatic tension builds methodically as characters grapple with the implications of a being so colossal that our mightiest weapons seem like toys, our cities mere dollhouses. What unfolds is pure science fiction anxiety—that distinctive 1950s dread of the unknown, amplified by nothing but skilled voice actors, sound design, and the listener's own vivid imagination.

2000 Plus inhabited a unique space in radio's golden age. Premiering on the Mutual Network in 1950, just as television was beginning to challenge radio's dominance, the show defiantly proved that science fiction's possibilities were limitless without visual constraints. Rather than competing with TV's emerging special effects, 2000 Plus leveraged radio's greatest strength: the human imagination. Each week transported listeners into futures both wondrous and terrifying, exploring humanity's relationship with technology, space travel, and cosmic forces beyond our control. "The Giant Walks" exemplifies this formula—a concept so visually spectacular that lesser mediums might struggle to convey its awesome, terrible scope.

Don't miss this extraordinary artifact of speculative fiction from radio's twilight era. Tune in to 2000 Plus and experience the particular thrill that only old-time radio can deliver: pure, unadulterated storytelling that demands your complete attention and rewards it with unforgettable drama.
