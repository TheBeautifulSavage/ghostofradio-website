Picture this: it's a moonless evening, and Lou Costello finds himself separated from his partner Bud Abbott in the corridors of a creaking Victorian mansion, where every shadow conceals a potential menace. The floorboards groan beneath uncertain feet, and mysterious voices echo through the darkness—but are they genuine phantoms or merely the product of the house's sinister reputation? In this delightfully spine-tingling episode, the comedy goldmine of miscommunication reaches new heights as Lou stumbles from room to room, his panicked exclamations punctuating the eerie atmosphere with perfectly-timed comedy that somehow makes the supernatural seem both terrifying and hilarious. Bud's smooth confidence and Lou's desperate vulnerability create an unstoppable comedic engine, especially when Lou encounters what he believes are genuine ghosts, only to discover increasingly ridiculous (and increasingly funny) explanations for the mysterious happenings. The script brilliantly weaves genuine suspense with belly laughs, keeping listeners on the edge of their seats throughout.

Abbott and Costello represented the golden age of American comedy, when radio was the supreme form of entertainment and their rapid-fire banter had become as familiar to American audiences as their own family conversations. This 1940s gem captures the duo at their comedic peak, when their chemistry was so finely-tuned that even a simple haunted house premise became a vehicle for timeless humor. Their success on radio would later translate to film and television, but something irreplaceable lives in these original broadcasts—the immediacy of live performance, the intimacy of voices in the darkness.

Don your headphones and prepare for a night of supernatural shenanigans. Whether you're a longtime devotee or discovering Abbott and Costello for the first time, this haunted house adventure proves why they remain comedy legends nearly a century later.
