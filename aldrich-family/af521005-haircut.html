Step into the Aldrich household on an ordinary afternoon when young Henry's simple decision to get a haircut spirals into the kind of domestic comedy that made America fall in love with this family. As the barber's scissors snip away, so too does Henry's composure—transformed by a cut far more drastic than he'd bargained for. The real entertainment unfolds at home, where Sam and Kate Aldrich must contend with their mortified son, each family member offering their own remedy for the disaster. You'll hear the crackle of genuine family dynamics, the warm but exasperated parental wisdom, and Henry's teenage anguish rendered with perfect comedic timing. It's the kind of episode that resonates across generations because it captures that universal teenage dread of standing out from the crowd, all wrapped in sparkling dialogue and impeccable ensemble chemistry.

The Aldrich Family thrived because it understood something fundamental about American radio audiences: they craved authentic family moments served with light humor and genuine warmth. Beginning in 1939, this NBC favorite ran for fourteen seasons precisely because it avoided saccharine sentiment while celebrating the ordinary crises that bind households together. Henry Aldrich, played by Ezra Stone, became an iconic character—the perpetually well-meaning adolescent whose innocent mistakes cascade into hilarious complications. The supporting cast, including House Jameson and Mary Shipp as the patient parents, created a family unit that listeners genuinely cared about.

In an era before television domesticated the family comedy, radio's Aldriches brought laughter directly into living rooms across the nation. "Haircut" is quintessential comfort listening—proof that the smallest domestic storms, when filtered through genuine affection and sharp writing, become unforgettable entertainment. Tune in and rediscover why this gentle comedy endured.
