Picture this: it's a typical evening in the Aldrich household, and young Henry has just announced—with the kind of casual bombshell delivery only a teenager can manage—that he needs a tuxedo for the school formal. What follows is a delightful twenty-minute whirlwind of domestic chaos as Mr. Aldrich attempts to navigate the minefield of his son's social obligations. From the cluttered bedroom where the search begins, listeners are transported into a world of misplaced rental forms, wildly inflated teenage expectations, and the gentle exasperation of a father trying to understand why a simple suit won't suffice. The writing crackles with authentic comedy drawn from the real frustrations of family life, punctuated by Ezra Stone's impeccable timing as the earnest, perpetually bewildered Henry, and the warm, bemused presence of Houseman as the perpetually patient patriarch.

The Aldrich Family became a phenomenon because it captured something radio audiences desperately craved during the 1940s: recognition. Here was their own lives reflected back at them—the small arguments, the generational misunderstandings, the underlying affection that held it all together. The show's success on radio led to a film series and later television adaptations, but the original broadcasts possessed an intimacy no other medium could quite replicate. These episodes became a cultural touchstone for American family life during the Depression and war years.

Tune in for this gem of wartime radio comedy, where the simple question of formal wear becomes a gateway into the heart of what made America's favorite family so beloved. You'll laugh, you'll recognize yourself, and you might even understand your own parents a little better.
