The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

"Home Is Where The Ghost Is," a haunting episode of CBS Radio Mystery Theater, aired on November 13, 1975, just as the United States was bracing for an economic downturn and the Vietnam War's endgame was unfolding. The story revolves around Barbara, a young woman who returns to her family's rural farmhouse after a decade away, only to find it inhabited by the ghost of her deceased brother. As Barbara navigates the eerie landscape of her childhood home, she uncovers secrets and lies that threaten to upend her entire existence.

Through Barbara's character, the show skillfully explores themes of trauma, grief, and the complexities of family relationships. The performances by actress Carolyn Clark Keene (who played Barbara) and Richard Sheridan (the narrator) are remarkable for their nuance and emotional depth, drawing listeners into the world of the story. The episode's atmospheric sound design, courtesy of James F. Clarke and David McCallum, perfectly captures the foreboding, isolated atmosphere of the farmhouse, making it easy to become fully immersed in the narrative.

As Barbara delves deeper into her family's past, she must confront the traumas that have haunted them for years – including a mysterious fire that took her brother's life. The slow-burning tension builds towards a stunning revelation that ties everything together, leaving listeners on the edge of their seats as they try to piece together the mystery alongside Barbara.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

In November 1975, the United States was in the midst of a severe economic recession, which had begun in 1973 and would last for several years. The Vietnam War had also reached its final stages, with President Gerald Ford's administration working towards a withdrawal of American troops. The Iran hostage crisis, meanwhile, was beginning to take center stage, with anti-American sentiment on the rise.

Domestically, Americans were grappling with rising crime rates, social unrest, and growing concerns about the environment. The energy crisis of 1973-74 had left many households struggling to afford basic necessities like gasoline and electricity. For young people like Barbara, who have grown up during these tumultuous years, finding their place in the world is a constant challenge.

In this context, "Home Is Where The Ghost Is" resonates as a story about trauma, family secrets, and the search for identity – all set against a backdrop of uncertainty and upheaval. By tapping into the fears, anxieties, and aspirations of its audience, the show skillfully captures the mood of a nation at a crossroads.

WHY IT MATTERS:

One aspect that stands out about "Home Is Where The Ghost Is" is its masterful use of suspense and plot twists to keep listeners engaged. Director Jeff Corey's deft handling of pacing and atmosphere ensures that the story unfolds slowly but inexorably, drawing the audience into Barbara's world. But it's not just the narrative itself – the show's craftsmanship also matters here.

The episode showcases the exceptional skill of its writers, who drew upon real-life experiences to craft a story that feels both deeply personal and universally relatable. The performances, too, are noteworthy for their emotional authenticity, which brings Barbara's journey from isolated outsider to vulnerable insider into stark relief. As we listen to "Home Is Where The Ghost Is" today, we're reminded of the enduring power of radio drama as a medium – its ability to evoke powerful emotions, create lasting impressions, and capture the complexities of human experience in all their messy, beautiful glory.
