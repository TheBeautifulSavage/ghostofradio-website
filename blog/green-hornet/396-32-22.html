The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

"The Green Hornet" crashes back into action in episode 396, "32-22," which aired on November 23, 1939. The script masterfully weaves together elements of espionage, crime, and romance as Britt Reid, a.k.a. the Green Hornet, finds himself entangled in a web of intrigue involving the mysterious and beautiful Kitty Kato. As tensions rise between the United States and Nazi Germany, the Hornet's adventures take on a sense of urgency and foreboding. The episode's central plot revolves around a stolen formula for a new type of explosive, which our heroes must navigate through a complex network of underworld figures and double agents.

The atmosphere is tense, with the sound design and music perfectly capturing the sense of danger and uncertainty that pervaded 1939 America. The Green Hornet's lair, a sleek and modernist space filled with gadgets and gizmos, feels like a beacon of hope in a world on the brink of war. Meanwhile, Kitty Kato, a.k.a. "The Lady in Red," emerges as a fully realized character, her beauty and brains serving as a perfect counterbalance to Britt's brawn. As their relationship deepens, so too does the stakes, making for a compelling narrative that keeps listeners on the edge of their seats.

Throughout the episode, Wilson Bennett, the creator of "The Green Hornet," masterfully balances action, drama, and humor, expertly juggling the tone to create an experience that feels both thrilling and intimate. The result is an episode that's as engaging today as it was when it first aired nearly a century ago.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

As 1939 dawned on America, the world teetered on the brink of war. The Spanish Civil War had just come to a close, with fascist forces emerging victorious in Spain. In Europe, tensions between Nazi Germany and democratic nations like France and the United Kingdom continued to escalate, while in the Far East, Japan was increasingly aggressive towards China. Meanwhile, back home, America was gripped by the Great Depression, which had left millions without work or a safety net.

In this climate of fear and uncertainty, "The Green Hornet" offered listeners an escape into a world of high adventure and derring-do. Radio dramas like "The Green Hornet" provided a unique form of escapism, allowing audiences to temporarily forget their worries about the world outside. The format – which combined elements of mystery, action, and romance with witty banter and comedic relief – proved particularly appealing in 1939.

Radio drama resonated with listeners in this period for several reasons. Firstly, it was an accessible form of entertainment that could be enjoyed by people from all walks of life. With radio waves able to penetrate even the most rural areas, broadcasts like "The Green Hornet" reached audiences across the United States and beyond. Secondly, radio drama offered a sense of community and shared experience that's hard to find elsewhere. Listeners gathered around their radios each evening, sharing in the excitement and suspense of the story unfolding before them.

WHY IT MATTERS:

From a craft perspective, "32-22" showcases Wilson Bennett's skill as a writer and creator of radio drama. The episode's intricate plot is expertly woven from multiple threads, requiring close attention and active listening to fully appreciate. At its core, the Green Hornet series represented a model for serialized storytelling that influenced generations of writers and producers.

Today, "32-22" remains an essential listen for anyone interested in the history of radio drama or the evolution of popular entertainment. Its blend of action, romance, and suspense continues to captivate listeners, offering a thrilling taste of what it was like to be a part of America's radio-going public during one of its most tumultuous periods.
