The fog rolls thick through Boston's streets on this August evening in 1945, and with it comes an irresistible proposition for our reformed jewel thief. When a prominent widow's priceless diamond necklace—destined for auction to benefit war orphans—mysteriously vanishes from a locked vault, Detective Farraday is baffled. But Blackie knows better. He's caught the scent of something darker beneath the philanthropic facade: a scheme to pocket the insurance money while the charity suffers. With his trusted sidekick The Runt at his heels and his wits sharper than any lockpick, Blackie must pull off the impossible—steal the necklace himself to expose the real crooks. What follows is a cat-and-mouse game of clever deceptions, narrow escapes, and the kind of high-stakes intrigue that made Boston Blackie the thinking man's crime fighter.

Since its inception, Boston Blackie has stood apart in the detective mystery pantheon, offering listeners something more than simple crime-solving theatrics. Here was a protagonist who walked the razor's edge between the underworld and respectability, a man whose criminal expertise served the cause of justice. The show's popularity throughout the mid-1940s reflected America's fascination with morally complex heroes—characters who understood the criminal mind because they'd lived it. This particular episode, broadcast as the war wound toward its conclusion, captures that unique charm: the marriage of entertainment with earnest social concern, wrapped in snappy dialogue and genuine suspense.

Don those earphones and step into the atmospheric underworld of 1940s Boston. Chester Morris's world-weary yet inherently decent portrayal of Blackie has captivated audiences for decades, and this episode stands as a sterling example of why. Boston Blackie awaits.
