When Boston Blackie receives a mysterious late-night visitor claiming to be attorney Bill Crane, the suave ex-convict finds himself drawn into a labyrinth of professional secrets and courtroom intrigue that threatens to unravel an innocent man's life. The fog-shrouded streets of post-war Boston crackle with tension as Blackie navigates between the criminal underworld he once knew intimately and the respectable legal profession where deception runs just as deep. Listeners will thrill to the rapid-fire dialogue and clever plot twists that defined the series' golden age, as Blackie's quick wit and questionable methods prove far more effective than the law's plodding procedures.

By 1945, Boston Blackie had become one of radio's most beloved antiheroes, a character who occupied that delicious gray space between criminal and hero that audiences found absolutely irresistible. Jack Lemmon would later immortalize the character on screen, but in these radio broadcasts, Chester Morris's voice captured something essential about the appeal: a man who operated by his own code, who used his knowledge of the underworld's mechanics to expose corruption in respectable institutions. The show's popularity reflected American audiences' growing skepticism of authority during and after World War II, their fascination with protagonists who bent the rules to achieve justice. Guest appearances by characters like Bill Crane—figures from allied crime stories and pulp fiction—enriched the show's universe and kept listeners guessing.

Tune in to experience Blackie at the height of his considerable powers, trading quips with the mysterious attorney while a guilty party circles ever closer. This episode exemplifies why Boston Blackie remained essential listening throughout the 1940s, proving night after night that sometimes the best detective work happens in the shadows.
