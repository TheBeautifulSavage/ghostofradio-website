Picture yourself settling into your favorite chair on a warm May evening in 1947, the soft amber glow of your radio's dial casting shadows across the room as Boston Blackie's smooth, knowing voice cuts through the static. "The Ghost of Florence Newton" pulls you immediately into the shadowy underworld of Boston's most notorious reformed jewel thief—except nothing is quite what it seems. A woman presumed dead for years has somehow returned from the grave, and Blackie finds himself entangled in a web of blackmail, murder, and supernatural terror that challenges even his quick wit and street-smart instincts. Every footstep echoes with menace; every phone call crackles with danger. Is Florence Newton's appearance genuine resurrection, elaborate hoax, or something far more sinister? Only Blackie and his faithful sidekick the Runt can untangle this mystery before midnight strikes.

What made Boston Blackie one of radio's most enduring detective series was its refusal to play it safe—here was a hero who operated in the grey spaces between law and justice, a charming rogue with a heart of gold whose dialogue sparkled with wit and urban grit. The show's writers crafted episodes that balanced humor with genuine suspense, allowing the series to thrive through the late 1940s when many radio dramas were beginning to fade. Kent Taylor's definitive portrayal of Blackie became iconic precisely because he embodied that contradiction: dangerous yet honorable, clever yet vulnerable.

Tonight, step back into the golden age of broadcasting when a creaking door and a narrator's voice could transport millions into mystery. Tune in to "The Ghost of Florence Newton" and discover why audiences kept their dials locked to Boston Blackie, week after week, never quite knowing what impossible crime awaited.
