When the needle drops on this sweltering August evening in Boston, you'll find yourself in the shadowy back room of a dingy waterfront tavern, where our hero Colin Coniston—better known as Boston Blackie—corners a terrified witness to what may be the most brutal murder the city has seen in years. John Austin, a seemingly ordinary shipping clerk, has been found dead in circumstances that defy explanation, and every lead Boston Blackie pursues only deepens the mystery. With the police breathing down his neck and danger lurking in every darkened alley, Blackie must use all his considerable cunning and street smarts to uncover the truth before another body hits the pavement. This episode crackles with the tension that made the show a late-era favorite—clever dialogue, unexpected twists, and the constant threat of violence that kept millions of Americans glued to their radios.

Boston Blackie arrived late to radio's golden age, premiering in 1944 after years of successful pulp magazine and motion picture success, and this 1950 episode represents the show at its mature peak. By this time, the formula was perfected: a reformed jewel thief with underworld connections, an inexplicable police captain, and a city teeming with secrets and double-crosses. The post-war audience craved these darker, morally ambiguous detective stories—Blackie operated in the gray zones where law-abiding citizens feared to tread, making him far more compelling than straight-arrow heroes. This particular mystery demonstrates why the show maintained its devoted following right up until the final broadcast.

Don't miss "The Murder of John Austin"—settle in with the lights dimmed low and let the sounds of the city draw you into one of Boston Blackie's most intricately plotted cases.
