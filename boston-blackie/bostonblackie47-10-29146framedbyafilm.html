When the scratchy NBC orchestra strikes up that familiar theme, Boston Blackie finds himself ensnared in a murder most cinematic—literally. In this October 29, 1946 episode, our gentleman thief and reformed rogue must navigate the glittering, treacherous world of Hollywood's golden age, where a starlet lies dead and all evidence points toward Blackie himself. As the police close in with handcuffs at the ready, Blackie must use his razor-sharp wits and shadowy connections to uncover which studio mogul, desperate director, or jealous rival planted the murder weapon in his dressing room. The tension crackles through the airwaves as Inspector Farraday's voice grows more menacing with each scene, while Blackie's wise-cracking companion Mary Wesley pleads for him to clear his name before the net tightens irreversibly.

Boston Blackie emerged as one of radio's most enduring heroes during the 1940s, a character born from pulp magazines who found his truest voice in the intimate medium of broadcast drama. Unlike the hard-boiled detectives who populated the airwaves with cynicism and brutality, Blackie represented something uniquely appealing: the reformed criminal using his underworld knowledge for justice, the charming rogue with a genuine moral compass. The show's success lay in this complexity—audiences loved rooting for a protagonist who existed in moral shades of gray, whose street-level connections often proved more valuable than official police work. This particular episode exemplifies why the series thrived through multiple networks and six seasons of loyal listenership.

Settle into your favorite chair, adjust the dial, and prepare yourself for an evening of mistaken identity, Hollywood intrigue, and the quicksilver mind of Boston Blackie working against the clock. This is detective mystery radio at its finest, where danger lurks behind every studio light and innocence itself becomes the most dangerous accusation of all.
