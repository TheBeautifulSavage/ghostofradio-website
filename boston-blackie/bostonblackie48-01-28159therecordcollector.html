# The Record Collector

When the lights dim and that distinctive harmonica theme cuts through the static, Boston Blackie is back on the case—and this time, a seemingly innocent record collector has become entangled in a web of deception that could prove deadly. As our reformed jewel thief-turned-detective investigates what appears to be a simple matter of stolen recordings, he discovers that some collections are worth killing for. With the smooth, streetwise voice of Chester Morris guiding us through Boston's shadowy underbelly, this January 1948 episode promises the kind of intricate plotting and snappy dialogue that made listeners rush home to their radios every week, anxious to learn what trouble Blackie and his faithful companion The Runt would stumble into next.

Boston Blackie represented something uniquely American during the golden age of radio—a character who lived outside the law but fought for justice, embodying the post-war appetite for morally complex heroes. Throughout the 1940s, as the show moved between networks (NBC, CBS, and Mutual), it maintained an extraordinary run by blending hard-boiled detective work with genuine warmth and humor. The writing was sharp, the pacing relentless, and Chester Morris's performance as Blackie created an iconic figure whose influence extended from radio into film and later television. These episodes captured the paranoia, excitement, and optimism of an America learning to settle into peacetime, where a crook-turned-detective could become America's trusted friend.
