When the clock strikes midnight on this February evening in 1948, Boston Blackie finds himself ensnared in one of his most diabolical cases yet—two murders, identical in method, separated by mere hours, and leading nowhere but deeper into shadow. A society woman lies cold in her mansion bedroom while across the city, a street hustler meets an identical fate. The only connection: a cryptic calling card left at each scene. As Blackie moves through fog-shrouded Boston streets with his faithful companion Mary and the reluctant aid of Inspector Farraday, the tension mounts with each new revelation. Listeners will feel the walls closing in as seemingly innocent clues become sinister threads in a killer's grand design, and Blackie discovers that beneath the surface of this apparently senseless double murder lies a motive so personal, so shattering, that even a criminal of his experience finds himself shaken.

Boston Blackie thrived during radio's golden age precisely because audiences craved a hero who existed in moral gray zones—a man more clever than lawful, a burglar and safecracker with a conscience that inevitably steered him toward justice. This particular episode showcases why the program maintained loyal listeners throughout its network run, capturing that quintessential 1940s blend of hard-boiled detective work and sophisticated urban mystery. Orson Welles's company had recently adapted the character, bringing literary credibility to what might have been mere pulp entertainment, and episodes like this demonstrate the show's surprising narrative depth.

Don your metaphorical fedora and step into the gaslit streets of Boston. Settle into your chair as the organ swells and that unmistakable voice announces our hero's name—and prepare yourself for ninety minutes of masterful storytelling that proves radio drama at its finest.
