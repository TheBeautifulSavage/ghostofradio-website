When the lights dim and that distinctive theme music swells—that jazzy, propulsive saxophone cutting through the static—listeners know they're about to follow Boston Blackie into the shadowed underbelly of the city where crime and conscience intersect in the most unexpected ways. In this March 3rd, 1948 episode, our reformed jewel thief turned private detective stumbles onto a case that begins innocuously enough at the Cobb Striking Company, but quickly spirals into a labyrinth of industrial espionage, double-dealing, and murder most cunning. With his ever-present sidekick the Runt riding shotgun, Blackie must navigate between corrupt factory bosses, desperate workers, and someone who's already left a body in their wake. The tension crackles through every commercial break as our protagonist uses those old underworld instincts for good, his quick wit and quicker mind the only things standing between justice and another unsolved crime.

What made Boston Blackie a cornerstone of American radio detective fiction was precisely this moral ambiguity—a protagonist who understood the criminal mind because he'd lived it, now using that hard-earned knowledge against those who prey on honest citizens. The show's success across NBC, CBS, and the Mutual network from 1944 to 1950 reflected post-war America's fascination with redemption and second chances, with the idea that experience in darkness could illuminate truth. These weren't cozy mysteries; they were gritty, atmospheric tales that mirrored the noir-soaked urban landscape of American anxieties.

Settle in with headphones or crowd around the speaker—this is old-time radio at its finest, where imagination and expert voice acting do what no television budget could match. Let Boston Blackie guide you through the Cobb Striking Company's deadly secrets.
