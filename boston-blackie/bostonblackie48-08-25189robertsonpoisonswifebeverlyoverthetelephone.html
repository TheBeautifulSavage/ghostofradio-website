When the telephone receiver crackles to life on that fateful August evening in 1948, listeners are thrust into one of Boston Blackie's most diabolical cases—a murder plot executed across the airwaves themselves. As the mysterious and reformed jewel thief Boston Blackie races against time, the very instrument meant to connect us becomes a weapon of cold-blooded murder. Beverly Robertson's desperate cries echo through the telephone line while her husband's voice drips with sinister purpose, and Blackie must unravel the poisoner's scheme before it's too late. The claustrophobic tension of voices isolated in separate rooms, the ticking of the clock, and the helplessness of a crime witnessed but unstoppable create an atmosphere of genuine dread. This episode masterfully exploits radio's unique power—the invisible made terrifyingly real through sound alone.

Boston Blackie occupied a singular place in the Golden Age of radio detective fiction. Unlike the genteel drawing-room mysteries of Ellery Queen or the hard-boiled cynicism of The Thin Man, Blackie brought the criminal underworld into American living rooms with unprecedented authenticity. The character, originally drawn from pulp magazines and silent films, became a cultural phenomenon on radio precisely because of his ambiguous morality—a man who'd crossed society's lines yet now fought for justice. The show's rotation between NBC, CBS, and Mutual networks speaks to its enduring popularity, while the sophisticated plotting and stellar voice performances elevated it beyond mere formula entertainment into the realm of genuine theatrical achievement.

Tune in to experience a masterwork of audio drama where the telephone itself becomes an instrument of doom. Boston Blackie awaits—where danger lurks in the static between voices, and redemption remains forever uncertain.
