On this thrilling March evening in 1949, Boston Blackie finds himself entangled in a glittering web of deception that reaches from the Boston docks to the mansions of Beacon Hill. When a string of priceless pearls—once the pride of a murdered socialite—surfaces in the underworld, Blackie must navigate the treacherous boundaries between his criminal contacts and his uneasy alliance with Inspector Farraday. With The Rogue and Mary Wesley at his side, our reformed jewel thief pursues a killer who operates in the shadowy margins between high society and low crime. The tension mounts as Blackie realizes he may be chasing a phantom—someone who knows his methods as intimately as he knows theirs. Expect breathless dialogue, clever misdirection, and the kind of plot twists that kept millions of Americans glued to their radios long after bedtime.

Boston Blackie occupied a unique place in the detective radio pantheon during the 1940s. Unlike the morally unambiguous G-men of The FBI in Peace and War, Blackie was a gentleman criminal—never quite reformed, always operating by his own code of honor. Created by Jack Boyle and brought to vivid life by Chester Morris's distinctive delivery, the show capitalized on post-war America's fascination with anti-heroes: men who walked the line between law and lawlessness. The writing was sophisticated, treating audiences as intelligent citizens who appreciated clever plotting over simple heroics.

If you've never experienced the electric charm of Boston Blackie, "Madame Don's Pearls" offers the perfect entry point—a masterclass in old-time radio mystery craftsmanship. Tune in and discover why this series remains a beloved classic.
