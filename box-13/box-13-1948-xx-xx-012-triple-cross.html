When a mysterious package arrives at DanHolliday's newspaper office bearing nothing but a cryptic number, the enterprising columnist finds himself ensnared in a web of deception that spirals into breathtaking danger. In "Triple Cross," our protagonist discovers that the seemingly simple case of a stolen diamond has been orchestrated by three separate parties—each convinced they are outsmarting the others, each unaware that Holliday has become the unwitting centerpiece of their schemes. As midnight deadlines loom and violence threatens to erupt, listeners will find themselves caught in a taut battle of wits where trust becomes a luxury no one can afford, and every shadow conceals another layer of betrayal.

Box 13 emerged during the golden twilight of network radio, when adventure serials still commanded millions of listeners and the crackle of static felt like an invitation into genuine danger. The show's premise—that Holliday solves crimes using only the cryptic clues contained in mysterious mail—proved irresistible to audiences weary of post-war uncertainty, offering escapism with intellectual appeal. With its rapid-fire dialogue, elaborate plots, and protagonist who relied on cunning rather than gunplay, Box 13 represented a distinctly American brand of clever heroism that defined late-1940s radio drama. This particular episode exemplifies the show's gift for Byzantine plotting, rewarding attentive listeners with the satisfaction of unraveling tangled schemes.

Don your headphones and step into the shadows of Dan Holliday's world. When that fateful box arrives, you'll understand why Box 13 captivated a nation desperate for mystery, suspense, and the promise that intelligence and courage could triumph against impossible odds.
