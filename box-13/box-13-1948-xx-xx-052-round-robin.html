Picture this: a mysterious box arrives at the offices of Dan Holliday, the globe-trotting adventurer and star reporter, and inside lies a cryptic clue that sets him careening across a dangerous landscape of deception and double-crossing. In "Round Robin," listeners will find themselves caught in a whirlwind of switchbacks and misdirection—where every contact Dan makes seems to lead only to another shadowy figure, another unanswered question, and another layer of intrigue. The tension mounts with each breathless scene as our hero races against time, never quite certain whom to trust, and the atmospheric sound design of creaking doors, ringing phones, and that unmistakable urgent musical sting will keep you pressed against your radio speaker.

Box 13 arrived in 1948 as a masterclass in economical storytelling, proving that a syndicated adventure show could match the sophistication and production values of network broadcasts. The show's genius lay in its simplicity: a mysterious box, a clue, and Dan Holliday's razor-sharp wits pitted against increasingly elaborate schemes. With its snappy dialogue, genuine sense of danger, and host Alan Ladd providing introduction and commentary, Box 13 captured audiences across America who craved smart, fast-paced entertainment. "Round Robin" exemplifies what made the series tick—clever plotting that kept even savvy listeners guessing.

If you're seeking that golden age thrill of not knowing what comes next, of heroes who rely on cunning rather than brute force, then tune in to Box 13 and discover why audiences made this show appointment listening nearly seventy-five years ago. Let the mystery pull you in.
