When Dan Holliday opens the mysterious Box 13 on this sweltering evening in 1948, he discovers a cryptic puzzle that will lead him into the shadowed corridors of academia and toward a killer who hides behind respectability and intellect. A renowned professor has disappeared under impossible circumstances, leaving behind only scattered clues that seem to mock conventional investigation. As our hero races against time, you'll hear the whispered conversations in darkened lecture halls, the frantic shuffling of papers, and the mounting dread that suggests the answer to this deadly riddle lies locked away in a brilliant but twisted mind. The puzzle itself becomes a character in this episode—one that kills, that deceives, that tantalizes with the promise of understanding just beyond reach.

Box 13, broadcast during the golden age of syndicated radio, represented something special in the mystery genre: a show that trusted its audience's intelligence while delivering genuine thrills. Host Alan Ladd's urbane narration framed each episode as a confidential case file, making listeners feel like they were reading classified documents rather than simply hearing a story. The program's appeal lay in its refusal to talk down to its audience, weaving together logic puzzles, genuine danger, and the kind of sophisticated banter that made radio drama an art form. "The Professor and the Puzzle" exemplifies why the show earned its devoted following—it's a mystery that rewards careful listening, where clues matter and solutions feel earned rather than arbitrary.

Tune in and discover why listeners across America couldn't afford to miss Box 13. In a world where answers rarely come easily, and where the cleverest among us can harbor the darkest secrets, your detective skills will be tested. Do you have what it takes to solve this puzzle before Dan Holliday does?
