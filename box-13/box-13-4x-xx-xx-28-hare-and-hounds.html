# Box 13: "Hare and Hounds"

Picture this: the static crackles, the eerie organ swells, and DanHolliday—that mysterious man of a thousand identities—finds himself caught in a deadly game where the hunters have become the hunted. In this pulse-pounding installment, our protagonist must navigate a maze of deception and danger when a seemingly innocent favor spirals into a cat-and-mouse chase across the city. With only his wits and whatever secrets lurk in that fateful Box 13, Holliday must outthink his pursuers before they close in. The tension mounts with each passing minute as sound effects—footsteps echoing through empty streets, car engines roaring, desperate gasps—paint a visceral portrait of a man running for his life. Will he escape with his secrets intact, or will this game finally prove too treacherous even for him?

Box 13 epitomized the golden age of syndicated radio adventure, thriving in that post-war period when Americans craved quick-witted heroes and intricate plots that could be wrapped up in thirty thrilling minutes. Starring Alan Ladd in its earliest broadcasts, the show's premise was devilishly simple yet endlessly malleable: each episode brought a new mystery, a new identity, a new complication emerging from that infamous Box 13. The show's rapid-fire dialogue, elaborate sound design, and commitment to leaving listeners perpetually off-balance made it a favorite among audiences hungry for intelligent, sophisticated entertainment—the kind that demanded your full attention and rewarded it generously.

Whether you're a devoted listener of classic radio or discovering Box 13 for the first time, "Hare and Hounds" exemplifies everything that made this series essential listening. Tune in and experience the thrill of the hunt—you won't know who to trust, but that's precisely the point.
