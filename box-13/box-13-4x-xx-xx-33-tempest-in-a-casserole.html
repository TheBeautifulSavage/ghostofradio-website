When investigative journalist Dan Halloween opens the mysterious Box 13 that arrives at his Chicago newspaper office, he discovers a case that begins not with gunfire or threats, but with something far more domestic—and infinitely more dangerous. A seemingly innocent casserole dish becomes the linchpin in a web of deception, jealousy, and murder that winds through the city's respectable neighborhoods. As Dan peels back the layers of this peculiar mystery, he finds himself caught between a grieving widow, a suspicious insurance investigator, and someone willing to kill to keep a secret buried. The episode crackles with the kind of tension that made Box 13 essential listening: that distinctive blend of hard-boiled detective work and the unsettling realization that evil often wears the mask of civility. Expect plot twists that come as quickly as Dan's cigarette smoke curls through his newsroom office, and dialogue sharp enough to cut glass.

Box 13 captured listeners' imaginations during radio's golden age by offering something networks rarely risked—a genuinely unpredictable mystery series where the audience couldn't anticipate how each week's puzzle would unfold. Airing in syndication from 1948 to 1949, the show featured the silky-voiced Dan Hallowell as both hero and narrator, drawing viewers into real crimes and fabricated mysteries with equal conviction. The series' appeal lay not just in the mysteries themselves, but in how they reflected post-war anxieties about trust, identity, and danger lurking in ordinary places.

Tune in to Box 13 and experience why audiences crowded around their sets each week, eager to discover what secrets would spill from that fateful box. In just thirty minutes, you'll find yourself transported to an era when radio could make your heart race with nothing but your imagination and a perfectly timed dramatic pause.
