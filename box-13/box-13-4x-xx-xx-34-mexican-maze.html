# Box 13: Mexican Maze

Picture yourself huddled around the radio on a crackling evening in 1948, when Dan Holliday's voice cuts through the static with urgent whispers and the distant sound of mariachi music twisted into something sinister. In "Mexican Maze," our globe-trotting adventurer finds himself ensnared in the shadowy underbelly of a border town, where a mysterious box numbered 13 holds the key to a conspiracy that reaches far beyond dusty cantinas and corrupt officials. Listen as Holliday navigates a labyrinth of double-crosses, hidden identities, and razor-sharp danger, where every corner harbors deception and every alliance could be his last. The episode pulses with authentic period ambiance—the clink of glasses, the shuffle of cards, the ominous tones of an unseen orchestra that mirrors our hero's mounting dread. By the final commercial break, you'll be desperate to know whether Holliday escapes with his life—or if this time, Box 13 has finally claimed him.

What made Box 13 a sensation in post-war America was its refusal to stay put. Unlike the stuffy drawing-room mysteries and hometown adventures of earlier radio's golden age, Holliday's exploits took listeners across continents and through moral gray zones that reflected the anxious, noir-soaked sensibilities of late 1940s audiences. Created by Phillips Lord, the show's syndicated run proved Americans hungered for sophisticated, location-hopping intrigue that treated them as adults capable of handling ambiguity and danger.

Don't miss this venture into the Mexican underworld. Tune in to Box 13: Mexican Maze and discover why audiences in 1948 couldn't wait to hear what impossible situation their favorite adventurer would talk his way out of next.
