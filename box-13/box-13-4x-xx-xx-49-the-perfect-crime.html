# Box 13: The Perfect Crime

When DanHolliday opens the mysterious Box 13 that arrives at his newspaper office this fateful evening, he discovers a case that seems impossible—a crime so perfectly executed that no detective has managed to unravel it. The victim lies in a locked room, the weapon has vanished without a trace, and every suspect possesses both motive and alibi in equal measure. As Holliday's distinctive voice cuts through the noir-drenched narrative, listeners are pulled into a labyrinth of red herrings and false leads, where the clever criminal has thought of everything—or has he? The tension mounts with each revelation, each clue that leads nowhere, and each confrontation with the increasingly desperate parties involved. By the time the broadcast reaches its climax, you'll find yourself leaning closer to the speaker, trying to solve the puzzle before Dan Holliday himself cracks the case wide open.

Box 13 represented the golden twilight of radio adventure programming in 1948-1949, arriving just as television began its inexorable march into American living rooms. The show's clever premise—that listeners could experience genuine mysteries before the solution was revealed—gave it a participatory quality that was revolutionary for its time. Each episode's central mystery was designed to be fair play; you possessed all the clues necessary to solve it yourself. This particular episode exemplifies what made the series a cult favorite among radio enthusiasts, showcasing the craftsmanship of writers who understood that the best mysteries don't rely on tricks, but on misdirection and human nature.

"The Perfect Crime" awaits you—a tangled web of deception and detection that proves some mysteries are only perfect until the right mind examines them. Tune in and see if you can solve it before Dan Holliday does.
