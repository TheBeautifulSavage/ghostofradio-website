# Box 13: "Archimedes and the Roman"

Picture the scene: midnight in a shadowy antiquities shop, where a mysterious package arrives at the office of Dan Holiday—a man who solves the puzzles hidden within anonymous boxes for a living. Inside Box 13 awaits a cryptic letter and an ancient bronze device, supposedly the lost mechanical calculator of Archimedes himself. But when a wealthy collector turns up dead, and Nazi agents close in from the shadows, Holiday finds himself racing against time through a labyrinth of deception, scholarly intrigue, and ruthless international espionage. The ticking clock and urgent violins underscore every tense moment as our hero must separate historical truth from elaborate forgery—all while unknown forces manipulate the game from the darkness.

What made *Box 13* such a sensation for radio audiences in 1948-49 was its perfect marriage of intellectual mystery and pulp adventure. Created by and starring the versatile Myron C. Cope, the show elevated the detective yarn into something more cerebral, where clever dialogue and historical puzzle-solving proved just as thrilling as any gunplay or chase scene. This particular episode exemplifies the show's brilliance—it weaves authentic classical history with wartime paranoia and personal danger, trusting listeners to follow complex threads of logic while remaining utterly gripped by the drama. The program was sophisticated yet accessible, demanding audience engagement without sacrificing entertainment.

*Box 13* represents a golden moment in syndicated radio, when producers dared to trust their listeners with stories of substance and style. If you've never experienced Dan Holiday's world of lethal puzzles and shadowy opponents, "Archimedes and the Roman" is the perfect entry point—a masterclass in suspense and storytelling. Tune in, settle into that familiar static crackle, and discover why radio's finest mysteries still resonate across the decades.
