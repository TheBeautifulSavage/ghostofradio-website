# Casey, Crime Photographer: "The Twenty-Minute Alibi"

Picture this: a sweltering New York night, the kind that makes the city sweat through its collar. Casey rushes into the darkroom of the *New York Illustrated*, photographs still wet in his hands, when a homicide detective bursts through the door with news that will turn his evening upside down. A socialite has been found dead in her Park Avenue penthouse, and the prime suspect claims he was nowhere near the scene—but his alibi is impossibly thin: just twenty minutes. As Casey develops prints under the red glow of the darkroom lamp, he realizes his photographs may have captured something the killer desperately wanted hidden. With his trusty camera and sharper wits, Casey must navigate the treacherous intersection of tabloid journalism and police procedure to expose a killer who's betting his alibi will hold up long enough to escape.

*Casey, Crime Photographer* captured something essential about post-war America: the thrill of the hunt, the romance of investigative reporting, and the notion that truth could be found in the smallest details—a shadow in a photograph, a timeline that doesn't quite add up. Running from 1943 to 1955, the show made reporter Casey Adams an unlikely hero, armed with a camera instead of a gun. The show's appeal lay in its gritty realism, authentic police procedural elements, and the chemistry between Casey and his contacts throughout the NYPD, building a world listeners couldn't resist visiting week after week.

The tension mounts as Casey's photographs develop in real-time alongside the investigation. Don't miss this masterclass in radio suspense—tune in now and discover how one photograph can change everything.
