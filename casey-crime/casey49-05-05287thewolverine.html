When the telephone rings in Casey's darkroom on a rain-soaked Chicago evening, the newsman finds himself drawn into the shadowy world of "The Wolverine"—a phantom figure whose crimes leave the city's police baffled and its newspapers screaming for answers. With nothing but a cryptic photograph and his instincts to guide him, Casey races against time through dimly lit warehouses and corruption-riddled precincts, each clue pulling him deeper into a mystery where the line between hunter and hunted grows perilously thin. Listeners will find themselves suspended in that distinctive Casey atmosphere—the scratch of a reporter's pencil, the ambient murmur of the city at night, and that unmistakable tension when danger lurks just beyond the next corner, waiting to spring.

Casey, Crime Photographer stands as one of broadcasting's finest achievements in the crime-drama genre, a show that thrived on the intimate connection between listener and protagonist. Premiering in 1943, the series offered something CBS's audiences craved: the authentic grit of newspaper work combined with genuine mystery and peril. Unlike the costumed heroes and elaborate fictional detectives populating the airwaves, Casey was refreshingly human—a working stiff with a camera and a conscience, solving crimes through legwork and instinct rather than superpowers. This 1940s episode exemplifies the show's golden era, when production values were sharp and the writing cut straight to the bone.

Don't miss "The Wolverine"—tune in to experience classic radio storytelling at its finest, where every sound effect and carefully modulated voice pulls you deeper into a world of noir mystery. This is Casey, Crime Photographer: where the camera never lies, but the city's secrets run dangerously deep.
