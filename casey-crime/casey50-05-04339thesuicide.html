# Casey, Crime Photographer: "The Suicide"

Picture yourself in a smoke-filled newspaper office on a rain-soaked evening in the late 1940s, where veteran crime photographer Casey jumps at the chance to investigate what appears to be an open-and-shut case—a man found dead with a gun in his hand, a suicide note on the desk. But Casey's instincts, honed by years chasing the real story behind the headlines, tell him something doesn't add up. As he moves through shadowy nightclubs, seedy apartment buildings, and the gleaming offices of the powerful, the truth slowly emerges, darker and more sinister than anyone suspected. Listeners will experience the tension mounting with each revelation, the sharp dialogue cracking like a gunshot, and the moral ambiguity that made Casey's world so compelling—where the camera's lens never lies, but human nature always does.

*Casey, Crime Photographer* was more than just another crime drama in radio's golden age; it was a show that transformed the newspaper racket into intimate psychological thriller. With CBS's backing from 1943 through 1955, the show pioneered the "gritty realism" approach to crime storytelling, replacing the grand-scale mysteries of earlier programs with close, personal investigations into human weakness and deception. Darren McGavin's portrayal of Casey—though in this earlier episode a different actor carried the role—created an archetype of the thinking detective, a man whose camera and instincts worked in tandem to expose the truth. The show's influence echoed through decades of television crime dramas yet to come.

Tune in to "The Suicide" and experience why listeners tuned in religiously each week, drawn into the murky world where nothing is ever quite what it seems, and the photograph always tells the tale.
