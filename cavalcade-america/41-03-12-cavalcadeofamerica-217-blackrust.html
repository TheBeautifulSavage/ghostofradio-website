# Cavalcade of America: Episode 217 – "Blackrust"

Picture this: the year is the 1940s, and across America, families gather around their radio sets as the unmistakable orchestral fanfare of *Cavalcade of America* fills their living rooms. Tonight's episode, "Blackrust," transports listeners to the dusty heartland, where an American farmer faces his greatest adversary—not enemy soldiers, but the relentless plague of soil erosion that threatens to swallow his livelihood whole. As the dramatic narrative unfolds, you'll hear the wind howling through abandoned fields, the desperate conversations of families clinging to their heritage, and the slow-burning tension of a man determined to reclaim what nature and neglect have nearly stolen. This is not a tale of romantic heroism, but of quiet courage and ingenuity in the face of an invisible enemy—a battle as real and urgent to Depression-era listeners as any headline in their morning paper.

*Cavalcade of America* distinguished itself by dramatizing the true stories of ordinary Americans who shaped the nation's character. Unlike many contemporary radio programs, DuPont's prestige anthology series refused to sentimentalize history, instead presenting morally complex narratives grounded in meticulous research. "Blackrust" exemplifies this commitment, drawing directly from the catastrophic Dust Bowl crisis of the 1930s—a national trauma still painfully fresh in listeners' memories. By translating historical tragedy into intimate human drama, the show honored the resilience of rural America while exploring the intersection of personal responsibility and environmental consequence.

Tune in to *Cavalcade of America* for "Blackrust" and experience why this program became essential listening for generations of Americans. Here is history not as dusty textbooks, but as living, breathing human struggle.
