Picture yourself huddled close to your radio on a fog-laden evening as the CBS Radio Mystery Theater's signature organ swells into the darkness. "Windandingo" draws you into a sprawling Southern Gothic estate where a family's greed collides with something far more sinister than mere human ambition. When a mysterious visitor arrives with knowledge of an old family curse, the walls seem to close in—every creaking floorboard, every whispered accusation becomes a thread in a tapestry of suspicion and dread. Is the supernatural force at work real, or is it merely a convenient mask for murder most calculated? As the drama unfolds across its pristine airwaves, you'll find yourself wrestling with the very question the characters cannot escape: what price will they pay to possess what they desire?

The CBS Radio Mystery Theater premiered in 1974 as a bold revival of a golden-age tradition many feared had died with television's rise. Yet this anthology series proved listeners still craved the intimate terror that only radio could deliver—that space between sound and imagination where genuine horror lives. "Windandingo," set against the 1940s American South, captures the show's mastery of psychological tension and its willingness to blur the line between rationality and the inexplicable. Each episode was crafted to exploit radio's unique power: the absence of visual distraction forces complete emotional surrender to the narrative.

If you've ever experienced that delicious shiver of uncertainty in the dark, if you've wondered whether skepticism or superstition better serves survival, then "Windandingo" awaits your ears. Settle in, dim the lights, and surrender to an evening of mystery as only the golden age of radio could provide.
