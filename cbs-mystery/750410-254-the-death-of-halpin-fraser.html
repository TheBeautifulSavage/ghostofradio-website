On a fog-shrouded night in this classic CBS Radio Mystery Theater production, listeners are drawn into a labyrinth of deception where nothing is quite what it seems. When the distinguished Halpin Fraser is found dead in his locked study—a bullet through his heart and a revolver at his side—the case appears simple: suicide. But as the investigation unfolds through tense interrogations and shadowy revelations, the truth becomes increasingly elusive. Was Fraser running from something darker than despair? What secrets did his household conceal behind closed doors? The program builds with masterful pacing, each clue a breadcrumb leading deeper into moral ambiguity, as the boundary between victim and perpetrator dissolves entirely.

CBS Radio Mystery Theater emerged in the 1970s as a golden-age revival, capturing the spirit of classic detective serials while bringing modern storytelling sensibilities to the airwaves. This particular episode exemplifies the show's gift for psychological complexity—the mystery isn't merely *who* killed Fraser, but *why*, and what it reveals about the human capacity for concealment and self-deception. Set in the atmospheric 1940s, the episode pulses with period authenticity, from the dialogue to the meticulous sound design that transforms a simple drawing room into a pressure cooker of suspicion. The cast delivers performances heavy with unspoken tension, their voices betraying the inner turmoil their composed facades attempt to hide.
