The Triangle

Picture this: a fog-shrouded night, the distant wail of a ship's horn cutting through the Atlantic darkness, and a woman's trembling voice confessing a secret that could destroy everything she holds dear. In "The Triangle," listeners are drawn into a claustrophobic web of maritime mystery where three people—a captain, his wife, and a mysterious stranger—are bound together by circumstance and an inexplicable triangle of deception. As the drama unfolds across the misty waters between ports, each character's hidden motives surface like wreckage after a storm. What begins as a simple voyage becomes a psychological chess game where trust is currency and the next confession could be someone's last. The expertly layered sound design—creaking ship timbers, swelling orchestral stabs, and the intimate whisper of confession—pulls you directly into the cabin where desperate decisions are made and dark truths emerge.

CBS Radio Mystery Theater emerged during the golden age of audio drama's renaissance, reviving the supernatural and mystery traditions that had captivated millions in the 1930s and 40s. Though broadcast in the 1970s and 80s, the show deliberately crafted episodes rooted in classic mystery conventions, and "The Triangle" exemplifies this timeless approach—eschewing modern sensibilities for the brooding atmosphere and moral ambiguity that defined earlier golden-age productions. The episode showcases why the show became appointment listening for mystery devotees: intelligent scripts, superb character work, and a commitment to genuine suspense over cheap thrills.

Tune in to "The Triangle" for an evening of expertly crafted suspense that proves some stories lose nothing in translation from page to airwave. In the dark, with only your imagination and that hypnotic sound design, you'll discover why radio drama remains the most intimate medium for terror.
