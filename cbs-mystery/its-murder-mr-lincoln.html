The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

"Its Murder Mr. Lincoln" is a gripping episode of CBS Radio Mystery Theater that aired on March 18, 1975. The story follows Jameson, a young lawyer who seeks the help of President Lincoln to clear his name after being wrongly accused of murder. As they journey through the treacherous streets of New Orleans, they encounter a web of deceit and corruption that threatens to destroy their lives. With the President's signature calm and wisdom guiding him, Jameson must navigate this complex landscape and confront the dark secrets that have been hiding in plain sight. This episode masterfully weaves together elements of mystery, drama, and historical fiction, drawing the listener into a richly textured world of 19th-century America.

The characters in "Its Murder Mr. Lincoln" are multidimensional and nuanced, with each one bringing their own unique voice to the story. The President's gentle yet firm leadership serves as a steady anchor for Jameson, while the enigmatic lawyer himself is driven by a fierce determination to prove his innocence. As they move through the city, they encounter a cast of colorful characters, from shady businessmen to corrupt politicians, each one with their own secrets and motivations. This complex interplay of personalities creates a sense of tension and suspense that propels the listener forward.

As Jameson and Lincoln work together to unravel the mystery, the stakes grow higher and higher, threatening to destroy not just Jameson's life but also the very fabric of American society. The episode's narrative is both thrilling and thought-provoking, inviting listeners to consider the power of truth and justice in a time of great social change.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

When "Its Murder Mr. Lincoln" aired on CBS Radio Mystery Theater in March 1975, America was still reeling from the aftermath of the Vietnam War. The country was grappling with the consequences of its involvement in the conflict, including widespread protests and demonstrations against the war. At home, Americans were also struggling to cope with economic uncertainty, as high inflation and unemployment rates had become increasingly common.

The news that week was dominated by stories of the Watergate scandal, which was slowly unraveling but still sending shockwaves through the nation's capital. The country was on edge, wondering if the government was capable of reform or whether it would continue to be wracked by corruption. In the midst of this turmoil, "Its Murder Mr. Lincoln" offered a welcome respite from the darkness, transporting listeners to a different era and a different world.

As the episode's protagonist Jameson navigates the treacherous streets of New Orleans, he is also confronted with issues that are very much relevant to contemporary America – issues of justice, morality, and the power of truth. In this way, "Its Murder Mr. Lincoln" resonated deeply with audiences at a time when many were questioning the foundations of American society.

WHY IT MATTERS:

"What makes 'Its Murder Mr. Lincoln' stand out as an episode is its masterful use of narrative technique. The episode's writer, Jeff Corey, skillfully weaves together multiple plot threads and timelines, creating a sense of complexity and depth that draws the listener in. But it is also the performances – especially those of Jameson and Lincoln – that elevate this episode to new heights.

As the President's voice takes center stage, his calm and wisdom serve as a powerful counterpoint to the darkness and uncertainty that surrounds him. The way he approaches the case with a quiet confidence, drawing on his vast experience and knowledge of human nature, is nothing short of mesmerizing. This kind of storytelling – both character-driven and plot-driven – reveals something fundamental about radio drama as an art form: its ability to evoke a sense of intimacy and immediacy that no other medium can match.

In an era dominated by television, which often prioritized spectacle over substance, "Its Murder Mr. Lincoln" stands out for its thoughtful pacing, its richly textured world-building, and its willingness to engage with complex themes and issues."
