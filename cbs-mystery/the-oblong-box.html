The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

"The Oblong Box" is a gripping episode of CBS Radio Mystery Theater, airing on January 8th, 1975. The story centers around Dr. Robert MacQueen, a brilliant and reclusive scientist who has spent his life studying the supernatural. MacQueen's latest obsession is the "oblong box," an ancient artifact rumored to hold the key to immortality. As he delves deeper into his research, strange occurrences begin to plague him, and it becomes clear that some forces will stop at nothing to keep the secrets of the oblong box buried.

As the tension builds, MacQueen finds himself in a desperate fight for survival, with the forces of darkness arrayed against him. The episode expertly weaves together elements of horror, science fiction, and mystery, creating a thrilling narrative that keeps listeners on the edge of their seats. The character of Dr. MacQueen is masterfully crafted by actor John Colicos, whose nuanced performance brings depth and complexity to the role.

The supporting cast, including actress Peg Sherwood as MacQueen's loyal assistant, adds to the sense of urgency and foreboding. As the story hurtles towards its climax, the listener is left wondering: will Dr. MacQueen uncover the secrets of the oblong box, or will he become its latest victim?

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

As "The Oblong Box" aired on CBS Radio Mystery Theater in January 1975, America was grappling with a complex and tumultuous world. The Vietnam War raged on, with protests against the conflict growing louder by the day. The economy was struggling, with high inflation and unemployment rates taking their toll on working-class families.

At home, the country was still reeling from the OPEC oil embargo, which had sent shockwaves through the global energy market. Gasoline shortages became common, and prices skyrocketed. As a result, ordinary Americans were worried about everything from the price of milk to the state of their own finances.

On the cultural front, 1975 saw the rise of punk rock as a reaction against mainstream culture's perceived complacency. The release of Alfred Hitchcock's "Family Plot" in August of that year marked a new era for horror cinema, one that would give birth to the slasher film genre. Meanwhile, the feminist movement was gaining momentum, with events like the Miss America Pageant protest march making headlines.

All these factors – economic hardship, social unrest, and cultural upheaval – combined to create an atmosphere in which radio drama like "The Oblong Box" could thrive. The show's ability to craft a gripping narrative around themes of science, mystery, and the supernatural resonated deeply with audiences looking for escapism from their daily worries.
