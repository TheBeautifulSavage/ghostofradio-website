# The Cisco Kid: "Seven Devils"

Picture this: a dusty desert road at sundown, the wind carrying the faint jingle of spurs as our hero Cisco Kid and his faithful companion Pancho ride toward trouble. In "Seven Devils," broadcast in the heart of the 1940s, listeners were transported to a lawless borderland where seven ruthless banditos have seized control of a remote canyon stronghold. But this isn't just another tale of good versus evil—it's a battle of wits and courage as Cisco must navigate treacherous mountain passes, outwit cunning adversaries, and rescue an innocent family caught in the crossfire. The tension crackles through every scene, from whispered conversations under starlit skies to the thunder of hoofbeats as justice pursues the wicked. Duncan Renaldo's silky, confident voice as Cisco perfectly captures the charm and quick thinking that made him radio's most beloved caballero, while Leo Carrillo's Pancho provides both comic relief and genuine heroism when it matters most.

What made *The Cisco Kid* such a phenomenon during its broadcast run was its perfect balance of action, humor, and moral clarity—qualities that resonated deeply with audiences navigating the uncertainties of wartime America. Unlike the gritty, morally complicated westerns that would emerge decades later, Cisco represented an idealized figure: dashing yet honorable, clever yet compassionate, a man who robbed from the corrupt to protect the innocent. The show's popularity spawned films, a television series, and countless rebroadcasts, cementing Cisco as an enduring icon of the golden age of radio.

If you haven't experienced the magic of *The Cisco Kid*, "Seven Devils" is the perfect entry point—a thrilling adventure that showcases why millions tuned in faithfully each week. So settle in, dim the lights, and let the desert night work its spell.
