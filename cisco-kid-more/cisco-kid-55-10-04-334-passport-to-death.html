# Cisco Kid: "Passport to Death"

When the organ's dramatic notes fade and the hoofbeats of El Cisco's golden palomino thunder across the desert night, listeners knew they were in for a tale of danger and cunning. In this October 1940s broadcast, a forged passport becomes the thread binding together a murder plot, international intrigue, and the legendary outlaw's race against time. As Cisco navigates shadowy border towns and encounters desperate men willing to kill for false identity papers, the tension crackles through every commercial break. The stakes feel deathly real—this isn't a simple cattle rustling or stage robbery, but a web of deceit where even the most innocent document becomes a weapon. Duncan Renaldo's smooth, confident voice carries the Kid through moonlit confrontations and tense negotiations, while his wisecracking companion Pancho (Leo Carrillo) provides both comic relief and genuine support. The episode masterfully uses sound design—creaking doors, whispered conversations, the clink of spurs on wooden floors—to build an atmosphere of genuine menace on the Mexican frontier.

The Cisco Kid's long radio run represented the Golden Age of adventure programming, when half an hour of swift-moving storytelling could transport millions of Americans to a romanticized Old West. Unlike the brutal reality of frontier life, Cisco embodied a noble outlaw myth—intelligent, charming, and devoted to justice despite operating outside the law. The show's popularity proved audiences craved escapism during the turbulent 1940s, finding comfort in the Kid's impossible heroism and his unwavering moral code.

This episode exemplifies why the series maintained devoted listeners throughout its fifteen-year broadcast life. Slip on your headphones, darken the room, and let the desert night close in around you. The Cisco Kid awaits, and danger lurks in every shadow of the borderlands.
