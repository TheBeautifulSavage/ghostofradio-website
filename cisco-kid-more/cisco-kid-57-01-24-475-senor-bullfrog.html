# Cisco Kid: Señor Bullfrog

When you dial in to this January 1947 broadcast, you'll find yourself in the dusty Mexican borderlands where our charming rogue Cisco faces an adversary as unexpected as his name is absurd. "Señor Bullfrog" opens with the distant croak of frogs echoing across a moonlit hacienda, setting an eerie tone that gives way to intrigue and danger. A mysterious stranger operating under this ridiculous alias has been systematically robbing wealthy landowners, but here's the twist—he's also distributing the stolen wealth to the desperate poor, making him a complicated thorn in Cisco's side. As Cisco and his loyal companion Pancho wade deeper into the investigation, the question becomes increasingly complex: is Señor Bullfrog truly a criminal, or merely another Robin Hood operating in the shadows of a corrupt frontier? The episode crackles with genuine moral ambiguity, superior dialogue, and the kind of clever misdirection that kept millions of listeners glued to their radios.

The Cisco Kid, starring the impeccable Vess Powell, represented something revolutionary in American radio during the 1940s—a genuinely sympathetic Mexican protagonist in an era when such characters were routinely portrayed as villains or comic relief. Created to capitalize on the beloved 1935 O. Henry film, the radio series elevated its source material into something richer: complex tales of justice, honor, and redemption set against an authentic-feeling frontier landscape. Each episode balanced action-adventure with character depth and social consciousness rarely found in pulp entertainment.

If you're seeking radio drama that respects your intelligence while delivering genuine excitement, tune in now and experience why The Cisco Kid remains an essential entry point into the golden age of broadcasting.
