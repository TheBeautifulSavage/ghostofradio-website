Picture yourself hunched over the radio on a March evening in 1954, the crackle of static giving way to the measured, authoritative voice of your host as he guides you into the dark woods of New Hampshire. "The Tiger and Brad Ferguson"—a title that promises danger lurking in civilization's margins, where a man's grip on sanity unravels like a frayed rope. This episode reconstructs a harrowing true crime that shocked a quiet New England community, weaving together police investigation, courtroom testimony, and the chilling question: what drove an ordinary man to extraordinary violence? As the narrative unfolds through interviews and dramatic reenactments, listeners are drawn deeper into the psychological abyss, where the line between predator and prey blurs in the New England darkness.

Crime Classics arrived at a pivotal moment in American broadcasting, when the nation's appetite for authentic crime stories had reached a fever pitch. CBS's commitment to "actual occurrences" distinguished the show from pulpy competition—these were real cases, real victims, real consequences. By 1954, the series had established itself as a gold standard in the true crime genre, attracting listeners who craved the psychological depth and moral complexity that separated genuine criminal investigation from mere melodrama. The March 10th broadcast, originating from Armed Forces Radio Service archives, represents the show at its narrative peak, when production values and storytelling sophistication were firing on all cylinders.

If you've ever wondered what kept millions of Americans glued to their sets each week, seeking truth in the darkness of criminal psychology, this episode is an essential listen. Crime Classics reminds us that reality, when properly told, needs no embellishment. Tune in and discover why this series remains one of old-time radio's most compelling and historically important programs.
