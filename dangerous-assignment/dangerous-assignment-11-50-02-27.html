# Dangerous Assignment: "The Smugglers of Port Said"

Picture yourself hunched close to your radio on a winter evening in 1950, the amber glow of the dial casting shadows across your living room as Steve Mitchell—that intrepid troubleshooter for the State Department—finds himself cornered in the labyrinthine alleys of Port Said, Egypt. In this episode, Mitchell must navigate a treacherous web of international arms smugglers, ruthless operatives, and double-crossing informants who would gladly see him disappeared into the Mediterranean. The percussion of distant gunfire echoes through the broadcast, mixed with the sultry wail of Egyptian strings, as our hero races against time to prevent a shipment of weapons from destabilizing an already volatile region. Will Mitchell's quick wits and diplomatic cunning be enough, or will he become another forgotten casualty in the shadowy world of Cold War espionage?

*Dangerous Assignment* arrived on American airwaves during a transformative moment in global affairs—when the nascent Cold War was reshaping international politics and the United States was establishing itself as a global power. The show's premise reflected real anxieties about American agents operating in exotic locales, combining the glamour of international travel with genuine geopolitical tension. Brian Donlevy's portrayal of Mitchell embodied post-war American confidence: resourceful, morally steadfast, yet pragmatic enough to operate in morally ambiguous situations. Each episode was ripped from headlines that listeners recognized, making the adventure feel immediate and consequential.

Don't miss this gripping installment of *Dangerous Assignment*—where every broadcast brought danger from the far corners of the world directly into American homes. Tune in and discover why audiences kept this show near the top of the ratings throughout the early 1950s.
