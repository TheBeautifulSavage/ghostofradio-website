# Death Valley Days: Sam Bass

*Original broadcast: 1936*

---

Settle in close to your receiver as Death Valley Days transports you to the dusty trails of Texas, where legend and infamy blur into one electrifying tale. In "Sam Bass," listeners will experience the rise and violent fall of one of the Old West's most notorious outlaws—a man whose name still echoes through saloon stories and frontier folklore. As the orchestra swells with dramatic strings and the announcer's voice cuts through the static with ominous authority, you'll find yourself riding alongside Bass and his gang through desperate robberies and thrilling chases. The program's signature blend of historical authenticity and theatrical tension crackles through your speakers: the thunder of hoofbeats, the crack of gunfire, the tense whispered conversations between desperate men. This isn't mere entertainment—it's history brought vividly to life, where moral ambiguity and the harsh realities of a lawless era unfold before your very ears.

Death Valley Days had become America's preeminent western anthology by 1936, a position earned through meticulous historical research married with compelling drama. The show's creators understood that listeners didn't want sanitized tales—they craved authentic stories of real figures like Sam Bass, the Texas bandit whose life embodied the dying gasps of the Old West. Each episode served as a kind of radio documentary, researching actual events and real personalities while maintaining the theatrical flair that kept millions tuned in night after night. The program's success lay in honoring its sources while never sacrificing the gripping human drama that made listeners forget, for thirty minutes, that they lived in an increasingly modern world.

Don't miss this classic chapter in radio's golden age. Tune in and discover why Death Valley Days captivated a nation hungry for stories of daring, danger, and destiny.
