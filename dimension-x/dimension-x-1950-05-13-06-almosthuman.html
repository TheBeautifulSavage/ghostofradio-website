# Dimension X: "Almost Human" (May 13, 1950)

Step through the laboratory door and prepare yourself for a chilling exploration of what it means to be alive. In "Almost Human," listeners will encounter a scientist's desperate gamble—an attempt to bridge the impossible gulf between mechanism and consciousness. As the tension mounts through crackling dialogue and the eerie hum of imagined machinery, you'll find yourself wrestling with profound questions about the nature of humanity itself. Is a thinking, feeling creation any less real than its maker? What price does ambition demand when it reaches toward the divine? The episode unfolds with the deliberate pacing that made Dimension X a master of psychological science fiction, building dread not through monsters or explosions, but through the quiet terror of ideas taken too far.

Dimension X arrived at a pivotal moment in American culture, when atomic anxiety and technological wonder collided in the popular imagination. Broadcasting during the golden age of radio drama, the show transformed NBC's Sunday evening slot into a gateway to fantastic worlds, attracting writers and actors at the height of their craft. This 1950 episode exemplifies the program's distinctive approach: grounded in plausible science yet unafraid to probe the existential mysteries that lurked beneath postwar optimism. Unlike the space opera adventures dominating the airwaves, Dimension X preferred intimate character studies set against cosmic backdrops, treating its listeners as intelligent adults capable of handling ambiguity and moral complexity.

Don't miss this forgotten gem of radio's final golden age. "Almost Human" endures as a masterclass in atmospheric storytelling, a reminder that the most powerful science fiction doesn't require elaborate special effects—only a skilled cast, a haunting script, and the listener's own imagination. Tune in and discover why Dimension X remains an essential listening experience.
