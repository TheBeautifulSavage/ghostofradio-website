The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

"Big Impossible" is a gripping installment of Dragnet, Jack Webb's iconic crime drama. The atmosphere is tense and foreboding, as Lieutenant Joe Friday and his trusty sidekick Sergeant Bill Gannon investigate the murder of a wealthy businessman at a Hollywood charity gala. The victim, a prominent film producer, was found dead in his hotel room with no signs of forced entry or struggle – making it a "big impossible" for the detectives to crack the case. As they dig deeper, Friday and Gannon uncover a web of deceit and corruption that leads them from the glamorous world of Hollywood to the seedy underbelly of Los Angeles' crime scene.

The drama is heightened by the presence of the victim's beautiful and enigmatic wife, who seems to be hiding secrets of her own. Her motives are unclear, but her actions suggest a deep-seated desire for revenge against her husband's business rivals. As Friday and Gannon get closer to the truth, they must navigate a complex landscape of alliances and rivalries that threaten to destroy their investigation altogether. The tension builds to a shocking climax as the detectives finally uncover the killer – but not before a stunning twist that changes everything.

The characters in "Big Impossible" are as well-crafted as ever, with Friday's straight-laced no-nonsense approach providing a perfect counterpoint to Gannon's more impulsive and emotional nature. The chemistry between the two leads is undeniable, and their banter adds a welcome layer of humor to an otherwise dark and gripping episode.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

It was a pivotal moment in American history when "Big Impossible" aired on NBC in 1955 – the height of the Cold War and the Red Scare. The threat of communism loomed large, with Senator Joseph McCarthy's witch hunts still in full swing. In this charged atmosphere, crime dramas like Dragnet resonated deeply with audiences who were desperate for escapism from the real-world anxieties of the day.

The production of "Big Impossible" coincided with a period of significant cultural and social change in America – the rise of suburbanization, the growth of the middle class, and the increasing visibility of women's roles in society. These changes are reflected in the episode itself, which explores themes of identity, power dynamics, and the complexities of human relationships. The show's writers cleverly wove these themes into the narrative, creating a rich tapestry that continues to captivate audiences today.

Despite its focus on crime and detection, "Big Impossible" also speaks to broader concerns about morality and social justice. In an era when trust in institutions was at an all-time low, the detectives' struggle for truth and justice resonated deeply with listeners who were feeling disillusioned with the system.

WHY IT MATTERS:

The craft of Dragnet is what makes "Big Impossible" a compelling listen today – it's a masterclass in storytelling, atmosphere, and character development. The episode showcases the show's unique blend of procedural drama and social commentary, which was unparalleled in its time.

The writing in "Big Impossible" is particularly noteworthy for its nuance and complexity – the characters are multidimensional and relatable, with rich inner lives that make them feel like real people. The pacing is expertly managed, building tension and suspense through a series of clever plot twists and red herrings. Even decades after its initial broadcast, "Big Impossible" remains a gripping and thought-provoking listen – a testament to the enduring power of radio drama as a medium for storytelling.
