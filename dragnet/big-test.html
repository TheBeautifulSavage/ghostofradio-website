The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

It's a sweltering summer evening in Los Angeles, and Sergeant Joe Friday is on the beat. The sound of sweat-drenched jazz music fills the air as the iconic theme song fades out, replaced by the eerie tone of the radio announcer: "The story you are about to hear is true. Only the names have been changed." This is Big Test, an episode that expertly weaves together the complexities of human nature with the anxieties of post-war America.

Sergeant Friday is tasked with investigating a high school where a mysterious exam has left two students dead and several more on the brink of insanity. As Joe digs deeper into the mystery, he uncovers a web of deceit and corruption that threatens to destroy not just the school, but the very fabric of society itself. The tension builds as the audience is led on a thrilling ride through the dark underbelly of suburban America.

Throughout the episode, Jack Webb's signature deadpan delivery brings gravity and nuance to the role of Joe Friday, while the supporting cast delivers equally impressive performances that add depth to the narrative. The sound design is masterful, with the crackling of police radios and the wail of sirens perfectly capturing the urgency and chaos of the situation.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

It's a hot summer in 1955, and America is still reeling from the aftermath of World War II. The country is plagued by fears of communism, nuclear war, and social upheaval. The Red Scare has reached its peak, with accusations of communist infiltration spreading like wildfire through the nation. Meanwhile, the Cold War is heating up, with the threat of Soviet expansion and atomic proliferation hanging over the world like a sword of Damocles.

As the country struggles to come to terms with these existential threats, radio drama becomes an increasingly popular form of escapism. Listeners tune in for stories that allow them to temporarily forget their worries and immerse themselves in thrilling adventures, clever mysteries, and heartwarming tales of everyday life. Dragnet, in particular, taps into this desire for reassurance, presenting a world where justice prevails and the forces of good ultimately triumph over evil.

The success of radio drama during this period can be attributed to its ability to provide a sense of comfort and familiarity in uncertain times. Listeners know what they're getting: a trusted narrator, expertly crafted storylines, and memorable characters that seem to leap off the airwaves. Dragnet's blend of realism and straightforward moralizing resonates with audiences seeking solace from the anxieties of the atomic age.

WHY IT MATTERS:

Despite being produced over 60 years ago, Big Test remains a compelling listen today for its masterful storytelling and nuanced exploration of human psychology. Jack Webb's performance as Joe Friday is particularly noteworthy, bringing a level of depth and nuance to the character that has influenced generations of actors and writers.

One of the most striking aspects of this episode is its prescience. The themes of anxiety, paranoia, and social upheaval are all eerily familiar today, making Big Test feel uncomfortably contemporary. The sound design, too, holds up remarkably well, with the crackling of police radios and the wail of sirens creating a sonic landscape that feels disturbingly relevant to our own era's fears about terrorism, surveillance, and social unrest.
