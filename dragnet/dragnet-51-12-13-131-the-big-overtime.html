# Dragnet: "The Big Overtime"

Picture this: a cold December night in Los Angeles, 1951. The streets are slick with rain as Sergeant Joe Friday of the LAPD Homicide Division finds himself working late into the evening, chasing down leads on a case that refuses to close. In "The Big Overtime," listeners are thrust into the unglamorous reality of police work—the grinding overtime, the tedious interviews, the meticulous documentation that separates solid detective work from wild speculation. As Friday and his partner methodically piece together clues, the tension builds not through Hollywood theatrics but through the authentic procedural details that made Dragnet a phenomenon: a suspect's wavering testimony, a timeline that doesn't quite add up, the relentless logic of a seasoned investigator who won't rest until the facts point unmistakably toward the truth.

Created by Jack Webb, a former police officer himself, Dragnet revolutionized radio drama by abandoning melodrama in favor of documentary realism. Working closely with the actual LAPD, Webb crafted episodes drawn from real case files, capturing the profession's true rhythm and moral weight. By 1951, the show had become must-listen radio, with millions of Americans tuning in to hear the unglamorous but compelling work of solving crimes through patience and procedure. Webb's deadpan narration—"This is the city: Los Angeles, California"—became iconic, and his emphasis on facts over fiction influenced not only radio drama but eventually transformed television and film crime storytelling.

If you've never experienced Dragnet's particular brand of compelling authenticity, "The Big Overtime" is the perfect entry point. Settle in with your radio dial and prepare for an evening of genuine detective work, where the real drama lies not in car chases or gunfire, but in the disciplined pursuit of truth. This is police work as it actually happens—methodical, exhausting, and absolutely riveting.
