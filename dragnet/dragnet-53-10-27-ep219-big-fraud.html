# Dragnet: "Big Fraud" (October 27, 1953)

Step into the shadowy streets of Los Angeles as Sergeant Joe Friday and Officer Bill Gannon pursue a cunning con that cuts to the heart of post-war American trust. When a victim stumbles into their precinct with a tale of deception and lost savings, the detectives begin their methodical unraveling of a sophisticated fraud scheme—the kind that preys on ordinary citizens trying to build their futures in a booming economy. With the terse, staccato delivery that made Dragnet legendary, Jack Webb guides listeners through the painstaking detective work: the interviews, the paper trails, the small contradictions that crack wide open. You'll hear the authentic sounds of the bullpen, the typewriters clacking, and the quiet determination of lawmen who refuse to let the guilty slip away. This is police work stripped of glamour—it's unglamorous, methodical, and utterly riveting.

By 1953, *Dragnet* had become the gold standard of police procedurals, pioneering a documentary-like realism that would influence television for decades to come. Webb's partnership with the LAPD lent the show unparalleled authenticity; cases were drawn from actual files, procedures were accurate to the minute, and the show's unflinching portrait of urban crime resonated deeply with audiences still adjusting to a changing America. "Big Fraud" exemplifies what made the program essential listening—not explosions or melodrama, but the honest work of justice pursued one careful step at a time.

Don't miss this masterclass in crime radio. Tune in to *Dragnet* and discover why America's ears were glued to their dials every week, watching the LAPD—one case at a time—protect and serve.
