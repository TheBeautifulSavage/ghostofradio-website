# Dragnet: "Big Little Jesus"

On this December evening in 1954, Sergeant Joe Friday returns to the streets where Los Angeles never truly sleeps. A stolen nativity figure—a small wooden Jesus—has vanished from a church, and what begins as a seemingly minor theft will pull Friday deeper into the shadowy world of black market religious artifacts and the desperate people who traffic in them. With his characteristic deadpan delivery and relentless attention to detail, Friday methodically pieces together a web of petty criminals, fences, and small-time operators, each interrogation a study in human nature. The episode crackles with the authentic procedural rhythm that made *Dragnet* essential listening: the careful notes, the verification of alibis, the slow accumulation of facts that build toward an unexpected moral complexity. This is police work stripped of glamour, presented as it truly was—methodical, sometimes mundane, but ultimately revealing something profound about faith, desperation, and redemption.

*Dragnet* had become appointment radio by the mid-1950s, with creator and star Jack Webb pioneering a revolutionary documentary realism that transformed the police procedural genre. Drawing from actual LAPD case files, Webb's show rejected melodrama in favor of procedure, making the unglamorous work of detective bureaus and patrol officers compelling through authenticity and restraint. "Big Little Jesus" exemplifies the show's sophisticated approach to moral storytelling, treating its stolen religious artifact not as mere plot device but as a lens through which to examine human vulnerability and faith in post-war America.

This is *Dragnet*—where every case matters, every detail counts, and truth emerges from the painstaking work of dedicated officers. Tune in and follow Sergeant Friday as he pursues justice through the fog-laden streets of Los Angeles, where the smallest object can reveal the largest truths about the human heart.
