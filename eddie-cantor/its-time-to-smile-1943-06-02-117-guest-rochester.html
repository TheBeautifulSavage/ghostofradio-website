# It's Time To Smile - June 2, 1943

Picture yourself settling into your favorite armchair on a warm June evening in 1943, the wooden cabinet radio glowing softly before you. As Eddie Cantor's unmistakable banjo-eyed enthusiasm fills your living room, you're transported into a world of infectious comedy and sparkling entertainment. This particular broadcast crackles with special energy—Rochester, Jack Benny's legendary sidekick and one of radio's sharpest comic minds, joins Eddie for an evening of rapid-fire banter, clever sketches, and the kind of improvisational magic that made appointment radio listening an American ritual. The chemistry between these two masters of timing is electric; their repartee builds momentum like a jazz solo, with perfectly placed zingers landing like musical notes in the swinging orchestral arrangements that underscore their every exchange.

By 1943, The Eddie Cantor Show had already cemented itself as one of broadcasting's most durable and beloved programs, having survived the transition from NBC to CBS while maintaining its essential character. Cantor himself was a vaudeville legend whose career spanned decades, and his ability to blend sentimental moments with laugh-out-loud comedy made him accessible to audiences of all ages. During wartime, when Americans huddled around radios for both news and escape, variety programs like this became essential cultural oxygen. Rochester's appearance was particularly significant—a Black entertainer commanding respect and laughs alongside white stars was still groundbreaking in an era of rigid racial barriers, though radio's intimacy allowed certain boundaries to become more permeable than in other entertainment mediums.

This episode represents radio at its most vital and alive—unscripted, spontaneous, and utterly of its moment. For collectors and newcomers alike, it's a masterclass in entertainment and a window into how Americans found joy and connection during wartime. Don't miss the magic.
