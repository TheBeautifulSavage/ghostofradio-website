# It's Time To Smile - May 24, 1944

Step into a Manhattan studio on a spring evening in 1944, where Eddie Cantor's infectious energy crackles through the airwaves with the urgency of wartime America. This particular broadcast crackles with special excitement—the legendary tenor Morton Downey is in the house, and the two performers immediately ignite the kind of musical sparring match that made radio appointments unmissable. You'll hear Cantor's rapid-fire gags punctuated by his signature falsetto, the studio audience roaring with approval, while Downey's rich baritone cuts through with Irish charm and sophisticated phrasing. The banter flows effortlessly between comedy sketches, sentimental ballads, and novelty numbers, with Cantor's eye-popping delivery ("Bananas!") landing perfectly amid the orchestral swells of the NBC house band. In May 1944, with American troops fighting across Europe, this show served as more than mere entertainment—it was a lifeline of laughter for families gathered around their receivers, a weekly reprieve from war news and worry.

The Eddie Cantor Show remained one of broadcasting's most durable hits, running continuously for over two decades and evolving from its vaudeville roots into sophisticated comedy variety. Cantor himself was already a legend by the 1940s, a Ziegfeld Follies veteran whose transition to radio proved effortless; his manic energy and rapid-fire delivery translated perfectly to the medium. Episodes like this one showcase why he commanded such loyalty—his willingness to share the spotlight with genuine talent, his impeccable timing, and his ability to make listeners feel like they were witnessing spontaneous magic rather than a carefully scripted show.

Settle in with this May 1944 broadcast and experience radio's golden age at its zenith, when a comedian and a singer could still surprise each other—and delight millions listening at home.
