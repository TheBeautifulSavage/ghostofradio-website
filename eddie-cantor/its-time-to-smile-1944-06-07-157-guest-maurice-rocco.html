# The Eddie Cantor Show: "It's Time To Smile" (June 7, 1944)

Picture yourself settling into your favorite chair on a warm June evening in 1944, tuning your radio dial to catch Eddie Cantor at his peak. Tonight's broadcast crackles with an electric energy as the legendary entertainer welcomes pianist Maurice Rocco, whose lightning-fast fingers and boogie-woogie virtuosity will set the studio ablaze. You can almost hear the audience lean forward in anticipation as Cantor's distinctive voice cuts through the static with his trademark warmth and mischief. Between comedy sketches that poke gentle fun at wartime life and musical interludes that showcase Rocco's dazzling technique, there's a palpable sense of escapism—a nation desperate for laughter during uncertain times finding exactly what it needs. The chemistry between host and guest sparkles across the airwaves, with Cantor's rapid-fire wit playing off Rocco's musical prowess in that golden-age variety format that made radio the heartbeat of American entertainment.

By 1944, Eddie Cantor had already dominated American popular culture for over two decades, transitioning seamlessly from vaudeville to Broadway to radio, where his gifts for comedy and musical performance found their perfect medium. The Eddie Cantor Show represented the zenith of network variety programming—a format that blended comedy, music, and guest stars in a carefully orchestrated hour designed to unite families across the continent. During World War II, shows like this one served a crucial cultural purpose, providing morale-boosting entertainment while Cantor himself remained an active supporter of the war effort and charitable causes.

If you appreciate the golden age of radio when variety was truly varied, when genuine comedic talent could make you laugh out loud, and when a pianist could make his instrument sing with unbridled joy, then "It's Time To Smile" awaits you. Tune in and experience why millions of Americans made this Thursday night appointment essential listening.
