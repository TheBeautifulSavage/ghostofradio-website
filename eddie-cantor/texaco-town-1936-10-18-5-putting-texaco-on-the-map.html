# Texaco Town (October 18, 1936) – "Putting Texaco On The Map"

Step into a bustling October evening in 1936 as Eddie Cantor, America's beloved "Banjo Eyes," takes the Texaco stage with his characteristic energy and infectious charm. This week's broadcast promises a rollicking hour of comedy sketches, snappy musical numbers, and the kind of rapid-fire wit that made Cantor a household name. With his supporting cast in tow—including the smooth harmonies of the DeMarco Sisters and the skilled musicianship of his orchestra—listeners will find themselves caught up in a whirlwind of entertainment as Eddie spins tales of small-town America and the resourceful characters who inhabit it. The episode's title, "Putting Texaco On The Map," hints at the show's clever integration of its sponsor's brand into the very fabric of the comedy, blending commerce and entertainment in a way that feels utterly natural to Depression-era radio audiences.

The Eddie Cantor Show had become a cultural institution by 1936, representing the golden age of radio variety entertainment. Cantor's vaudeville roots and impeccable comedic timing made him perfect for the medium, while the Texaco sponsorship provided the financial backing for elaborate production values that set the show apart from its competitors. This was radio at its most ambitious—live performers, orchestral arrangements, and writing that balanced comedy with genuine artistry, all designed to attract and hold millions of listeners week after week.

For fans of classic radio, this episode exemplifies everything that made the format so captivating: the immediacy of live performance, the warmth of familiar voices in your living room, and the magic of imagination taking flight. Tune in and discover why Eddie Cantor remained America's entertainer for over two decades.
