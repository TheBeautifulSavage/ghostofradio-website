# Texaco Town 1937 01 10 (17) - Oscar Is Still Missing

Picture this: January 10th, 1937. Across America, families gather around their glowing radio sets as Eddie Cantor's unmistakable voice crackles through the speaker with characteristic manic energy. But tonight, there's an edge beneath the comedy—Oscar, the show's beloved running character, has vanished without a trace, and Eddie is beside himself. What unfolds is a masterclass in vintage radio comedy: a frantic search punctuated by Eddie's rapid-fire one-liners, slapstick sound effects, and cameo appearances from guest stars who become reluctant participants in the chaos. The tension between genuine concern and comedic absurdity keeps listeners on the edge of their seats, never quite sure if this will resolve into heartwarming reunion or spectacular comedic disaster. It's the kind of episode that had neighbors gathering at windows, desperate to know what happened next.

By 1937, The Eddie Cantor Show had become appointment radio, drawing millions of listeners to Texaco's sponsored variety program. Cantor himself—already a vaudeville legend and motion picture star—had perfected the art of translating his manic, physical comedy style to the intimate medium of radio. His ability to make listeners laugh while keeping them genuinely invested in the show's characters demonstrated why he remained one of entertainment's most bankable names. During an era when radio was the primary form of mass entertainment, episodes like this one showcased the medium's unique power to create shared cultural moments that transcended geography and class.

Don't miss this delightful snapshot of 1930s entertainment at its peak. Tune in now and experience the magic that made families abandon their dinner conversations and the energy that made Eddie Cantor a household name.
