# Texaco Town: April 11, 1937 - "Casting Gone With The Wind"

Picture yourself settled into your favorite chair on an April evening in 1937, the warm glow of your radio dial beckoning you into Eddie Cantor's world. Tonight's episode crackles with the electricity of Hollywood's biggest sensation—the casting frenzy for "Gone with the Wind." Eddie and his writers have seized on the nation's obsession with who will play Scarlett O'Hara and Rhett Butler, launching into sketch after hilarious sketch of would-be starlets and desperate actors auditioning in the most ridiculous ways imaginable. You'll hear the rapid-fire comedy that made Cantor a household name, watch him transform voices for each character, and feel the audience's laughter wash over you like you're sitting in the studio itself. The energy is infectious, the timing impeccable, and the satire of Hollywood's vanity bites just sharp enough to make you chuckle knowingly.

Eddie Cantor's variety show was the gold standard of American radio comedy, blending vaudeville sensibility with contemporary topical humor that kept listeners coming back week after week. In 1937, as David O. Selznick's search for Scarlett had become national news—captivating millions who'd read Margaret Mitchell's novel—Cantor understood the cultural moment perfectly. His ability to mine comedy from real headlines while maintaining warmth and charm made his program essential listening for families across America. The Texaco sponsorship added a touch of commercial glamour befitting the era's most polished entertainment.

Let yourself experience the golden age of radio comedy, where wit, timing, and the power of the human voice created entire worlds in the listener's imagination. Eddie Cantor's masterful performance on this episode captures a vanished era when Hollywood dreams felt tangible to every American with a radio set. Tune in and discover why millions made this their appointment with entertainment.
