# Texaco Town - May 30, 1937

Step into the gleaming studio of NBC's broadcasting temple as Eddie Cantor takes the stage for this special transatlantic transmission, a technological marvel beamed across the Atlantic to listeners huddled around their wireless sets in England. The audience erupts with anticipation as the banjo-eyed comedian bounds into the microphone, his infectious energy crackling through the airwaves with the kind of manic exuberance that made him vaudeville royalty and radio's reigning court jester. You'll hear the lush orchestrations of his live band, the roaring applause of the studio audience, and Cantor's rapid-fire patter weaving comedy sketches, popular songs, and shameless plugs for Texaco gasoline with the practiced ease of a man who has perfected the art of live entertainment. The episode crackles with that golden-age spontaneity—you can almost smell the greasepaint and cigarette smoke wafting from Studio 8-H.

By 1937, Eddie Cantor had already ruled radio for six years, transforming The Texaco Town variety hour into appointment listening for millions of Americans and, now, Britons experiencing this special broadcast. The Texaco sponsorship gave the show unlimited resources for guest stars and musical numbers, making it the gold standard of radio entertainment. Cantor's ability to blend Jewish vaudeville sensibilities with mainstream American humor while maintaining an edge of sophisticated silliness made him irreplaceable—a performer whose ad-libbed remarks and improvisational genius meant no two broadcasts were ever quite the same.

This is radio at its zenith: live, unpredictable, and thrumming with the electricity of the moment. Tune in and experience the unmistakable magic that made Eddie Cantor an entertainment legend and radio itself the heartbeat of American popular culture.
