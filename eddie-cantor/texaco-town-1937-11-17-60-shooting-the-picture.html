# The Eddie Cantor Show: "Shooting The Picture" (November 17, 1937)

Step into the glittering chaos of a Hollywood sound stage as Eddie Cantor takes you behind the velvet curtain of moviemaking in this delightful Texaco Town broadcast. With his trademark rapid-fire patter and infectious energy, Eddie guides listeners through the hilarious mishaps of producing a picture, complete with temperamental stars, befuddled directors, and the kind of physical comedy that leaps right through your radio speaker. You'll hear the clink of equipment, the exasperated shouts of crew members, and Eddie's own masterful comedic timing as he finds himself caught between ambitious producers, demanding actors, and the relentless march of the shooting schedule. It's a behind-the-scenes romp that captures both the glamour and the absurdity of 1930s Hollywood at its peak.

By 1937, The Eddie Cantor Show had become America's most treasured comedy broadcast, with Cantor's vaudeville-trained wit and his ability to blend topical humor with universal comedy making him a household name. This particular episode arrives at a fascinating moment—just as talking pictures had fully displaced radio as Hollywood's primary medium, yet when radio remained the dominant entertainment force in American homes. Cantor's decision to satirize the very film industry that was simultaneously competing for his audience's attention shows his confidence and sharp understanding of contemporary culture. His guests and supporting players bring their own considerable talents to the production, creating an ensemble performance that exemplifies why millions tuned in each week.

Don't miss this gem of pre-war radio comedy. "Shooting The Picture" showcases Eddie Cantor at his creative peak—a master showman holding court in America's living room, turning the machinery of entertainment itself into comedy gold. Adjust your dial and settle in for sixty minutes of genuine, vintage entertainment.
