# The Eddie Cantor Show: Texaco Town - December 29, 1937

Picture yourself in a comfortable living room on New Year's Eve, 1937, as Eddie Cantor's unmistakable voice crackles through your radio speaker with infectious energy and warmth. This Texaco Town broadcast finds the master of ceremonies in rare form, presiding over an evening of vaudeville excellence with special guests Dave Rubinoff, the celebrated violinist whose instrument seemed to sing with human emotion, and the crooner Tony Martin, whose smooth baritone was making young ladies swoon across America. Eddie's rapid-fire jokes, delivered with that characteristic rapid patter and infectious giggle, punctuate musical numbers and comedic sketches that range from the topical to the absurd. You can almost hear the live studio audience erupting in laughter as Cantor performs his legendary routines—the kind of entertainment that made families gather around their sets and forget, if only for an hour, the lingering anxieties of the Depression.

The Eddie Cantor Show represents the golden age of American radio comedy at its zenith. By 1937, Cantor had become one of the most beloved entertainers in the nation, a man whose genuine warmth and immigrant's appreciation for American opportunity won him audiences across class and geographic lines. His variety format—blending comedy, music, and drama—became the template that countless shows would follow, and his ability to attract top-tier talent like Martin and Rubinoff ensured that listeners received genuine artistry alongside genuine laughs. This episode, recorded just days before the new year, captures a moment of American popular culture at peace with itself, before the world would change forever.

Don't miss this window into an America that still believed in radio as the hearth of home entertainment. Tune in and experience the artistry, the immediacy, and the irrepressible spirit that made Eddie Cantor a legend.
