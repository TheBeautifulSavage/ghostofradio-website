# The Eddie Cantor Show: Texaco Town - March 23, 1938

As the opening theme swells on this historic spring evening, listeners across America settle in for what they don't yet know will be the final broadcast of Eddie Cantor's legendary variety hour. The energy crackles with a bittersweet electricity—Cantor's distinctive voice, that rapid-fire delivery and infectious laugh, leads the audience through a carefully crafted evening of comedy, music, and charm. The Texaco sponsorship has dressed the broadcast in the gleam of modernity, yet there's an unmistakable poignancy hanging over the proceedings. Cantor and his cast of regulars deliver their sketches and musical numbers with the practiced precision of seasoned professionals, unaware that this March night marks the end of an era. The studio audience's applause seems particularly warm, particularly engaged, as if sensing something momentous is slipping away.

For seven years, The Eddie Cantor Show has been must-listening for millions, a fixture in American homes as reliable as the Texaco pump on the corner. Cantor, the vaudeville legend with the enormous eyes and infectious comic genius, has dominated radio's golden age alongside Fred Allen and Jack Benny. This final episode represents the twilight of variety radio's first great flowering—a format that brought live entertainment directly into parlors and kitchens, binding a nation through shared laughter and song. The show's cancellation signals shifting tastes and the medium's own evolution toward dramatic serials and news programming.
