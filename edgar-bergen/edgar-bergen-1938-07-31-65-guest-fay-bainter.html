# Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show

## July 31, 1938

Step into the summer evening of 1938 and settle into your listening chair as Edgar Bergen and his impudent wooden partner Charlie McCarthy prepare for another evening of mischief and merriment. On this sweltering July night, the show welcomes the talented Fay Bainter, bringing Hollywood glamour into living rooms across America. What unfolds is pure magic—the kind only radio can conjure—as Bergen's masterful ventriloquism translates into perfectly timed dialogue, Charlie's caustic one-liners land with machine-gun precision, and the chemistry between the wooden dummy and his human foil crackles with the kind of rapport audiences simply couldn't get anywhere else. Bainter's presence elevates the comedy into new territory, as the elegant actress plays straight woman to Charlie's insolent quips, creating moments of genuine warmth beneath the rapid-fire gags.

This episode exemplifies why The Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show became one of radio's most beloved institutions, attracting millions of listeners despite—or perhaps because of—the fundamental absurdity of a ventriloquist dominating a medium where nobody could see him. Bergen's brilliance lay in his ability to make listeners *forget* they were listening to a dummy, investing in Charlie's personality so completely that the wooden partner became utterly real. By 1938, the show had already become a cultural phenomenon, with Charlie McCarthy receiving thousands of fan letters weekly and even inspiring his own comic strip. Bergen's ability to generate comedy from the interplay between host and dummy, all while welcoming top guest stars, created an irresistible formula.

If you've never experienced the particular joy of golden-age radio comedy at its finest, or if you're a devoted fan revisiting old favorites, this 1938 episode offers the perfect window into why millions tuned in faithfully each week. Tune in and discover the magic of an era when imagination and impeccable comic timing were all you needed to hold a nation spellbound.
