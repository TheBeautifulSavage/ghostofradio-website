# Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show – September 3, 1939

As the NBC orchestra strikes up that familiar, jaunty theme, you can almost hear the studio audience settling into their seats at Radio City—this is the night everything changes. With Hitler's armies rolling into Poland that very morning, Americans are tuning in to their radios for comfort, and Edgar Bergen knows just how to deliver it. Charlie McCarthy is in rare form tonight, his wooden lips ready with a barrage of wisecracks aimed at Bergen himself, while the witty Wendy Barrie—fresh from the silver screen—trades banter with the dummy in that breathless, flirtatious way that sets hearts racing. But there's something else in the air tonight, an electric tension beneath the laughter; the world is lurching toward war, and this half-hour of pure vaudeville magic feels suddenly precious, a defiant spark of American humor and innocence flickering against gathering darkness.

The Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show had already become a national institution by 1939, proof that a ventriloquist could command the most intimate medium ever invented. Bergen's genius lay not in fooling anyone—listeners *knew* Charlie was a dummy—but in the intimacy of the illusion, the way Charlie's sardonic personality seemed to emerge from some deeper truth about Bergen himself. With guests like Barrie and the rubber-faced Vera Vague, the show balanced scripted comedy sketches with genuine, spontaneous moments that kept audiences guessing. This episode arrives at a pivotal moment, capturing radio comedy at its height while the real world waits breathlessly for what comes next.

Step back to September 1939, when the world was smaller and a wooden dummy could make millions forget their worries for thirty glorious minutes. Tune in and discover why America loved Charlie McCarthy—and why this particular broadcast feels like a snapshot of a nation on the cusp of transformation.
