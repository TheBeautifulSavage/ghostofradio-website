# Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show - November 16, 1941

Step into Studio 8-H at NBC Radio City as Edgar Bergen settles into the microphone with his wooden partner Charlie McCarthy for an evening of sophisticated comedy that defined an era. On this November night in 1941, the show crackles with the energy of Hollywood stardom—ethereal beauty Gene Tierney, already ascending to cinematic heights, brings glamorous charm to the stage, while Tierney's presence ignites Bergen's razor-sharp wit and Charlie's notorious jealousy. What unfolds is a delicious interplay of ventriloquism, rapid-fire banter, and the kind of ensemble comedy that kept millions of Americans glued to their radio sets, laughing in their living rooms and parlors. Bergen's masterful control of his dummy's personality—spoiled, vain, and endlessly entertaining—creates comedy gold as he plays off his illustrious guest, while the studio audience roars with approval. You can practically hear the cigarette smoke and smell the anticipation filling that grand studio.

The Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show represented the pinnacle of radio entertainment during the Golden Age, a phenomenon that captivated audiences from 1937 through the mid-1950s. Bergen's ventriloquism translated into pure magic over the airwaves—listeners could see nothing, yet they believed absolutely in Charlie's independent existence. This 1941 episode captures the show at its zenith, just weeks before America's entry into World War II would reshape entertainment and national consciousness. The program's success spawned a film career for Bergen and Charlie, proving that this dummy was no mere prop but a genuine cultural icon.

Tune in to experience classic American radio at its finest—where a man, his wooden dummy, and brilliant guests created entertainment that transcended the medium itself. This is comedy as it was meant to be heard.
