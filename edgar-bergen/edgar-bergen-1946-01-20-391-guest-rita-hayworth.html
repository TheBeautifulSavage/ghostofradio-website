# The Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show: January 20, 1946

Step into the NBC studios on this crisp January evening as Edgar Bergen takes the stage with his impudent wooden companion, Charlie McCarthy, to welcome one of Hollywood's most glamorous stars—the legendary Rita Hayworth. What unfolds is a masterclass in live variety entertainment: Bergen's quick wit sparks against Charlie's precocious wisecracks while Mortimer Snerd stumbles through his rustic malapropisms, all building to the delicious moment when the incomparable Hayworth graces the microphone. The interplay between the ventriloquist's invisible puppets and the radiant actress creates an electric tension—can you almost hear the studio audience leaning forward in anticipation? There's an undeniable frisson as Bergen and his dummies trade innuendos with Hayworth, testing the very boundaries of what radio propriety will allow, all while the orchestra swells beneath them.

By 1946, Bergen had already spent nearly a decade as radio's most improbable star, proving that a man and his wooden dummy could captivate millions of listeners who would never see a single gesture or expression. This was the golden age of radio comedy, when wit traveled through airwaves and imagination completed the picture. Hayworth's appearance represents the magnetic pull radio held on Hollywood's brightest talents, each eager to reach the vast evening audiences gathered around their sets. The war has just ended, and America is hungry for glamour, laughter, and escape.

Tune in to experience the crackle and charm of an era when entertainment meant ensemble talent, quick comedy, and the thrill of live broadcast. Hear how Bergen and his companions navigate the minefield of comedy with a visiting legend—no second takes, no safety net, just the pure electricity of performers at their peak. This is radio as it was meant to be heard.
