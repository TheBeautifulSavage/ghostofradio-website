# The Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show

## February 10, 1946

Picture yourself settling into your favorite armchair on a winter's evening, the warm glow of your radio dial beckoning you into the Beverly Hills playhouse where Edgar Bergen's wooden dummy comes delightfully alive. This February broadcast crackles with the particular magic that made America fall in love with ventriloquism through sound alone—as Bergen and the impertinent Charlie McCarthy spar with their trademark rapid-fire wit, the studio audience erupts in knowing laughter at jokes that somehow feel fresh despite the pair's extraordinary popularity. Tonight's guest, the brilliant Spanish pianist José Iturbi, brings an unexpected elegance to the chaos, his virtuosity contrasting beautifully with Charlie's adolescent sass and Mortimer Snerd's rural innocence. The interplay between high culture and vaudeville comedy creates an irresistible tension, a uniquely American collision of sophistication and slapstick that only Bergen's skillful hand—literally and figuratively—could orchestrate.

By 1946, Bergen had already spent nearly a decade as radio's most peculiar phenomenon: a ventriloquist whose true artistry lay not in fooling audiences, but in creating an entire world of compelling characters that transcended the impossibility of the medium itself. Charlie McCarthy had become a household name, invited to Hollywood parties and receiving fan mail by the thousands, while Bergen's ability to breathe distinct personalities into both his dummies and his guests had transformed variety radio into something approaching theatrical art. This episode captures the show at its confident zenith, post-war America hungry for entertainment that demanded both intelligence and uninhibited laughter.

Don't miss this chance to witness a genuine golden age celebrity collision, where a world-class musician becomes willing participant in Bergen's comedic universe. Tune in and discover why millions of listeners made this appointment their most cherished evening ritual.
