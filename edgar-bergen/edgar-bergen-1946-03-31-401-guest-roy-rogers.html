# Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show - March 31, 1946

Step into a radio studio packed with a live audience on this spring evening in 1946, where ventriloquist Edgar Bergen prepares to share the microphone with none other than Roy Rogers, the King of the Cowboys himself. The energy crackles with anticipation as Bergen settles in with his celebrated wooden dummy, Charlie McCarthy—whose razor-sharp wit and impeccable timing have made him a household name. Listen as Charlie trades barbs with Bergen before Roy Rogers arrives, bringing the authentic charm of the American West into the studio. With Rogers on hand, expect hilarious exchanges between the cowboy star and the wise-cracking dummy, musical performances that showcase Rogers' considerable talent, and the kind of spontaneous comedic gold that only live radio could deliver. The audience's laughter punctuates every moment, making you feel transported directly into that bustling NBC studio.

What makes this particular broadcast remarkable is its snapshot of post-war American entertainment in its golden age. Bergen's dummy act was no novelty—Charlie McCarthy had become so culturally significant that listeners debated whether the dummy or Bergen was the true comedian, a testament to the magic of radio that demanded nothing but voices and imagination. By 1946, Bergen commanded top dollar and A-list guests, having recently returned from war bond tours that kept morale high across the nation. Roy Rogers' appearance here represents the collision of two entertainment titans at their peak: radio's undisputed comedy champion meeting Hollywood's biggest western star, both at the height of their powers.

Tune in to experience why millions of Americans made this their must-listen appointment each week. This is entertainment when spontaneity reigned supreme and comedic timing was everything—before tape machines, before retakes, broadcasting live into the American living room.
