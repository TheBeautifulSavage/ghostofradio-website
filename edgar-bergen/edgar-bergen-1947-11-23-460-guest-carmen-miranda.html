# The Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show: November 23, 1947

Settle into your favorite chair and prepare yourself for an evening of sophisticated comedy and uninhibited laughter as Edgar Bergen and his impudent wooden partner Charlie McCarthy welcome the incomparable Carmen Miranda to the studio. Picture the scene: a live audience buzzing with anticipation, the orchestra tuning their instruments, and Bergen preparing for the carefully choreographed chaos that ensues when Charlie's jealous, quick-tongued personality collides with showbiz glamour. Miranda's legendary charm and exotic mystique provide the perfect foil for the dummy's relentless heckling and wisecracks, while Bergen orchestrates the mayhem with the precision of a master puppeteer—both literally and figuratively. This is vaudeville translated to the microphone, where timing, chemistry, and the audience's inability to see the strings makes the magic all the more potent.

The Bergen-McCarthy partnership had become a national institution by 1947, an unlikely phenomenon that elevated ventriloquism from carnival tents to prime-time prestige. What made this show revolutionary was Bergen's refusal to let audiences forget they were watching a dummy speak—instead, he weaponized that knowledge, letting Charlie's very artificiality become his greatest asset. The wooden dummy said what real people couldn't, flirted shamelessly, and interrupted his creator with impunity, all while Bergen maintained his bemused composure. With guest stars like Carmen Miranda—herself a larger-than-life personality who'd conquered Hollywood—the show became a high-wire act of competing egos and comic timing.

Don't miss this sparkling artifact of American entertainment at its most confident and imaginative. This is radio at its finest: pure performance, unbounded creativity, and the kind of spontaneous magic that kept millions of listeners glued to their sets every Sunday night.
