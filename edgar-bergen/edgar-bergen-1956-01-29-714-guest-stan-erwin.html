# The Edgar Bergen & Charlie McCarthy Show – January 29, 1956

Settle into your favorite chair and prepare for an evening of sophisticated comedy that only Edgar Bergen and his irreverent wooden partner Charlie McCarthy could deliver. On this January night in 1956, the ventriloquist and his dummy return with guest Stan Erwin, whose impeccable timing and versatility promise delightful comic interplay with Bergen's ensemble. Expect the familiar warmth of the orchestra's opening strains, Bergen's smooth patter, and Charlie's perfectly-timed wisecracks—that beloved wooden voice carrying an edge of mischief that somehow feels more human than many flesh-and-blood performers. The chemistry between Bergen and his dummy had become the gold standard of American entertainment; listeners tuned in knowing they'd experience something approaching magic: a man conjuring laughter from sawdust and paint, yet somehow creating a character more real and beloved than many Hollywood stars.

By 1956, Bergen had been perfecting this act for nearly two decades on radio, transforming what could have been a vaudeville novelty into an institution. The show's format—mixing ventriloquism with comedy sketches, musical numbers, and celebrity guests—had proven endlessly adaptable, allowing Bergen to remain relevant even as television began reshaping entertainment. This particular broadcast arrives near the end of the show's run on network radio, a period when the medium itself was undergoing seismic change. Yet Bergen's artistry transcended the technology; millions still gathered around their receivers, eager to hear Charlie's impudent observations and Bergen's genial responses.

Don your imaginary tuxedo and join generations of listeners who understood that true entertainment transcends the medium. This is classic American radio at its most polished and entertaining—a master performer at the height of his considerable powers.
