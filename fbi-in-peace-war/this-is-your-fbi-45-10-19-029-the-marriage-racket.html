As the familiar trumpet fanfare fades and the narrator's authoritative voice cuts through the static, listeners are plunged into the shadowy world of matrimonial swindlers preying on desperate, lonely hearts. In this October 1945 broadcast, an unsuspecting woman's dreams of romance become the bait in an elaborate con, drawing the FBI into a tangled web of forged documents, assumed identities, and shattered trust. You'll hear the click of typewriters in the Bureau's field offices, the tense dialogue between agents hot on the trail, and the pitiful testimony of victims whose savings and dignity have been stripped away by smooth-talking charlatans. The tension builds methodically—as it always does on This Is Your FBI—toward the moment when federal agents close the net and justice prevails. This is crime as it happened, ripped from actual case files, delivered with the gritty realism that made America's living rooms feel like front-row seats to the nation's law enforcement.

This Is Your FBI debuted in 1945 with unprecedented access and cooperation from J. Edgar Hoover's FBI itself, lending the series an authenticity that competitors couldn't match. Each episode dramatized real cases, with scripts vetted by the Bureau to ensure accuracy while maintaining entertainment value. The show perfectly captured postwar America's anxieties about con artists and criminals exploiting the vulnerability of ordinary citizens—particularly the lonely and the hopeful. In an era before television's dominance, radio dramas like this one wielded enormous cultural power, shaping public perception of federal law enforcement while entertaining millions.

Don't miss "The Marriage Racket"—a cautionary tale wrapped in first-rate dramatic storytelling that reminds us why the FBI earned America's trust. Tune in as the Bureau's finest uncover the truth behind the con.
