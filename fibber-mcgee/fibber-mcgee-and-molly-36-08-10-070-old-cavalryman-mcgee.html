Picture this: it's a quiet evening on Maple Avenue when Fibber's latest tall tale threatens to unravel the entire McGee household. In "Old Cavalryman McGee," our lovable fibber decides that his mundane civilian existence simply won't do—he's convinced the neighbors (and himself) that he once served as a distinguished cavalry officer with a chest full of medals and a horse named Buttercup. As Molly rolls her eyes with that perfect blend of exasperation and affection only a long-suffering wife can muster, the chaos spirals delightfully out of control. When a genuine ex-cavalry officer appears on Maple Avenue, Fibber's elaborate web of embellishments comes dangerously close to unraveling, leading to mistaken identities, slapstick misunderstandings, and some of the most authentic neighborhood gossip you've ever heard crackle through your radio speaker.

For nearly two decades, Fibber McGee & Molly dominated American airwaves as one of broadcasting's most beloved comedies, proving that a simple premise—a slightly dishonest husband and his clever wife—could sustain an empire of laughter. The show's genius lay in its specificity: the McGees weren't caricatures but rather recognizable people living on an ordinary street, dealing with recognizable neighbors like the ever-present Mr. Harlow and Teeny. By the 1940s, as the nation grappled with wartime anxieties, Fibber's harmless fibs and Molly's sharp comebacks provided the perfect respite.

Tune in to experience radio comedy at its most masterful—where impeccable timing, genuine chemistry between actors Jim and Marian Jordan, and razor-sharp writing created magic that still resonates seventy-plus years later. This is Fibber McGee & Molly in its golden age.
