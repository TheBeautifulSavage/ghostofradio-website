# Fibber McGee and Molly: "Forty Percent Off" (December 9, 1941)

Step into the cluttered living room of 79 Wistful Vista as Fibber McGee stumbles upon the deal of a lifetime—a clearance that promises forty percent savings on goods that nobody particularly needs. What follows is a masterclass in comedic escalation as the scheming Fibber attempts to convince his sharp-tongued wife Molly that this bargain is worth the precious wartime dollars in their pocket. With war just two days old and the nation reeling from Pearl Harbor, this episode captures radio audiences seeking refuge in the familiar comfort of Fibber's tall tales and Molly's withering rejoinders. Listen for the running gag that builds to near-hysteria, the perfectly-timed sound effects of domestic chaos, and the razor-sharp banter that made this husband-and-wife team America's favorite fibbers. The laughter that erupts from your speaker feels doubly precious now—a moment of normalcy in an uncertain world.

Fibber McGee and Molly was more than comedy; it was the national living room during America's defining moments. Premiering in 1935, Jim and Marian Jordan's characters had become so woven into the fabric of American life that when Pearl Harbor shattered the nation's peace just days before this broadcast, listeners turned to their radio sets seeking the one thing that hadn't changed: the predictable, hilarious machinations of Fibber's get-rich-quick schemes and Molly's patient exasperation. The show's chemistry, refined through hundreds of episodes, offered a psychological anchor during crisis—proof that the world, despite everything, could still be funny.

Tune in to this December 1941 episode and hear how a nation laughed at the edge of the abyss, finding hope and humanity in the simple domestic comedy of a man too clever for his own good and a woman too smart to fall for his schemes. This is radio gold from radio's golden age.
