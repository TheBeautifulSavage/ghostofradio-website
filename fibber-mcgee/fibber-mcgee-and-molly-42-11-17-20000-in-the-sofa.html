# Fibber McGee and Molly: November 17, 1942

Picture this: it's a crisp November evening in 1942, and the McGees' modest home at 79 Wistful Vista hums with delicious possibility. When Fibber discovers a mysterious envelope stuffed deep within the sofa cushions containing a staggering twenty thousand dollars, chaos erupts in the form of schemes, schemes within schemes, and Molly's increasingly exasperated asides. What follows is pure comic gold—Fibber's wild theories about the money's provenance, his increasingly elaborate plans to keep it hidden from tax collectors and nosy neighbors, and the steady, knowing interjections from his long-suffering wife who sees through every transparent lie with the precision of a woman who's spent years perfecting the art of marital patience. The famous McGee sofa, that repository of the ridiculous and sanctuary of slapstick, becomes the focal point of an evening that bristles with misdirection, mistaken identities, and the kind of physical comedy that made listeners clutch their sides even though they couldn't see a thing.

This episode captures Fibber McGee and Molly at the height of its popularity, when the show dominated Thursday nights and families across America gathered around their radios as faithfully as they attended church. Created by Don Quinn and starring Jim and Marian Jordan as the titular couple, the program's genius lay in its blend of character-driven humor and theatrical zaniness—a perfect escape during wartime's uncertainties. The couple's chemistry was genuine, their timing impeccable, and their ability to mine comedy from the ordinary details of domestic life was unmatched.

Tune in and experience why this show captivated a nation—where every creak of a floorboard and hesitant "now, don't get mad, Molly" became the stuff of comedy legend.
