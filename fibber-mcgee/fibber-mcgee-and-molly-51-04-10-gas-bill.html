# Fibber McGee and Molly: The Gas Bill (April 10, 1951)

Picture this: the McGee living room on Maple Avenue, that familiar haven of domestic chaos where truth takes a permanent holiday. Tonight, an innocent gas bill arrives at the door—seemingly harmless enough—but in Fibber's hands, it becomes the spark for an elaborate web of fibs, excuses, and increasingly desperate explanations. As Molly calmly observes from her seat, listeners can almost hear her knowing smile through the static. What begins as a simple matter of an overdue payment spirals into Fibber's characteristic masterpiece of misdirection: the meter reader's been faulty, the utility company's made a terrible error, or perhaps—knowing Fibber—he's been secretly heating the entire neighborhood out of pure Christian charity. Each excuse builds upon the last with that rapid-fire comedic timing that kept America glued to their radios every Tuesday night, while Molly delivers her devastating deadpan rejoinders that deflate Fibber's bravado with surgical precision.

For over two decades, Fibber McGee and Molly represented radio comedy at its finest: a show rooted in the genuine affection between its titular characters, where the gags emerged organically from their relationship rather than relying on forced setup-and-punchline mechanics. By 1951, the show had become an American institution, beloved by millions who tuned in faithfully to witness Fibber's latest flights of fancy and his wife's patient, witty demolition of his schemes. The supporting cast—from the harried meter reader to the neighbors drawn into the confusion—created a richly comic world that felt lived-in and authentic.

Slip on your headphones and step back into 1951 Maple Avenue, where a simple gas bill becomes an occasion for comedy that sparkles with genuine human warmth beneath every exaggerated tall tale. This is radio comedy at its golden best.
