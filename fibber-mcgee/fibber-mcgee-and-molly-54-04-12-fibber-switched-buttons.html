# Fibber McGee and Molly: "Fibber Switched Buttons"

Settle into your favorite chair and prepare for mayhem of the highest order. In this delightful April 1940 installment, Fibber McGee's latest scheme unravels with gloriously predictable results when he attempts a simple wardrobe swap—one that leaves him literally buttoned into someone else's life. What begins as an innocent mix-up spirals into a symphony of confusion, misunderstandings, and physical comedy that could only play out over the airwaves with such perfect timing. You'll hear the familiar creak of the McGee's front door, the worried tone of Molly's voice as she realizes what her husband has done, and the wonderfully varied characters of their Madison Street neighborhood descending upon the chaos. The writing crackles with the kind of rapid-fire dialogue and sight gags (performed entirely through sound) that made this show an American institution.

What made Fibber McGee and Molly so beloved was its perfect balance of heart and hilarity. Jim and Marian Jordan had been performing these characters since 1931, and by the 1940s, they had refined their craft to an art form. The show thrived on the gentle domestic comedy of a well-meaning husband constantly outwitted by his smarter, wiser wife—a dynamic that resonated deeply during Depression and wartime America. Each episode was a small escape, a guaranteed half-hour of laughter in an increasingly uncertain world.

Don't miss "Fibber Switched Buttons"—it's a perfect example of why millions of Americans tuned in every Tuesday night, eagerly awaiting whatever predicament Fibber's scheming would create. This is radio comedy at its finest: clever, character-driven, and endlessly entertaining.
