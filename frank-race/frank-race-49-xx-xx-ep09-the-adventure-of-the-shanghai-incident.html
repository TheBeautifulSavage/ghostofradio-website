Picture this: it's late evening, the broadcast crackles to life, and you're transported to the fog-shrouded docks of Shanghai, where Frank Race—globe-trotting investigator and man of action—finds himself ensnared in a web of international espionage and murder most foul. When a mysterious intelligence broker turns up dead in his hotel room, and a priceless jade artifact vanishes into the night, our hero must navigate the shadowy underworld of wartime intrigue, dodging both criminal syndicates and government agents who may or may not be allies. With nothing but his wits, his revolver, and a collection of dangerous contacts, Frank must uncover whether the Shanghai Incident is a simple theft gone wrong or something far more sinister—a plot that could reshape the balance of power in the Pacific itself.

The Adventures of Frank Race arrived in 1949 as syndicated radio was experiencing its final, glorious golden age, offering audiences the kind of sophisticated adventure storytelling that television would soon render obsolete. Created during a time when post-war America hungered for tales of international intrigue, each episode of Frank Race crackled with authenticity—writers wove actual geopolitical tensions into their narratives, and the show's attention to exotic locales and espionage tradecraft gave it a documentary-like credibility that kept listeners riveted. The Shanghai Incident exemplifies the show at its finest, capturing both the noir atmosphere of the era and the specific paranoia of late 1940s Cold War anxieties bleeding backward into wartime nostalgia.

Settle into your favorite chair, dim the lights, and let yourself drift back to an era when radio could transport you halfway around the world with nothing but your imagination and the velvet voice of a first-rate cast. The Shanghai Incident awaits.
