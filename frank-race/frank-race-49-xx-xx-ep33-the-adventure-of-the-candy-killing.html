When Frank Race arrives at the gleaming confectionery of Morton's Fine Candies on a rain-slicked Manhattan street, he expects a simple robbery investigation. Instead, he discovers something far more sinister—the proprietor lies dead among toppled displays of imported Belgian chocolates, and the only witness is a mute shopgirl who communicates solely through frantic gestures. As thunder rumbles over the city and the police close in with their pat answers, our intrepid reporter must unravel a web of greed, passion, and deception that reaches into the highest echelons of New York's business elite. Every shadow holds danger, every clue a puzzle, and with only his wits and a trusted revolver to guide him, Frank Race descends deeper into a mystery that threatens to consume everyone connected to Morton's precious confections.

The Adventures of Frank Race emerged in 1949 as a response to listeners' hunger for smart, sophisticated mystery programming that balanced pulp excitement with genuine detective work. Unlike the hammier adventure serials flooding the airwaves, Frank Race's world was one of urban realism and moral complexity—a hardboiled dream filtered through the sensibilities of post-war America. The show's writers drew inspiration from the literary mysteries of the era while maintaining radio's unparalleled ability to create atmosphere through sound design alone: the crack of a revolver, the distant wail of a siren, the tense silence between suspects.
