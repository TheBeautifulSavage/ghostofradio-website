Mountain Malfeasance

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

Picture this: it's a crisp evening in 1938, and Fred Allen's unmistakable nasal voice crackles through the static with barely contained mischief. In "Mountain Malfeasance," our quick-witted host and his wife Portland venture into the treacherous backwoods where simple folk harbor darker intentions than mere hospitality. What begins as an innocent camping trip spirals into a delightful farce of mistaken identities, absurd local characters, and comedic mayhem that only Allen could orchestrate. Listeners will find themselves breathless as Fred's rapid-fire ad-libbed quips collide with the show's skilled ensemble cast, creating moments of pure comedic gold that couldn't possibly be replicated twice the same way. The mountain setting provides perfect cover for Allen's signature brand of satirical humor—poking gentle fun at rural stereotypes while celebrating the genuine oddities of American life.

By 1938, Fred Allen had established himself as radio's most sophisticated satirist, a comedian unafraid to tackle timely topics with intellectual wit while keeping working families laughing through Depression-era hardships. Unlike the safer comedy of his contemporaries, Allen's show was a free-wheeling variety hour where anything could happen, where celebrity guests mingled with Allen's beloved recurring characters like Senator Claghorn and Titus Moody. His willingness to improvise, to let his writers and performers run with absurdity, made the show appointment listening for Americans craving sharp comedy alongside quality entertainment.

Don't miss this sterling example of Allen's comedic brilliance—a master class in timing, character work, and the lost art of spontaneous radio performance. Tune in and discover why Fred Allen remains a towering figure in American comedy, a voice that defined an era and continues to enchant new generations of radio enthusiasts.
