Buying Citronella Incomplete

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

Step into Portland's bustling streets on a spring evening in 1946, where Fred Allen—radio's sharpest wit and most nimble improviser—tangles with the absurdities of wartime commerce. In "Buying Citronella Incomplete," listeners are treated to Allen's masterful dissection of the bureaucratic nonsense that gripped the nation as it emerged from rationing and wartime restrictions. When a simple errand to purchase insect repellent spirals into a Kafkaesque nightmare of incomplete forms, missing signatures, and officious clerks, Allen's razor-sharp commentary cuts through the veneer of American normalcy with surgical precision. The supporting cast—including the inimitable Peter Donald and Portland Hoffa—weaves an intricate tapestry of comic timing and verbal gymnastics that builds to hilarious, unexpected crescendos. You'll hear the crackle of that distinctive Allen delivery, the audience roaring in recognition of truths too ridiculous to deny.

By 1946, The Fred Allen Show had become radio's intellectual comedy masterpiece, standing apart from the sentimentality and slapstick that dominated the medium. Allen's "Allen's Alley," where he interviewed neighborhood characters with names like Senator Claghorn and Titus Moody, had revolutionized sketch comedy, proving that listeners craved wit that made them think as much as laugh. In an era when radio reigned supreme and comedians wielded genuine cultural influence, Fred Allen was arguably America's sharpest voice—fearless in skewering politicians, corporations, and social pretense alike.

This episode captures Allen at the height of his powers, when his show was a must-listen appointment for Americans hungry for intelligent entertainment.
