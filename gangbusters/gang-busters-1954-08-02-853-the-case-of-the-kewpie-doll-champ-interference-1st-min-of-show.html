# Gang Busters: The Case Of The Kewpie Doll Champ

Picture this: a carnival midway bathed in the glow of summer twilight, where the sharp crack of a rifle range mingles with barkers' calls and calliope music. It's August 2nd, 1954, and listeners tuning into their radios will find themselves thrust into one of radio's most electrifying true crime dramas—*Gang Busters*. In "The Case Of The Kewpie Doll Champ," a seemingly innocent carnival contest becomes the centerpiece of a dangerous criminal scheme. The episode opens with a crackle of static and authentic police radio chatter, that signature sonic signature that made *Gang Busters* unmistakable. As narrators Phillips H. Lord guides us through the investigation, we descend from the bright lights of the carnival into the shadowy world of small-time crooks and big-time trouble, where a prized doll becomes the unlikely centerpiece of deception and crime.

For nearly two decades, *Gang Busters* held listeners in thrall with its commitment to actual case files and real police procedures. Drawing from real FBI and police records, the show achieved a documentary-like authenticity that separated it from mere pulp fiction. By 1954, the series had become an institution of American radio, its influence extending beyond entertainment into public consciousness about law enforcement. Each episode wasn't just drama—it was a window into the actual work of detectives, federal agents, and beat cops fighting crime across the nation.

Don't miss this exciting window into post-war crime and carnival intrigue. Settle in with the crackle of that authentic police radio, the tension in every voice, and the satisfaction of justice served. *Gang Busters* awaits—where real cases meet radio's golden age.
