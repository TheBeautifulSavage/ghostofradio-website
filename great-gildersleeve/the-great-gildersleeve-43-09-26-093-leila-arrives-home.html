# The Great Gildersleeve: Leila Arrives Home

Picture yourself settling into your favorite chair on a September evening in 1943, the amber glow of your radio dial warming the parlor as Throckmorton P. Gildersleeve's world is about to be delightfully turned upside down. In "Leila Arrives Home," the good-natured Gildersleeve finds himself in a state of comic chaos—his niece Leila is returning, and the prospect of her homecoming sets off a chain reaction of hilarious misunderstandings, hastily made preparations, and the sort of neighborly interference that made listeners return week after week. Harold Peary's distinctive, booming voice carries us through the tumult with perfect comedic timing as Gildersleeve bumbles through arrangements, while the supporting cast—including the ever-present Birdie Winthrop and the other residents of Summerfield—adds layers of warmth and absurdity to the proceedings.

The Great Gildersleeve was groundbreaking in its own right, representing radio comedy's sophistication in the 1940s. Spun off from the wildly popular Fibber McGee and Molly in 1941, the show pioneered the situation comedy format that would later dominate television. Peary's creation of the lovable, slightly pompous bachelor uncle became iconic, and his portrayal earned him loyal millions who tuned in faithfully. The show's humor was gentle yet clever, its charm rooted in small-town American life and the universal trials of family obligation and social embarrassment.

Don't miss this window into the golden age of radio comedy, where stellar writing, impeccable voice acting, and the artistry of sound effects combined to create an evening's entertainment that needs no picture—only imagination. Let Harold Peary and the residents of Summerfield welcome you into their world for thirty minutes of genuine laughter and heart.
