# The Great Gildersleeve - Campaign Officially Starts / Easter Show

When you settle in for this rollicking April 1944 broadcast, you'll find the good Throckmorton P. Gildersleeve in full political fever as his campaign for mayor officially kicks into high gear. Harold Peary's distinctive voice—that booming, affable baritone—commands the airwaves as Gildy navigates the delicious chaos of small-town electoral ambitions, complete with bungled strategy meetings, well-meaning but hapless supporters, and the sort of domestic disruptions that made this show an institution in American living rooms. The episode captures perfectly the wartime spirit of earnest civic engagement mixed with genteel bumbling, all seasoned with the show's trademark wit and impeccable comic timing. You can practically hear the studio audience roaring with laughter as our protagonist stumbles through another installment of self-inflicted mishaps, while the Easter-themed elements add a seasonal warmth that grounds the comedy in the real American experience of spring 1944.

By this point in the series' history, *The Great Gildersleeve* had become one of NBC's most beloved programs, a spin-off from *Fibber McGee and Molly* that had evolved into something entirely its own—a sophisticated sitcom that proved radio comedy could be both intellectually clever and genuinely heartwarming. The show's formula of placing an lovable egotist at the center of small-town life resonated deeply with listeners, particularly during wartime when such programs offered comforting escapism and timeless humor. Episodes like this one, blending civic comedy with holiday spirit, showcased why the program maintained such devoted audiences throughout the 1940s.

Slip on your headphones and experience why millions tuned in weekly for Gildersleeve's campaigns, both political and personal. This is golden-age radio at its finest.
