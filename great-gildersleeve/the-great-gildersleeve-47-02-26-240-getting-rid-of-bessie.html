Step into the parlor of Throckmorton P. Gildersleeve's stately Summerfield home as domestic chaos reaches a fever pitch. The pompous, middle-aged bachelor has finally decided that his housekeeper Bessie is more trouble than she's worth—but anyone who knows Gildy knows that executing such a plan requires far more cunning than he possesses. As this February 1947 episode unfolds, listeners will delight in the mounting hysteria as Gildy's schemes to replace Bessie spiral spectacularly out of control, with his hapless nephews Marvin and Leroy caught in the crossfire of his misguided machinations. The rapid-fire banter, classic sound effects, and impeccable comedic timing that define Harold Peary's signature character create an atmosphere of genteel pandemonium that crackles through the airwaves.

The Great Gildersleeve stands as one of radio's most beloved situation comedies, the first spinoff series to achieve phenomenal success in the medium's golden age. Premiering in 1941 as a character born from Fibber McGee and Molly, Gildy's world represented a uniquely American vision of small-town life—one filled with golf clubs and country clubs, pompous pronouncements delivered by a man perpetually outmaneuvered by his own pretensions. What made the show resonate across the 1940s and '50s was its perfect balance of slapstick and sophistication, crude humor tempered by genuine warmth and surprisingly thoughtful character development.

For those seeking an authentic slice of classic radio comedy—the kind that had families gathering around the set, laughing in unison—Getting Rid Of Bessie delivers everything the format promised. Tune in and discover why this half-hour of expertly crafted mayhem remains a masterclass in comedic radio production, a testament to an era when a well-timed pause and a perfectly executed sound cue could bring an entire nation to laughter.
