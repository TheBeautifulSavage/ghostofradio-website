Thornton Gildy Gildersleeve has faced many predicaments in his comfortable Summerfield home, but nothing quite like the ominous official envelope that arrives on this June evening in 1947. The Internal Revenue Service wants answers about his taxes, and our rotund hero's characteristic bluster and charm may not be enough to smooth-talk his way out of this one. As the doorbell rings and the stakes climb higher, listeners will find themselves caught between genuine anxiety for Gildersleeve's fate and the irresistible comedy that erupts when bureaucratic red tape tangles with one man's creative accounting. The talented Harold Peary, the voice and creative force behind Gildersleeve, masterfully balances genuine jeopardy with hilarious misunderstandings, crafting a narrative that crackles with both tension and laughter.

For nearly two decades, The Great Gildersleeve stood as NBC's flagship comedy, a show that transcended the typical sitcom formula by grounding its humor in real, relatable anxieties of American life. Gildersleeve embodied the everyman—vain, well-meaning, perpetually scrambling—and his encounters with authority figures, romantic entanglements, and domestic chaos resonated deeply with post-war audiences seeking comfort in familiar characters and proven laughs. This particular episode, from the show's robust middle years, exemplifies why the program remained appointment listening for millions.

The sound effects crack with authenticity, the timing is impeccable, and Peary's performance as the increasingly flustered Gildersleeve is nothing short of masterful. Whether you're a devoted fan revisiting an old favorite or discovering this golden-age gem for the first time, this episode offers the perfect snapshot of American radio comedy at its finest—intelligent, warmly human, and thoroughly entertaining.
