Picture yourself settling into your favorite chair on a crisp autumn evening, the radio dial glowing warmly in the darkened parlor, when the familiar, boisterous chuckle of Throckmorton P. Gildersleeve bursts through the speaker. In this delightful episode, our favorite bumbling bachelor discovers an abandoned baby nestled in the back seat of his automobile—and what follows is a masterclass in comedic chaos. As Gildy careens between moments of unexpected tenderness and his trademark verbal mishaps, listeners are treated to the kind of heartwarming domestic scramble that made this show an American institution. Will he manage to locate the child's rightful parents? How will his fussy housekeeper Birdie and his ward Marvin react to this tiny houseguest? The answer lies somewhere in the frantic, hilarious territory between Gildy's good intentions and his complete inability to manage even the simplest tasks.

The Great Gildersleeve represented something revolutionary in 1940s radio comedy—a show that proved domestic humor could be both raucously funny and genuinely touching. Starring the incomparable Harold Peary, whose distinctive tenor voice could convey both pompous self-satisfaction and vulnerable sincerity in a single sentence, the program pioneered the sitcom format that would later dominate television. This episode exemplifies the show's perfect balance: genuine sentiment alongside slapstick predicament, all wrapped in the warm sound design and talented ensemble cast that made NBC's Thursday night lineup unmissable for millions of American families.
