Step into the darkened parlor of an old, creaking mansion where Throckmorton P. Gildersleeve finds himself face-to-face with genuine terror—or is it? In this delightfully spine-tingling episode, the Great Gildersleeve's jovial confidence meets its match as mysterious sounds echo through the corridors, spectral figures materialize in the shadows, and even his quick wit seems to falter. What begins as a simple evening takes a chilling turn when our protagonist discovers he's trapped in what locals swear is genuinely haunted. Harold Peary's masterful comedic timing transforms the mounting dread into moments of hilarious panic, as Gildersleeve bumbles through cobwebbed rooms and encounters increasingly bizarre phenomena. The writing brilliantly balances genuine suspense with the comedic relief that makes this character a household favorite.

The Great Gildersleeve represents the golden age of character-driven comedy radio, where a single performer's versatility could anchor an entire program. Running from 1941 through 1957, the show became one of NBC's most beloved properties, spawning a film and comic book adaptations. Peary's creation—a wealthy, slightly pompous, ultimately lovable bachelor managing his household and navigating romantic misadventures—captured the imagination of millions of listeners who tuned in weekly. Episodes like "Haunted House" showcase why: the show could shift effortlessly between broad slapstick, sophisticated humor, and gently spooky atmosphere, all while maintaining the warmth that made audiences care deeply about Gildersleeve's fortunes.

If you've never experienced the delicious thrills of The Great Gildersleeve, or if you're a devoted fan seeking to revisit this classic tale, prepare yourself for an evening of genuine entertainment. Dim the lights, settle in, and let Harold Peary transport you back to radio's greatest era.
