When Matt Dillon faces down a cold-blooded killer in Dodge City, the tension crackles through your radio speaker like distant lightning before a storm. In "Target," our marshal must navigate the treacherous moral landscape of frontier justice when a mysterious gunslinger arrives in town with blood on his hands and vengeance burning in his eyes. William Conrad's weathered voice carries the weight of impossible choices as Dillon discovers that not every case has a clear line between right and wrong. The supporting cast of Dodge's finest—Doc Adams, Kitty, and Chester—add texture to a tale that asks whether justice served in haste becomes injustice in hindsight. Every footstep down Front Street could be the last; every conversation might be a final goodbye.

Gunsmoke revolutionized radio drama by treating the western format with the complexity of genuine literature. Rather than simple good-versus-evil shootouts, the show explored the psychological and moral dimensions of law enforcement in a lawless land. From 1952 through the early 1960s, CBS brought Dodge City into living rooms across America with scripts that valued character development and ethical ambiguity over mere gunplay. This particular episode, preserved from Armed Forces Radio and Television Service broadcasts, represents the show at its height—lean, purposeful storytelling that made listeners lean closer to their sets, desperate to know what Dillon would do next.

Slip on your boots and saddle up for a half-hour that reminds us why Gunsmoke remains the gold standard of radio westerns. You'll find no easy answers in Dodge City, only the unflinching portrait of a lawman doing his best in an impossible world.
