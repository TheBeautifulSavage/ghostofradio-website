Step into the smoky, tension-filled studios of NBC on this December evening in 1939, where Jack Benny's meticulous comic timing meets genuine mystery and intrigue. In part two of "Football Murder Mystery," our hapless star finds himself deeper in the mire—a body has been discovered, suspects abound, and Jack's characteristic nervousness has reached fever pitch. Expect the usual cast of characters caught in uncommon circumstances: Rochester delivering dry-witted observations from the sidelines, Mary Livingstone's wry interjections, and a guest star or two who'll discover that football season carries more dangers than a hard tackle. The writers have crafted something delightfully unusual here—a sustained narrative that defies the variety show format, proving that radio drama and comedy aren't mutually exclusive when executed with Benny's impeccable sense of pacing.

By 1939, The Jack Benny Program had become America's most beloved comedy broadcast, a sophisticated blend of scripted hijinks and vaudeville sensibility that appealed equally to schoolchildren and their grandparents. Benny's willingness to experiment—mixing genres, breaking the fourth wall, treating his radio family with genuine affection beneath the gags—set the standard for entertainment broadcasting. This football mystery two-parter exemplifies his fearless approach: a serialized mystery comedy was virtually unheard of on commercial radio, yet Benny trusted his audience's loyalty and imagination to follow him into uncharted territory.

This is radio at its most magical—no laugh track, no studio audience to signal when to laugh, just the intimate voice of Jack Benny whispering directly into your living room. Tune in and discover why millions tuned in every week to follow their favorite miser, his violin, and his endless misadventures. The mystery awaits.
