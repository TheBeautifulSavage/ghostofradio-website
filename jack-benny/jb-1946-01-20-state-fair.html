State Fair

Step right up, folks, and prepare yourself for one of radio's most delightful misadventures! In this rollicking episode from early 1946, Jack Benny decides to take his entire troupe to the state fair, where the master of comedic timing finds himself in precisely the kinds of ridiculous situations that made millions tune in week after week. You can practically hear the calliope music and smell the corn dogs as Jack navigates the midway games, the livestock exhibits, and all the small-town Americana that made the fair a timeless American institution. Listen as Rochester delivers perfectly timed zingers, as the CBS orchestra punctuates the mayhem with masterful musical cues, and as guest stars add their own flavor to the controlled chaos. The fair becomes a playground for Jack's legendary cheapness and vanity—will he actually win a prize, or will he find some elaborate way to avoid spending a dime?

By 1946, The Jack Benny Program had evolved into America's most sophisticated comedy engine, a weekly masterclass in ensemble timing and character-driven humor. Unlike cruder competitors, Jack's show thrived on subtle delivery, running gags that stretched across entire seasons, and a cast so perfectly calibrated they could mine comedy from the space between laughs. This post-war episode captures the show at its peak, when radio comedy had reached an art form that would never be equaled once television arrived to change entertainment forever.

If you cherish the golden age of radio when comedy meant clever writing and expert vocal performance rather than punch lines, this is essential listening. Tune in and discover why Jack Benny's program remained a cultural touchstone for over two decades.
