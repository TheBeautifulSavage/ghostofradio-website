# The Jack Benny Program: "The Dishwasher Is Broken"

Picture this: it's a crisp January evening in 1953, and Jack Benny's household is in complete chaos. The dishwasher has broken down, and what should be a simple domestic inconvenience becomes a cascade of comedic catastrophe that only Jack Benny could orchestrate. As listeners settle in with their radios, they're immediately thrust into Jack's world of elegant paranoia and calculated miserliness—where a broken appliance isn't just a problem to be solved, but a disaster requiring the full intervention of his supporting cast. Phil Harris arrives with characteristic swagger, Mary Livingstone delivers her trademark zingers, and Rochester's dry wisdom cuts through the mounting absurdity like a knife through butter. The episode builds with the kind of impeccable timing that made Benny a master of his craft, each joke landing with surgical precision as the cast improvises around the central premise.

By 1953, The Jack Benny Program had already spent two decades perfecting the art of domestic comedy, and this episode exemplifies why audiences tuned in religiously across America. Jack had pioneered a revolutionary style of comedy centered on character and timing rather than rapid-fire gags—listeners loved him not despite his cheapness and vanity, but because of them. The supporting cast had become like family, their chemistry unmistakable and their ability to riff off one another unmatched. Radio was entering its twilight era as television beckoned, yet Benny's show remained a beacon of quality entertainment, proving that imagination and wit needed no pictures.

Don't miss this gem from radio's golden age. Tune in and discover why Jack Benny's program remained America's favorite thirty minutes for nearly a quarter century. The dishwasher may be broken, but the comedy is in perfect working order.
