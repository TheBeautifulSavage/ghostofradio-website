# Jack Rents His Maxwell To 20th Century Fox

When the NBC cameras rolled on April 24th, 1955, Jack Benny found himself in a predicament only he could manufacture with such comic precision: his beloved Maxwell automobile—that wheezing, sputtering relic that had become as much a character on the program as Jack himself—was about to become a movie star. What begins as a simple business proposition spirals into a beautifully orchestrated comedy of errors, complete with studio executives, insurance complications, and Jack's pathological inability to make a sensible decision. Listen as Don Wilson's mellifluous announcer's voice introduces the chaos, as Rochester delivers withering asides about the car's dubious Hollywood prospects, and as Jack's trademark stinginess wars against his vanity over contributing to a major motion picture. The Maxwell may be ancient, but in Jack's hands, it becomes the fulcrum upon which an entire episode of madcap comedy balances.

By 1955, The Jack Benny Program had already spent more than two decades perfecting the art of subtle, character-driven humor that would influence comedy for generations. Unlike the broad slapstick that dominated much of early radio, Jack's show relied on timing, deadpan delivery, and the chemistry between its ensemble cast—Dennis Day's Irish tenor, Phil Harris's buffoonish charm, and especially the razor-sharp interplay between Jack and his real-life wife Mary Livingstone. The Maxwell gag itself epitomized this approach: an object so mundane it became mythological through repetition and affection. This particular episode captures the show at its zenith, when the line between Jack's on-air persona and genuine life had become deliciously blurred.

Tune in to experience comedy crafted with the precision of a Swiss watch and the warmth of an old friend's company—a reminder of when radio was America's shared living room.
