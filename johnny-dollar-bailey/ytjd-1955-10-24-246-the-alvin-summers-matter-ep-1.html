# The Alvin Summers Matter, Episode 1

The rain hammers against the windows of a seedy Manhattan hotel as insurance investigator Johnny Dollar lights his second cigarette of the evening. A case lands on his desk that smells wrong from the start—a missing railroad executive, fifty thousand dollars in unaccounted funds, and a widow whose story doesn't quite add up. Bob Bailey's voice crackles through the static with that distinctive weariness, that perfect blend of world-weary cynicism and dogged determination, as Johnny settles into what promises to be a labyrinthine case that will take him from the glittering facade of Park Avenue to the shadowed underbelly of Atlantic City. This is a masterclass in serialized mystery, where the first episode plants seeds of doubt and intrigue that will blossom across multiple installments, keeping audiences glued to their radio sets.

*Yours Truly, Johnny Dollar* was CBS's crown jewel of the investigator genre, and this 1955 episode exemplifies why the show commanded loyal listeners throughout the decade. Unlike the episodic detective shows that had dominated the medium, Johnny Dollar's cases unfolded across multiple episodes—a revolutionary narrative structure that brought cinematic storytelling to radio. Bob Bailey, who took over the role in 1954, brought a naturalistic gravitas to the character, ditching the theatrical bombast of his predecessors for a more grounded, conversational approach. The show's meticulous attention to insurance procedures and investigative legwork made it feel authentic, while Bailey's ad-libbed asides and internal monologues gave audiences an intimate window into a detective's actual thought process.

Settle in with your dial tuned to this classic installment and discover why radio audiences made Johnny Dollar an institution. In an era when television was beginning to lure listeners away, this show proved radio was far from finished.
