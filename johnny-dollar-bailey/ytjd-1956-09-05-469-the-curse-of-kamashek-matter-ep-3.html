Yours Truly, Johnny Dollar (Bob Bailey) CBS · 1956

The Curse Of Kamashek Matter

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

From the moment the opening theme strikes—that crisp, authoritative CBS fanfare—listeners are transported deep into the shadowy world of insurance investigation with hardboiled insurance adjuster Johnny Dollar on the case of the cursed Kamashek artifact. When a priceless Eastern relic surfaces in an estate claim, the line between superstition and sabotage blurs dangerously. Is the series of accidents plaguing those who've possessed the Kamashek merely coincidence, or is someone deliberately manufacturing the legend of a curse to conceal a more calculated crime? Bob Bailey's gruff, world-weary narration pulls you into smoky hotel rooms and dimly lit galleries where danger lurks in every shadow, and the truth remains tantalizingly out of reach. The episode crackles with the kind of low-key menace that defined mid-1950s noir radio—no melodrama, just the steady accumulation of clues, motives, and mounting tension as Johnny inches closer to unmasking either genuine supernatural forces or a brilliantly executed deception.

Yours Truly, Johnny Dollar* represented something unique in American radio's twilight years: intelligent, adult detective storytelling that treated listeners with respect. Unlike the superhero serials that dominated earlier decades, these episodes featured a blue-collar protagonist solving legitimate insurance cases through logic and persistence rather than superpowers. Bailey's delivery—intimate, conversational, tinged with dry humor—became the gold standard for radio noir, and the show's 1955 CBS revival proved audiences still craved this sophisticated brand of mystery entertainment even as television was reshaping American entertainment.

Settle in, dim the lights, and let Johnny Dollar guide you through the labyrinth of the Kamashek curse. In forty-five minutes of pure classic radio, you'll discover whether some mysteries are better left unsolved—or whether the truth, once found, is far stranger than any legend.
