# The Temperamental Tote Board Matter

Picture this: a sultry evening at the racetrack, where the neon glow of the tote board flickers like a dying heartbeat, and everyone from the track manager to the lowliest bettor has something to hide. When a quarter-million-dollar malfunction threatens to expose a web of corruption, insurance investigator Johnny Dollar steps into the murk—his sharp eyes cutting through cigarette smoke and lies with the precision of a surgeon's blade. This June 1954 installment pulls you into a world of fixed races, desperate gambling debts, and the kind of moral ambiguity that made the series legendary. As Dollar peels back layer after layer of deception, you'll find yourself wondering who's really running the game—and whether anyone at the track is telling the truth.

*Yours Truly, Johnny Dollar* occupied a unique space in radio's golden age: it was smart, urban noir for the grown-up listener who wanted their mysteries layered with cynicism and dry wit. John Lund's portrayal of Dollar—the "man with the action-packed expense account," as the show's opening promised—became the template for the hard-boiled private investigator. During its CBS run in the early 1950s, the show thrived on exactly these kinds of cases: small-scale crimes with big implications, where greed and human weakness intersected in shadowy corners of American commerce. Each episode was a masterclass in radio drama, balancing crackling dialogue, atmospheric sound design, and plots intricate enough to reward close attention.

Don't let this one slip past. *The Temperamental Tote Board Matter* is quintessential Johnny Dollar—where a simple insurance claim unravels into something far darker. Tune in and discover why this show still captivates listeners over seventy years later.
