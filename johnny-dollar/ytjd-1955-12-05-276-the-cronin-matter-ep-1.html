The rain hammers against the windows of a San Francisco hotel as insurance investigator Johnny Dollar lights another cigarette and surveys the crime scene before him. A wealthy businessman lies dead under suspicious circumstances, and the fifty-thousand-dollar insurance claim sits on Johnny's desk like a loaded gun. What begins as a routine investigation spirals into a maze of blackmail, hidden identities, and lies that run deeper than anyone expected. Dollar's wry observations and sharp instincts guide listeners through fog-shrouded streets and smoky back rooms where every witness has something to hide and every answer spawns two new questions. The tension builds methodically—this is the first chapter of a story that demands your full attention.

Yours Truly, Johnny Dollar became a phenomenon precisely because it refused to talk down to its audience. Premiering in 1949, the show distinguished itself through tight scripts, sophisticated plotting, and the brilliant performance of Mandel Kramer as the eponymous protagonist—a man whose expense account became as famous as his detective work. By 1955, when "The Cronin Matter" aired, the show had perfected its formula: a mystery that rewarded careful listening, dialogue that crackled with genuine wit, and a hero who belonged in the same breath as Sam Spade or Philip Marlowe. The program proved that radio drama could be intelligent, adult entertainment without sacrificing the thrills that kept audiences coming back.

Settle into your favorite chair, adjust the dial to find that perfect signal, and prepare for ninety minutes of genuine suspense. Johnny Dollar needs your attention—the answers to the Cronin matter won't unfold themselves, and neither will the dark secrets waiting in the San Francisco night.
