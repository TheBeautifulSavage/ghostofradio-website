# The Open Town Matter (Part 4)

Picture this: it's a sweltering July night in 1956, and insurance investigator Johnny Dollar finds himself tangled in the shadowy underbelly of a wide-open gambling town where the stakes are personal and the secrets run deeper than anyone bargained for. In this fourth installment of "The Open Town Matter," Dollar's trademark sharp-tongued wit collides with genuine peril as he closes in on a mystery that reaches into the highest corridors of small-town power. The danger crackles through the static—every conversation fraught with double meanings, every shadow potentially harboring the next twist. You'll hear the ambient sounds of a town that never quite sleeps, the clink of glasses and the murmur of suspicious voices, all building toward a climax that promises answers, but perhaps not the ones anyone wants to hear.

*Yours Truly, Johnny Dollar* stood apart in the golden age of radio as a thinking man's mystery series, one that demanded listeners follow intricate plots across multiple installments without the safety net of a predictable formula. Johnny Dollar himself—that wisecracking, expense-account-obsessed investigator voiced by Bob Bailey—became an icon of mid-century American noir, embodying the postwar paranoia and moral ambiguity that defined the era. By 1956, the show had perfected its craft: snappy dialogue that could deliver exposition without sounding like exposition, sound design that transported you into smoky hotel lobbies and dusty back roads, and plots convoluted enough to keep audiences guessing week after week. This particular episode represents the series at its peak, working through a serialized narrative that rewards attentive listeners.

If you've ever wondered what made radio drama the consummate entertainment of an earlier generation, *The Open Town Matter* is essential listening. Settle in, pour yourself something strong, and prepare to follow Johnny Dollar into the darkness—the resolution awaits, and it's worth every suspenseful moment.
