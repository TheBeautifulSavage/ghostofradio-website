Picture this: San Francisco, 1956. A fog-thick night descends on the Barbary Coast as insurance investigator Johnny Dollar steps into the garish neon glow of the Meg's Palace nightclub—where champagne flows freely, the jazz is hot, and somebody's about to get burned. When a high-rolling gambler winds up dead under suspicious circumstances, Johnny finds himself tangled in a web of blackmail, false identities, and dangerous secrets that someone in this den of sin is willing to kill to protect. The first installment of this two-part case crackles with that unmistakable post-war noir atmosphere: the smart dialogue, the femme fatales lurking in shadows, the sense that in this world, nobody's quite what they seem. As our dollar-a-day man digs deeper, the stakes climb higher, and you'll be left hanging on every word wondering what really happened at the Meg's Palace.

Yours Truly, Johnny Dollar stands as one of radio's last great detective series, thriving during the medium's final golden years when television was already luring audiences away from their sets. Airing on CBS from 1949 to 1962, the show perfected the formula that made radio noir so intoxicating—sharp writing, impeccable sound design, and an unforgettable lead performance by actor Bob Bailey as the world-weary investigator. By 1956, when this episode aired, the show had become legendary for its tightly plotted mysteries and the sense that Johnny Dollar inhabited a real world of genuine danger.

Don your fedora, dim the lights, and let the fog roll in. The Meg's Palace Matter awaits—and with a mystery this twisted, you won't want to miss a single moment. Tune in now and discover why Johnny Dollar remains one of radio's most celebrated detectives.
