# Kraft Music Hall – February 8, 1934

Picture yourself settling into your parlor on a winter's evening, the Philco glowing warmly in the corner as Al Jolson's familiar voice welcomes you to America's most beloved musical variety program. Tonight, the spotlight falls on two titans of American jazz and songwriting: the incomparable trombonist Jack Teagarden, whose golden horn seems to weep with every sliding note, and the young lyricist Johnny Mercer, whose clever wordplay and infectious melodies are already winning hearts across the nation. As the orchestra swells and the commercials for Kraft cheese fade into memory, listeners will experience a magical collision of New Orleans jazz sensibility and modern songcraft—the kind of musical conversation that could only happen on live radio, where spontaneity and artistry reign supreme.

This episode captures Kraft Music Hall at its apex, when the program commanded nearly every radio in America on Thursday nights. Broadcast live from Hollywood, the show represented a new kind of democratic entertainment, beaming sophisticated music and comedy into farmhouses and penthouse apartments alike. Teagarden's appearance was particularly significant; though already a jazz legend at just twenty-eight, his rare formal radio exposure brought his revolutionary trombone technique to millions who would never see him perform in person. Mercer's presence signaled the program's finger on the pulse of Tin Pan Alley, where the next generation of American standards was being born.

Don your headphones and slip back to 1934, when radio was still young, voices still live, and the boundary between performer and listener seemed wonderfully thin. This is where American popular music was made real.
