Kraft Music Hall NBC · 1945

First Song Sentimental Journey, Guest King Cole Trio

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

Step into the warm glow of your living room on this December evening in 1945, as Bing Crosby welcomes you to America's favorite musical gathering place. Tonight's broadcast sparkles with an electricity that permeates the studio—the King Cole Trio, whose silken harmonies have captivated the nation, joins Bing for an unforgettable performance of "Sentimental Journey," a song that has just captured the hearts of a nation yearning for peace. As the orchestra swells and Nat King Cole's velvet voice weaves through the arrangement, you can almost hear the homesick soldiers tuning in from bases across the world, transported by three minutes of pure nostalgic longing. The banter between Crosby and Cole carries the easy warmth of two artists at the height of their powers, trading quips and stories between musical numbers, while sponsor Kraft keeps the evening's lavish production humming smoothly along.

By 1945, Kraft Music Hall had become more than a radio program—it was an American institution, a weekly reminder that despite the war's shadow, culture and entertainment remained vibrant and essential. Bing Crosby's mastery of the medium was unparalleled; his relaxed microphone technique set the standard for generations. The appearance of the King Cole Trio represented a significant moment for Black artists in network radio, still a medium deeply segregated, making this broadcast a quietly historic affair.

This is the golden age of radio, when millions gathered simultaneously around their sets for shared experiences no other medium could provide. Tune in to hear why Americans considered these broadcasts essential as the evening meal—where music, personality, and live performance created magic that transcended the humble speaker.
