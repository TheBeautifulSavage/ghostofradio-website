# Kraft Music Hall: First Song Smile (1948)

Picture yourself settling into your favorite armchair on a Thursday evening in 1948, tuning your radio dial to that familiar warm glow of NBC. As the orchestra swells with the Kraft Music Hall's signature opening, you're transported into an evening of refined entertainment where America's favorite musical talents gather under the studio lights. Tonight's guest is the luminous Doris Day, whose recent film success has made her the nation's sweetheart, and the program promises an intimate showcase of her vocal prowess—a rare opportunity to hear her perform live, unfiltered by the constraints of Hollywood's sound stages. The announcer's confident baritone introduces the evening's selections, and you can almost smell the coffee and sense the crackling anticipation of the studio audience as Day takes her place before the microphone, ready to charm millions of listeners across the country with her crystalline voice and winning charm.

By 1948, the Kraft Music Hall had already established itself as one of radio's crown jewels, having entertained audiences continuously since 1933. This was the golden age of sponsored entertainment, when a commercial product could underwrite an entire evening of high-quality music and humor, and Kraft cheese had become synonymous with sophisticated entertainment. The show represented everything listeners loved about radio: live orchestration, spontaneous moments, and the direct connection between performer and audience that no other medium could replicate. Having Doris Day as a guest during her ascendant year only amplified the episode's cultural significance—she represented the fresh, optimistic spirit of post-war America.
