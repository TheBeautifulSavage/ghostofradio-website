# Let George Do It - Episode: The Robber

Picture yourself in a smoke-filled office on a rain-soaked November night in 1946. George Valentine, the quick-witted private detective with a ready quip and a faster draw, stumbles into his biggest case yet when a panicked woman bursts through his door with a desperate plea. A masked robber has been terrorizing the jewelry district, but there's more to this job than petty theft—there's murder, blackmail, and a dangerous secret waiting in the shadows. With only his wits, his gumshoe instincts, and the crackling voice of announcer Bob Readick urging him deeper into danger, George must navigate a treacherous underworld where every clue could be his last. The sound effects crew brings the noir atmosphere alive: the screech of tires, the blast of gunfire, the ominous creak of a warehouse door. This is detective radio at its finest—fast-paced, atmospheric, and wholly captivating.

*Let George Do It* emerged from Mutual Broadcasting in 1946 just as America was rediscovering the detective genre it had loved for years. Created at the golden age's twilight, the show captured everything that made radio drama irresistible: clever writing, authentic performances, and production values that cost thousands but traveled through the ether for free. Bob Bailey's George Valentine became an instant favorite, a character equally at home trading barbs with cops or criminals. These early episodes, recorded while post-war radio was still thriving, represent a perfect snapshot of the medium's sophisticated storytelling at its peak.

Don't miss this opportunity to experience radio as it was meant to be heard—with your imagination as your only lens and the crackling urgency of professional performers your only guide. Tune in now.
