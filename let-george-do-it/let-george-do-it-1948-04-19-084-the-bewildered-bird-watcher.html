# Let George Do It: The Bewildered Bird Watcher

When George Valentine answers his telephone on a foggy April evening, he finds himself entangled in a peculiar case that begins innocuously enough—a bird watcher gone missing in the marshlands outside the city. But as George prowls through rain-slicked streets and smoky hotel lobbies, the simple disappearance unravels into something far more sinister. There's a woman with secrets, a rival naturalist with darker intentions, and a collection of valuable specimens that someone would kill to possess. Bob Bailey's masterful narration guides listeners through an atmospheric maze of clues and red herrings, where danger lurks not in the shadows of alleyways, but in the quiet, deceptive world of amateur ornithology. The tension builds deliberately, like storm clouds gathering over the wetlands, as George pieces together a puzzle that grows more bewildering with each revelation.

*Let George Do It* thrived during radio's golden age as a triumph of efficient storytelling and character work. Created for the Mutual network, the show ran from 1946 to 1954 during the genre's peak, capturing the essence of American noir during the transition from wartime to Cold War anxieties. Bailey's everyman detective George Valentine became a beloved fixture in living rooms across the nation—a problem-solver who relied on wits and determination rather than superhuman abilities, making him accessible to millions of ordinary listeners. Each episode exemplified the genre's commitment to plot mechanics and genuine mystery craft, with scripts that rewarded careful listening.

This April 1948 episode stands as exemplary radio detective work—a reminder of when entertainment meant genuine puzzle-solving rather than spectacle. Tune in to experience the crackle of period authenticity, the authentic anxiety of the post-war era, and storytelling at its finest. George Valentine is ready to take the case. Are you ready to follow him into the mystery?
