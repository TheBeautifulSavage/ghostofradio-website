When George Valentine answers that fateful phone call on a sweltering June evening in 1948, he doesn't know he's about to step into a labyrinth of twisted family secrets and desperation. A concerned parent's plea for help spirals into something far darker than a simple missing persons case—the investigation pulls George through shadowy tenement hallways and into the gilded drawing rooms where respectability masks unspeakable cruelty. Bob Bailey's measured, world-weary narration guides listeners through each tense confrontation as George peels back layer after layer of deception, his steady voice cutting through the crackling static and orchestral stings that punctuate every shocking revelation. The title itself carries an ominous double meaning: is the "problem child" a runaway, or something far more sinister? By the episode's climax, listeners will discover that sometimes the most dangerous people wear the most respectable faces.

Let George Do It represented the tail end of radio's golden age, a period when millions of Americans gathered around their sets for escapism, thrills, and the comfort of familiar characters. The show's formula—a private detective available for hire, cases that range from mundane to murderous—struck a chord with post-war audiences grappling with anxiety and uncertainty. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine became iconic precisely because he embodied everyman competence; George wasn't a brilliant eccentric but a dogged, resourceful investigator who relied on street smarts and persistence. By 1948, the show had perfected its craft, delivering tight narratives with genuine stakes in just thirty minutes.
