# Let George Do It: The Seven Dead Years

When George Valentine answers the telephone in his modest Chicago office that October evening, he's drawn into a labyrinth of buried secrets and deception that will test every ounce of his detective's intuition. The case begins innocuously enough—a wealthy widow seeks his help locating a missing painting—but as George peels back the layers of her carefully constructed story, he discovers that some years in a person's life leave no trace, no record, no witnesses. What unfolded in those seven missing years? Why do the people who might know seem desperate to keep the past buried? Bob Bailey's distinctive voice carries listeners through fog-shrouded streets and into smoke-filled offices where every shadow harbors a potential suspect. The tension builds methodically, punctuated by the distinctive jazz-tinged score that makes *Let George Do It* instantly recognizable to loyal radio fans. By the final commercial break, George is racing against time to uncover the truth before the widow's past catches up with her—permanently.

*Let George Do It* represented the golden age of radio detective fiction, arriving just as post-war America craved smart, fast-paced mysteries that could be savored in one sitting. The show's success lay in Bailey's naturalistic delivery and the writers' ability to craft intricate plots that didn't rely on gimmicks or supernatural elements. This particular episode, "The Seven Dead Years," showcases the program at its finest—a mystery that feels genuinely puzzling while remaining grounded in the shadowy realism that defined noir.

If you've never experienced George Valentine's particular brand of determined investigation, this is an ideal entry point. Tune in and discover why thousands of listeners made *Let George Do It* an appointment with danger and intrigue every week.
