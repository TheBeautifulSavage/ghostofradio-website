# The Motif Is Murder

When the lights dimmed on March 14th, 1949, radio audiences settled in for another evening with George Valentine, private investigator—a man who'd learned that in this world, murder doesn't always announce itself with a bang. In "The Motif Is Murder," our hero stumbles into a case where the pattern matters more than the crime itself. Someone is killing methodically, deliberately, with a signature so precise it becomes terrifying. As George peels back the layers of this carefully orchestrated conspiracy, the tension crackles through every shadow and whispered confession. This is detective fiction at its finest: the kind where brains outsmart brawn, where the smallest detail—a repeated phrase, a musical note, a glimpse of something ordinary seen from the wrong angle—becomes the thread that unravels everything. The orchestra swells with discordant strings as George realizes he's not hunting a killer; he's chasing a philosophy, a twisted artistic vision rendered in blood and betrayal.

*Let George Do It* arrived in the golden age when radio detectives had become America's nighttime companions, but Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine carved out something distinctly less glamorous than his contemporaries. This wasn't the witty banter of *The Saint* or the superheroics of *Superman*—this was street-level noir, where every case left scars and every solution raised new questions. Running for nine seasons on the Mutual network, the show built a devoted following among those who craved authenticity in their mystery programming, the kind that made you believe George was a real man working real cases in a real, dangerous world.

Tune in now to discover what deadly motif connects these murders—and why understanding it might be the only thing standing between George Valentine and his own final curtain call.
