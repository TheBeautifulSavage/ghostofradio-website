# Let George Do It — "The Four Sided Triangle"

Picture this: a rain-slicked Manhattan street at midnight, the kind of night where shadows pool in doorways and every passerby is a potential suspect. When George Valentine, private investigator, takes on the curious case of "The Four Sided Triangle," he stumbles into a labyrinth of deception where a simple geometry lesson becomes a blueprint for murder. Four people, four motives, four alibis that don't quite add up—and George caught squarely in the middle, his quick wit and sharper instincts his only weapons against a killer who's already proven they can get away with the impossible. This March 1949 episode crackles with the energy of classic noir: the nervous informant's stutter, the sultry dame who may be guilty of more than flirtation, and the steady, sardonic voice of Bob Bailey as George, cutting through the fog of deception with cigarette smoke and keen observation.

*Let George Do It* was one of radio's most sophisticated detective series, thriving during those golden post-war years when Americans couldn't get enough of hard-boiled mystery. Unlike the more theatrical detective programs of the '30s, this Mutual Broadcasting staple grounded its stories in procedural realism and genuine psychological tension. Bob Bailey's naturalistic performance—free of exaggerated theatrical flourishes—made George Valentine feel like a real man solving real crimes, and audiences loved him for it. By 1949, the show had hit its creative stride, weaving increasingly complex plots that rewarded close listening.

Don't miss this tangled tale of deception and murder. Tune in to "The Four Sided Triangle" and discover why *Let George Do It* became a listener favorite—and why George Valentine remains one of radio's most compelling detectives. After all, sometimes the simplest shapes hide the darkest secrets.
