# The Host Of Casa Diablo

Step into the smoky, neon-soaked underbelly of post-war intrigue as George Valentine finds himself trapped in the sultry confines of Casa Diablo—a nightclub where the drinks flow as freely as the secrets, and where every shadow conceals a suspect with motive and means. When the club's enigmatic host turns up dead under mysteriously theatrical circumstances, our quick-witted private investigator must navigate a treacherous web of torch singers, two-bit hustlers, and society figures slumming in the wrong part of town. Bob Bailey's masterful vocal performance captures every nuance of Valentine's razor-sharp deductions and wry observations as the case peels back layers of blackmail, passion, and desperation. The crackle of the orchestra and the ambient hum of the nightclub create an atmosphere thick enough to cut with a knife—you can practically smell the cigarette smoke and expensive perfume as George prowls the premises, separating fact from fiction.

*Let George Do It* stood apart from its detective contemporaries during radio's golden age, eschewing the brutal violence of shows like *The Shadow* in favor of genuine mystery craft and character development. Bailey's Valentine became beloved precisely because he remained accessible—clever but not infallible, brave but recognizably human. This March 1949 episode exemplifies the show's sophisticated writing and production values that kept audiences tuned in faithfully across the Mutual network's far-reaching stations for nearly a decade.

If you've yet to discover why *Let George Do It* commanded loyal listeners from coast to coast, this is the perfect entry point. Dial in for a master class in detective noir storytelling, where cunning beats violence and a quick quip can be as deadly as a .38 Special. The Casa Diablo awaits.
