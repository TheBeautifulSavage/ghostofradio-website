# Let George Do It: End of Summer (August 29, 1949)

As summer's final days fade into autumn haze, private investigator George Valentine finds himself ensnared in a case that promises far more than the usual missing persons and insurance fraud. "End of Summer" opens with the unmistakable sound of Atlantic waves crashing against a New Jersey boardwalk—a deceptively tranquil setting that quickly descends into menace when a wealthy widow's prized emerald vanishes during a charity gala, and the prime suspect turns up dead in a bathing cabin before George can ask his first question. What follows is a breathless seventy-five minutes of red herrings, double-crosses, and the kind of razor-sharp dialogue that made audiences lean closer to their radios, hanging on every revelation. The jazz-inflected score punctuates each twist, while Chester Morris's world-weary delivery cuts through the Atlantic salt spray with the certainty of a man who's seen every con in the book—but perhaps not this one.

By 1949, "Let George Do It" had become the gold standard of Mutual's detective programming, a show that proved noir wasn't merely a cinematic invention but a uniquely American sensibility suited perfectly to radio's intimate medium. Created by writer Henry Curtiss, the program leaned into the everyman detective trope, avoiding the theatrical excess of some of its competitors in favor of genuinely clever mysteries and character work. This particular episode exemplifies why the show remained vital even as television began casting its shadow across the medium—it's a reminder that radio's greatest gift wasn't spectacle, but the infinite theater of the imagination.

Tune in as George Valentine pursues the truth through the last golden days of summer, where every suspect lies, every clue misleads, and only George can navigate the darkness beneath the boardwalk's glittering surface.
