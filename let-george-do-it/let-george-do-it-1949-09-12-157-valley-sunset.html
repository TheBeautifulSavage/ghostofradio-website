# Let George Do It: Valley Sunset

Picture this: A private investigator's office bathed in the amber glow of late afternoon, venetian blinds casting long shadows across a desk scattered with case files and cold coffee. George Valentine is about to stumble into a case that will take him deep into the seedy underbelly of Los Angeles' San Fernando Valley—where orange groves give way to dark secrets, and the sunset promises violence before darkness falls. In "Valley Sunset," our hero finds himself entangled with a missing starlet, a corrupt real estate mogul, and a web of blackmail that threatens to unravel the glittering facade of post-war Hollywood. With snappy dialogue, the unmistakable click of a gun's safety, and sound effects that place you right in the Valley's shadowy corners, this September 1949 episode delivers the hard-boiled thrills that made *Let George Do It* a listener favorite throughout the late 1940s.

By 1949, *Let George Do It* had established itself as one of radio's most reliable purveyors of detective noir, running on the Mutual network with the kind of gritty realism that set it apart from more comedic fare. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine—a man of principle in an unprincipled world—captured something essential about post-war American anxieties: the sense that beneath the nation's prosperity and optimism lurked corruption, betrayal, and darkness. This episode exemplifies the show's golden age, when radio drama commanded the attention of millions of Americans huddled around their sets, and when a skilled sound engineer and tight script could transport you to another world entirely.

Don't miss your chance to step into George's shoes as he navigates the treacherous landscape of "Valley Sunset." Tune in, dim the lights, and let the master of detective radio work his magic—because sometimes, letting George do it means experiencing drama at its finest.
