# Let George Do It - "Double Death" (October 17, 1949)

Picture this: the rain hammering against the windows of a dimly lit office, a telephone ringing in the darkness, and a man's voice—calm, knowing, world-weary—answering the call that will drag him into a maze of murder and deception. In "Double Death," our man George Valentine takes on a case that shouldn't exist: two corpses, identical in every gruesome detail, discovered hours apart across the sprawling city. As George moves through shadowy hotel corridors and smoke-filled speakeasies, the mystery deepens with each clue. Who would want to stage a murder twice? And more troubling still—is the killer sending George a message, or is there something far more sinister at play? This is detective noir at its finest: quick dialogue, taut plotting, and the constant sense that danger lurks just beyond the next corner.

*Let George Do It* emerged as one of radio's supreme achievements in the detective genre, thriving throughout the late 1940s when noir sensibilities dominated both cinema and the airwaves. The show's genius lay in its simplicity—a competent private investigator, snappy scripts by writer extraordinaire Lawrence Klee, and the commanding presence of actor Bob Bailey, whose delivery could shift from flippant charm to steely determination in a heartbeat. By 1949, the show had found its perfect rhythm, balancing humor with genuine menace. "Double Death" exemplifies this golden period, offering listeners the kind of intricate plotting and moral ambiguity that made the genre so captivating to post-war America.

Don't miss this masterclass in radio suspense. Settle in, dim the lights, and let George solve the unsolvable. Your evening wouldn't be complete without it.
