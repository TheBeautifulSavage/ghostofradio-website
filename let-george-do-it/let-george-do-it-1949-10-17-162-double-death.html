# Let George Do It: "Double Death" (October 17, 1949)

When George Valentine answers his telephone on this crisp autumn evening in 1949, he's drawn into a case that will test every ounce of his detective cunning—a murder so elaborate, so meticulously orchestrated, that the killer seems to have pulled off the impossible. Two deaths, separated by circumstance yet bound by malice, threaten to unravel in George's capable hands as he navigates the shadowed streets and dimly-lit parlors where secrets fester. The orchestra swells with ominous strings as our hero peels back layers of deception, following clues that lead nowhere and suspects with alibis as solid as brick walls. This is vintage noir: cigarette smoke curling through dialogue, the relentless tick of a clock counting down to revelation, and the kind of twisted plot that made listeners lean closer to their radios, desperate to uncover the truth before George does.

By 1949, *Let George Do It* had already established itself as a cornerstone of detective radio drama, with Bob Bailey's magnetic performance as George Valentine creating a character both world-weary and incorruptibly honest. The show's formula—one telephone call, one mystery, thirty minutes of pure suspense—became legendary for its economy of storytelling and psychological depth. Unlike the flashier detectives of the airwaves, George solved his cases through reasoning and humanity rather than gunplay, earning him a devoted following among audiences hungry for intelligent crime drama.

Don't miss "Double Death" as George confronts a killer who has seemingly beaten the odds. Tune in to *Let George Do It* and discover why this 1949 episode remains a showcase for everything the golden age of radio detective drama did best.
