# The Silent Waterfall

When George Valentine answers a desperate call from a frightened woman on a rain-soaked January night in 1950, he finds himself drawn into a mystery as deep and dark as the gorge where a priceless emerald has vanished—along with a man's reputation. The waterfall that gives this case its name roars with secrets, its deafening cascade providing the perfect cover for deception and danger. As George descends into the shadowy world of high society theft and blackmail, listeners will be transported to moonlit riverside cliffs and shadowy manor rooms where every shadow conceals a suspect and every whispered word carries the weight of betrayal. Bob Bailey's world-weary narration, crackling with cigarette smoke and cynicism, carries us through a labyrinth of clues where the obvious suspect is rarely the guilty party, and the silent waterfall stands as a monument to perfectly executed crime.

What made "Let George Do It" a beloved staple of the Mutual network throughout the late 1940s and early 1950s was its formula of intelligent mystery writing paired with Bailey's masterful, naturalistic performance—a refreshing departure from the hammy heroics of earlier radio detectives. The show arrived at the perfect moment when Americans, adjusting to postwar life, craved sophisticated storytelling delivered with unflinching noir sensibility. Each episode, like this January broadcast, proved that radio mystery could be cerebral and atmospheric without sacrificing the pulse-pounding excitement that kept listeners huddled around their sets.

Tune in to "The Silent Waterfall" and discover why thousands of devoted fans made George Valentine their after-dark appointment for danger, romance, and the kind of mystery that keeps you guessing long after the final commercial fades.
