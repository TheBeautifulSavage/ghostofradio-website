# The Floaters

When the body of a racetrack tout washes up on the city docks, private detective George Valentine finds himself neck-deep in the murky underworld of fixed races and desperate gamblers. It's January 23rd, 1950, and the fog rolls thick along the waterfront as George navigates a dangerous web of bookmakers, crooked jockeys, and vengeful criminals—each with their own reasons to keep the dead man's secrets buried. This episode crackles with the authentic grit of post-war noir: the rain-slicked streets, the smoky back rooms, the ever-present threat of a bullet with your name on it. Listeners will be drawn into George's relentless investigation as he uncovers not one murder, but a conspiracy that reaches from the betting parlors into the highest circles of the racing establishment. The stakes have never been higher, and neither has the body count.

*Let George Do It* thrived during radio's golden age when a half-hour of suspense and sharp dialogue could transport millions into the underbelly of American crime. This 1950 episode represents the show at its peak—Bob Bailey's weary, world-wise delivery had become iconic, and the writers crafted scripts that balanced fast-paced action with genuine character depth. The show's success lay in its formula of a cynical but essentially honest detective facing corruption on every front, a perfect reflection of postwar anxieties about morality and justice in an uncertain world.

Tune in to *The Floaters* and discover why radio audiences remained glued to their sets each week, eager to follow George Valentine into the shadows. This is detective storytelling at its finest—no visual effects, no fancy camera work, just superb acting, intelligent writing, and the power of the human voice to create an entire world of danger and intrigue. Let George do it—and let him take you along for the ride.
