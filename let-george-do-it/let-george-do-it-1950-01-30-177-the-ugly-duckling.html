# Let George Do It - "The Ugly Duckling"

Step into the shadowed streets of post-war America with George Valentine, the quick-witted private investigator who takes on the cases nobody else will touch. In this January 1950 episode, "The Ugly Duckling," our hero finds himself entangled in a twisted tale of identity, beauty, and the deadly secrets people will keep to hide the truth. When a seemingly plain-looking woman walks into his office, George thinks it's a routine case—but nothing about this dame adds up. As the mystery deepens, we're drawn into a noir world where appearance deceives and danger lurks in the most unexpected places. The atmospheric sound design pulls you right into those gritty office corridors: the click of a revolver, the crackle of cigarette ash, the whispered warnings that come too late. This is detective radio at its finest—taut dialogue, genuine menace, and a mystery that keeps you guessing until the final dramatic revelation.

*Let George Do It* represented something uniquely American about the golden age of radio: an accessible, affordable form of sophisticated entertainment that reached millions of listeners in their living rooms each week. Running from 1946 to 1954 on the Mutual network, the show became a template for the modern private eye drama, later influencing television's detective genre. Actor Bob Bailey brought Valentine to life with a perfect blend of hard-boiled cynicism and everyman charm, making audiences feel they were sitting right beside him, unraveling each week's convoluted case.

Don't miss "The Ugly Duckling"—tune in to hear why George Valentine remained radio's most dependable detective. This is classic noir storytelling preserved in time, waiting to transport you back to 1950.
