# Let George Do It - "Go Jump In The Lake"

When George Valentine accepts what seems like an easy case—a simple matter of a missing diamond brooch—he finds himself neck-deep in something far more sinister than a society theft. Set against the frozen shores of Lake Michigan on a bitter winter's night, this February 1950 episode plunges listeners into a world of blackmail, jealousy, and secrets that shimmer beneath the surface of polite society like ice threatening to crack. The sharp dialogue crackles with menace as George circles closer to a truth someone would kill to keep buried, while the haunting orchestration and carefully placed sound effects—the wind howling off the water, footsteps crunching on frozen ground—create an atmosphere thick with dread. By the time George uncovers the real reason the brooch vanished, listeners will understand why the title takes on a far more sinister meaning.

*Let George Do It* arrived on the Mutual Broadcasting System at a pivotal moment for radio drama. By 1950, the golden age was beginning its twilight, yet this show remained one of the finest examples of hard-boiled detective fiction adapted for audio—sharper and more character-driven than its competitors, with a protagonist defined not by invincibility but by persistence and moral conviction. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine became iconic precisely because George was always in over his head, relying on wits and conscience rather than heroic invincibility. This episode exemplifies the show's mastery of atmospheric storytelling and complex plotting during its peak years.

Don't miss this classic slice of noir atmosphere. Tune in to "Go Jump In The Lake" and discover why *Let George Do It* remains the thinking person's detective drama.
