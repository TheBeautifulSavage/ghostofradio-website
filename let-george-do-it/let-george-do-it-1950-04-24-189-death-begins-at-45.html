# Let George Do It – Death Begins At 45

Picture this: a rain-slicked street corner in the shadowy underbelly of the city, where a woman's desperate plea for help crackles through George Valentine's telephone line at midnight. She whispers a cryptic warning—"Death begins at 45"—before the line goes dead. From that haunting opening, *Death Begins At 45* plunges listeners into one of the most atmospheric episodes of the 1949-1950 season, as our weary private investigator must decode an impossible riddle while threading through a labyrinth of blackmail, betrayal, and deadly secrets. The clock is ticking. The stakes have never been higher. Bob Bailey's world-weary narration cuts through the crackling static like a knife through velvet as he races against time to save a life and uncover the sinister truth lurking behind those cryptic words.

Since its debut in 1946, *Let George Do It* had become the Mutual network's crown jewel of detective drama, a show that proved radio's golden age could still deliver genuine thrills without relying on the supernatural or the sensational. Bailey's George Valentine was no caped crusader—he was a gritty, cynical gumshoe navigating a morally ambiguous world where not everyone could be saved and justice was often compromised. By 1950, the show had hit its stride, with writers crafting increasingly inventive mysteries that showcased Bailey's impeccable timing and the sound design wizardry that made listeners feel they were personally shadowing him through rain and danger.

Don't miss this masterwork of radio noir. Tune in and discover why *Let George Do It* captivated millions across America, one dark and dangerous case at a time. Death may begin at 45, but the thrills begin right here.
