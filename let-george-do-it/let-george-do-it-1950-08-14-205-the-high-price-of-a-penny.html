# Let George Do It: The High Price of a Penny

On a sweltering August night in 1950, private investigator George Valentine finds himself entangled in a case where a single penny becomes the thread connecting murder, blackmail, and desperate greed. When a wealthy businessman turns up dead in his locked study, the only clue seems laughably insignificant—a gleaming penny left deliberately on his desk. As George peels back the layers of this seemingly simple mystery, he discovers that someone's past misdeeds, long thought forgotten, have come crashing back with lethal consequences. The noose of suspicion tightens around a cast of characters, each harboring secrets dark enough to kill for, and George must navigate treacherous terrain where trust is currency more valuable than any coin. The tension builds like humidity before a storm, punctuated by sharp dialogue and the ever-present threat that the killer might be closer than anyone suspects.

*Let George Do It* remains one of the golden age's finest detective dramas, distinguished by its grounded approach to noir storytelling and Bob Bailey's magnetic portrayal of a private eye who never takes the easy way out. The Mutual network series, running from 1946 through 1954, stood apart from more sensational competitors by grounding its mysteries in genuine detective work and psychological complexity rather than mere theatrics. By 1950, the show had hit its creative stride, with writers crafting intricate plots that rewarded careful listeners—the kind of story where a penny's significance only becomes clear in the final moments.
