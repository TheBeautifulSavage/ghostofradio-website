# Let George Do It: How Guilty Can You Get

The fog rolls thick through the Chicago streets on this February evening in 1951 as George Valentine finds himself tangled in a web of circumstantial evidence and desperate lies. When a seemingly innocent man walks into his office claiming he's been framed for a murder he didn't commit, George must navigate a labyrinth of false confessions, blackmail schemes, and witnesses whose memories prove as unreliable as a two-bit alibi. The question isn't simply whodunit—it's how guilty can you get when everyone around you is willing to take the fall? As the pressure mounts and time slips away, listeners will find themselves questioning their own assumptions, drawn deeper into a mystery where the truth seems almost impossible to untangle from the mountain of deception.

Let George Do It arrived at a unique moment in radio's golden age, when the hard-boiled detective genre had reached its sophisticated peak. Bob Bailey's portrayal of the quick-witted, world-weary George Valentine became the template for a thousand TV detectives that would follow in the coming decade, and episodes like "How Guilty Can You Get" showcase exactly why the show earned its devoted following across America's airwaves. The writers understood that modern criminology wasn't just about the facts—it was about psychology, motive, and the dangerous spaces where desperation meets opportunity. Broadcast during the post-war years when America was grappling with its own complicated questions about guilt, innocence, and justice, the show resonated with listeners seeking sharp storytelling and moral complexity.

Tune in and discover why George Valentine became the private eye that listeners trusted to cut through the lies. This is detective work at its finest—where every clue matters and the wrong conclusion could destroy an innocent person.
