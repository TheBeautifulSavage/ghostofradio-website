# Let George Do It: The Eight Ball (April 2, 1951)

George Valentine finds himself tangled in the dangerous underworld of underground gambling when a desperate woman walks into his office with blood on her hands and a story that doesn't quite add up. She claims self-defense, but the corpse of a notorious pool shark and loan shark named "Eight Ball" Eddie Corso lying in a downtown hotel room tells a different tale entirely. As George peels back the layers of this seemingly simple crime, he discovers a web of blackmail, fixed games, and a criminal syndicate willing to kill to protect their secrets. The clock ticks relentlessly as shadowy figures close in, and George must navigate dimly lit back rooms and dangerous interrogations to uncover the truth before the real murderer pins the crime on his vulnerable client—or silences George himself.

By 1951, *Let George Do It* had become a cornerstone of detective radio drama, with Bob Bailey's portrayal of the quick-thinking, wiseccracking private investigator captivating audiences across the Mutual network. The show's success lay in its perfect balance of hard-boiled noir atmosphere and genuine humanity; George was no invincible superman, but a resourceful everyman constantly improvising his way out of impossible situations. "The Eight Ball" exemplifies the show at its finest, delivering the snappy dialogue and intricate plotting that kept listeners glued to their sets for six years.

If you've never experienced the golden age of radio detective work, this episode is an ideal entry point. Settle back, dim the lights, and let Bailey's voice transport you to a world of neon signs, suspicious alibis, and moral ambiguity—where a private investigator's quick wits are his only reliable weapon.
