# Let George Do It: War Maneuver

Picture yourself huddled near your radio on a spring evening in 1952, the warm glow of the tubes casting dancing shadows across your living room. This week, private eye George Valentine finds himself entangled in a dangerous game of military intrigue when a mysterious officer arrives at his office with a proposition that could cost him everything. "War Maneuver" plunges listeners into a shadowy world where real war games become something far more sinister, where a simple job turns into a labyrinth of double-crosses and hidden agendas. The crackle of the AM band carries you into smoky interrogation rooms and midnight rendezvous on rain-slicked streets, where every shadow could conceal an enemy and every handshake might be your last. Bob Bailey's distinctive deadpan delivery cuts through the noir darkness like a knife, while the sound effects team conjures the clang of military barracks and the ominous silence of classified operations.

For six years, *Let George Do It* had established itself as one of radio's most reliable purveyors of hard-boiled detective fiction, sitting comfortably in the Mutual network's evening lineup and delivering exactly what post-war audiences craved—the reassurance of a street-smart private investigator who could navigate the complex moral landscape of the atomic age. By 1952, with the Korean War dominating headlines and Cold War anxieties running high, episodes like "War Maneuver" resonated with listeners who understood that danger wasn't just a pulp magazine fiction anymore. Bailey's George Valentine had become an American institution, a working-class hero you could trust when the world seemed increasingly untrustworthy.

Don't miss this thrilling chapter in George Valentine's casebook. Tune in and discover why *Let George Do It* remained appointment listening for millions of Americans who understood that sometimes, you need a man like George to cut through the lies and find the truth.
