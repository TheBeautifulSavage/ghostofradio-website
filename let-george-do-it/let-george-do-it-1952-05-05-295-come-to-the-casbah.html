# Let George Do It: Come To The Casbah

When George Valentine answers that fateful phone call on a sweltering May evening in 1952, he has no idea he's about to be drawn into the shadowy underworld of North African intrigue. *Come To The Casbah* plunges listeners straight into the exotic danger of Tangier's infamous district—where smugglers deal in secrets as freely as they do in contraband, and a dame's misdirected letter of introduction could cost a man his life. Bob Bailey's distinctive, weary baritone carries us through dimly-lit cafés thick with cigarette smoke and suspicion, past veiled figures in narrow alleyways where trust is a luxury nobody can afford. The orchestra punctuates every moment with its sharp, unsettling strings, while sound effects place us right there beside George as he navigates a web of double-crosses and dangerous alliances. By the episode's conclusion, listeners will be gripping their radio sets, wondering whether our hero will escape the Casbah alive—or if this assignment will finally be the one that proves impossible, even for George.

*Let George Do It* had become, by 1952, one of radio's most reliable sources of intelligent detective fiction. Unlike the sanctimonious law-and-order drama of so many contemporaries, the show reveled in moral ambiguity and existential dread. Bailey's George wasn't a Boy Scout—he was a pragmatist, a man who solved problems because someone would pay him to do it, consequences be damned. Over six years on the Mutual network, the series had earned a devoted following among listeners who craved their noir with psychological depth and exotic locales.

Don't miss this thrilling journey into the shadows of the Casbah. Tune in and discover why *Let George Do It* remains the thinking listener's detective drama.
