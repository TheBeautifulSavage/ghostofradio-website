# Let George Do It - September 29, 1952

On a fog-thick evening in 1952, George Valentine finds himself tangled in a web of mathematical precision and human desperation. When a small-time gambling operation collapses under suspicious circumstances, George is hired to investigate—but every clue seems to defy rational explanation. The numbers don't add up. The witnesses contradict themselves. And somewhere in the smoky underbelly of the city, someone is playing a dangerous game with probability itself. As George peels back layers of deception, the line between chance and conspiracy grows razor-thin. You'll hear the creak of a leather office chair, the sharp click of a lighter igniting in the darkness, and the measured cadence of Bob Bailey's voice cutting through the static—a man determined to understand the unknowable. This isn't a straightforward case of theft or betrayal; it's a puzzle where every answer breeds three new questions.

*Let George Do It* stands as one of radio's most enduring detective series, running strong through eight seasons on the Mutual network when this episode aired. Unlike the high-profile glamour of *The Shadow* or the procedural authority of *Dragnet*, George Valentine inhabited the grittier margins of the mystery world—a working private investigator for everyday people with everyday nightmares. Bob Bailey's naturalistic delivery and the show's refusal to soften its noir edges made it a favorite among listeners seeking authenticity over spectacle. The September 1952 episode "Chance and Probability" exemplifies the show's sophisticated approach to storytelling, weaving philosophical questions into a tight plot.

Tune in to hear George navigate the murky intersection of luck and design, where a single wrong move could mean the difference between solving the case and joining the victims. This is detective work as it really was—messy, uncertain, and always one step away from danger.
