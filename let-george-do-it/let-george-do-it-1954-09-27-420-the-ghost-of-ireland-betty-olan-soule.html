# Let George Do It – "The Ghost of Ireland Betty"

When private investigator George Valentine answers a late-night telephone call from a panicked woman's voice, he finds himself drawn into one of the season's most chilling cases. A specter from the past—literally—has returned to haunt the streets of the city, and innocent lives hang in the balance. As our hero ventures into fog-shrouded alleyways and dimly lit speakeasies, the line between the supernatural and the sinister blurs. Is Ireland Betty truly a ghost seeking vengeance, or is something far more sinister at work? Bob Bailey's signature smooth baritone cuts through the static and shadow, guiding listeners deeper into a mystery where murder and mystery intertwine with the inexplicable. The tension builds with every footstep, every cryptic clue, and every startling revelation that forces George to confront forces beyond his usual experience. This is detective noir at its finest—gritty, atmospheric, and utterly gripping from opening bell to final resolution.

By 1954, *Let George Do It* had become the Mutual Network's crown jewel of the detective genre, captivating millions of listeners for nearly a decade. The show's remarkable longevity stemmed from its perfect formula: Bailey's charismatic performance, sharp writing that balanced hard-boiled wisecracks with genuine menace, and stories that never talked down to their audience. Each week, George Valentine proved that radio could deliver the same compelling noir atmosphere as the silver screen, without losing the intimate immediacy that made people huddle around their sets in rapt attention.
