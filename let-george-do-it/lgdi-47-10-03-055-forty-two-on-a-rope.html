# Forty Two On A Rope

When George Valentine answers that fateful phone call on a rain-soaked October evening, he has no idea that a simple favor will plunge him into a maze of blackmail, betrayal, and murder most cunningly concealed. *Forty Two On A Rope* opens with the kind of atmospheric menace that made *Let George Do It* appointment listening for millions: the distant wail of a siren, the crackle of a dying cigarette, and Bob Bailey's world-weary drawl setting the scene for a case where the evidence literally hangs in the balance. George finds himself hunting a killer among Chicago's underworld, where forty-two knots in a rope become the cryptic calling card of a desperate criminal. As the investigation tightens, listeners are pulled deeper into a labyrinth of false leads and genuine danger, where every conversation crackles with the electricity of hidden motives and the ever-present threat of violence.

*Let George Do It* became one of radio's most enduring detective dramas precisely because it avoided the theatrical bombast of its contemporaries. Rather than relying on supernatural gimmickry or contrived plot twists, the show grounded its mysteries in the gritty, authentic world of private investigation—a world where luck and quick thinking mattered as much as deduction. Bob Bailey's George Valentine wasn't a brilliant eccentric but an ordinary man consistently thrust into extraordinary circumstances, making him instantly relatable to Depression and war-weary audiences seeking escapism through genuine human drama.

Slip on your fedora and dim the lights—*Forty Two On A Rope* awaits you with all the tension, intrigue, and hard-boiled atmosphere that made this series a beloved classic of the golden age. Tune in and discover why listeners kept coming back to George, case after case, for nearly a decade.
