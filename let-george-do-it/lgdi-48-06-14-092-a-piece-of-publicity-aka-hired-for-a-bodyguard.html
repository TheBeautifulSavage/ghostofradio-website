# A Piece of Publicity

When a desperate Hollywood starlet walks into George Valentine's office with a wad of cash and a sinister shadow at her heels, our unflappable private detective knows he's stumbled into the kind of case that makes headlines—or makes men disappear. In "A Piece of Publicity," the airwaves crackle with the tension of blackmail, mistaken identity, and the cutthroat world of show business where reputations are currency and lies are the house rules. George finds himself hired as a bodyguard for reasons far murkier than protection, maneuvering through the glittering, dangerous underbelly of 1940s Hollywood where every starlet has a secret and every secret has a price. What begins as a simple job spirals into a twisted web of deception that will test George's quick wit and quicker reflexes—because in this town, one wrong move and you're yesterday's news.

*Let George Do It* became a staple of Mutual Broadcasting's detective lineup precisely because it understood the postwar American appetite for smart, snappy crime drama delivered with movie-star charisma. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine struck the perfect balance between hard-boiled cynicism and genuine humanity, making him feel less like a two-dimensional gumshoe and more like the fellow next door who just happened to solve murders for a living. The show's writers crafted episodes that crackled with authentic period detail, whether George was navigating the Hollywood system or unraveling small-town mysteries—each episode a compact thriller that proved radio could deliver cinematic excitement through voice and sound alone.

Settle in with a cup of coffee and prepare for an evening of first-rate detective work. *Let George Do It* awaits, and George Valentine is ready to take your case.
