# A Minor Case Of Murder

When a desperate mother arrives at George Valentine's office clutching a bloodstained handkerchief, what begins as a routine favor spirals into a labyrinth of blackmail, betrayal, and a corpse that nobody wants to claim. In this taut episode of *Let George Do It*, our quick-witted private detective must navigate the murky underworld of 1940s Minneapolis where innocence is a luxury and silence is bought with blood money. As George peels back layer after layer of misdirection, the stakes grow dangerously personal—and a "minor" case threatens to consume him entirely. Expect the sharp repartee that made this series a favorite, punctuated by the distant wail of sirens and the ominous crackle of noir atmosphere that crackles through your loudspeaker like a live cigarette.

*Let George Do It* arrived during radio's golden age, capturing the postwar appetite for smart, cynical entertainment that reflected America's complex moral landscape. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine became iconic precisely because he embodied a new kind of detective—not the superhuman acrobat of earlier serials, but a shrewd, street-level operator who relied on brains and improvisation rather than bullets. The Mutual network's commitment to locally-produced content meant these episodes carried authentic regional flavor, with Minneapolis serving as a fully realized character rather than mere backdrop. This particular installment showcases the series at its finest: tight writing, genuine suspense, and that ineffable quality that made listeners return faithfully, week after week.

Slip on your headphones and step into the rain-slicked streets of a city where nothing is ever quite what it seems. George Valentine is waiting—and he's got another case that demands solving.
