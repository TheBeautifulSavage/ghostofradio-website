On a rain-slicked evening in September 1948, George Valentine finds himself entangled with a woman as dangerous as she is beautiful—a redhead with a proposition and a problem that could get them both killed. When she walks into his office with murder already in her past and desperation burning in her eyes, George knows he's stepped into the kind of case that keeps a man awake at night, looking over his shoulder. The mystery deepens with every cigarette smoked and every wrong turn down a darkened alley. Someone wants her dead, or worse—wants to frame her for a crime she didn't commit. As the hours tick away with mounting tension, George must unravel a web of lies, blackmail, and hidden secrets before the impatient redhead's past catches up with them both.

Let George Do It thrived during radio's golden age by delivering exactly what audiences craved: a cynical, capable detective who asked the right questions and wasn't afraid of the answers. Running from 1946 to 1954 on the Mutual Broadcasting System, the show became a cornerstone of the detective noir genre on radio, with Bob Bailey's world-weary portrayal of George Valentine setting the standard for the everyman gumshoe. These episodes captured postwar America's fascination with moral ambiguity and urban danger, offering listeners a weekly escape into a world of shadows and hard choices.
