Picture the neon-slicked streets of a rain-soaked city, where shadows pool in doorways and a mysterious figure seems to turn up at every crime scene—always one step ahead of Detective George Valentine. In this September 1948 episode, our intrepid protagonist finds himself chasing a phantom, a diminutive man whose uncanny ability to appear in impossible places suggests something far more sinister than coincidence. As clues pile up and witnesses speak in hushed, frightened tones, George must untangle whether he's hunting a criminal mastermind or something altogether more disturbing. The tension crackles through every commercial break, building toward a conclusion that will leave you questioning what you heard long after the final fadeout.

Let George Do It thrived on the kind of smart, economical storytelling that defined the golden age of radio noir. With Bob Bailey's world-weary narration anchoring each episode, the show became a stalwart of the Mutual network throughout the late 1940s and early 1950s, proving that detective fiction didn't need big budgets or recognizable names to captivate millions of listeners hunched around their radios. This particular episode exemplifies the show's genius for turning everyday settings into labyrinths of suspicion, where the smallest details become crucial clues and paranoia feels entirely reasonable.

The beauty of Let George Do It lies in its intimate scale—you're never watching from outside but riding directly behind George's eyes, hearing his thoughts, sharing his confusion and growing dread. "The Little Man Who Was Everywhere" is quintessential Golden Age radio: clever, atmospheric, and utterly gripping. Tune in for thirty minutes of pure detective noir magic.
