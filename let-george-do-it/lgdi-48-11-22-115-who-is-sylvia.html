# Let George Do It: "Who Is Sylvia"

When George Valentine answers the phone on a rain-slicked November evening, he doesn't expect the breathless voice of a woman he's never met—a woman who claims her life depends on his discretion and his gun. *Who Is Sylvia* plunges listeners into the murky underbelly of postwar anonymity, where a simple question becomes a dangerous obsession. The case unfolds through fog-thick dialogue and the sharp crack of danger around every corner, as George navigates shadowy hotel rooms, double-crossing informants, and a mystery that peels back layer after layer of deception. By episode's end, you'll never be quite sure who's telling the truth—or if anyone survives to tell it at all.

For nearly a decade, *Let George Do It* defined the golden age of detective radio drama for millions of listeners huddled around their sets. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine became the gold standard for the hard-boiled private investigator—world-weary but incorruptible, quick with his fists and quicker with his wit. Broadcasting on the Mutual Network from 1946 to 1954, the show captured the fractured morality and cynical sophistication of the emerging Cold War era, when trust itself had become a luxury nobody could afford. Each episode was a self-contained noir fever dream, recorded with obsessive attention to sound design: the hiss of traffic, the whisper of turning pages, the ominous silence before violence.

If you've never experienced the genuine article of American radio noir, *Who Is Sylvia* is the perfect entry point—a masterclass in suspense told through voice alone. Dim the lights, pour yourself something strong, and let George do what he does best. This is radio as it was meant to be heard.
