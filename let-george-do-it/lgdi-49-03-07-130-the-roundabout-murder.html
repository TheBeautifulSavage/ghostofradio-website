# The Roundabout Murder

George Valentine steps into a fog-thick night where nothing is quite what it seems. A society dame walks into his office with trembling hands and a story that circles back on itself like a serpent eating its tail—a murder that couldn't have happened, yet somehow did. As George peels back layer after layer of alibis, secret affairs, and carefully constructed lies, the tension mounts with each commercial break. The victim lies cold, the suspects circle like vultures, and our protagonist must navigate a labyrinth of deception where every answer spawns three new questions. This is classic detective radio at its finest: intimate, claustrophobic, and absolutely gripping.

*Let George Do It* arrived on the Mutual Network in 1946 as radio's golden age was beginning its slow fade toward television. Yet this series captured the essence of what made detective noir so intoxicating to post-war audiences—the gritty realism, the cynical wit, and the relentless moral ambiguity of a world turned upside down. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine became iconic, a weary but incorruptible private eye prowling a America that felt more authentic than the clean-cut heroes dominating other networks. With over 200 episodes broadcast, the show earned devoted listeners who tuned in religiously to follow Valentine through the criminal underworld.

"The Roundabout Murder" exemplifies why the series remained a staple for eight years. The title itself promises complexity—not a straightforward whodunit, but a puzzle wrapped inside a mystery, a case that demands your full attention. Settle in with the lights dimmed low, let the orchestral opening wash over you, and prepare yourself for an evening of stellar character work, razor-sharp dialogue, and the kind of storytelling that made radio the dominant entertainment medium of its era. George is ready—are you?
