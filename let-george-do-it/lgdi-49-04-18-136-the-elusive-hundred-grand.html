# The Elusive Hundred Grand

When George Valentine answers that fateful phone call in the rain-soaked streets of the city, he thinks it's just another job—another chance to earn his usual fee. But a missing hundred grand, a desperate widow, and a trail of dead ends pull our intrepid detective deeper into a web of double-crosses and dangerous secrets than he bargained for. As cigarette smoke curls through dimly lit offices and footsteps echo ominously down darkened alleys, George finds himself racing against time, his sharp wits and sharper instincts his only weapons. This is the stuff of classic noir—the kind of case that separates the brilliant detectives from the desperate ones, where every suspect has motive, opportunity, and a reason to lie. The tension builds relentlessly as George narrows his search, peeling back layers of deception until the final, shocking revelation that will leave listeners stunned.

*Let George Do It* defined an era of detective radio drama during its nine-year run on the Mutual Broadcasting System. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine became iconic—a voice that audiences trusted implicitly, even as the character himself questioned everyone around him. Unlike the overly theatrical detectives of earlier radio shows, George was grounded, cynical, and utterly human; his cases were intricate puzzles that demanded intelligence rather than just bravado. This 1949 episode exemplifies why the show earned devoted listeners across America, blending sophisticated storytelling with the atmospheric magic that only radio could deliver.

Tune in now to *The Elusive Hundred Grand* and discover why George Valentine's name became synonymous with detective excellence. Feel the crackle of authentic 1940s atmosphere, the snap of clever dialogue, and the unmistakable thrill of a mystery unfolding exactly as it should.
