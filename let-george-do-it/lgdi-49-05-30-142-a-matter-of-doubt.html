# Let George Do It: A Matter Of Doubt

When George Valentine answers that fateful phone call on a rain-slicked evening in May 1949, he finds himself entangled in a case that hinges on the most dangerous weapon of all—uncertainty. A prominent businessman's alibi is unraveling like wet thread, and George must navigate a labyrinth of conflicting testimonies and hidden motives to uncover the truth. As organ music swells and the city's neon-soaked streets come alive with menace, listeners will find themselves drawn into an atmosphere thick with suspicion and moral ambiguity. Is the accused truly guilty, or is he simply a convenient scapegoat for those with far more to lose? The answer lurks somewhere in the shadows between what can be proven and what feels true.

"Let George Do It" represented radio's golden age at its finest—a show that understood that the real mysteries of human nature couldn't be solved with a simple deduction but required genuine empathy and moral courage. Created by Herbert Moore and starring Bob Bailey as the ever-capable George Valentine, the series thrived on exploring the gray areas of justice and guilt. By 1949, the show had honed its craft into something remarkable: detective noir that felt both intimate and dangerous, broadcast directly into millions of American homes. Each episode balanced procedural elements with the psychological complexity that would later define television's greatest dramas.

If you've never experienced the particular thrill of "A Matter Of Doubt," now is your chance to discover why this show remained a listener favorite throughout its nine-year run. Settle into your favorite chair, dim the lights, and let George Valentine's world pull you in—because on radio, the images conjured in your own mind were always the most vivid of all.
