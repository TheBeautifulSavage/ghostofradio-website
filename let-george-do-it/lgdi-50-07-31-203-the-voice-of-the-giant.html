# The Voice of the Giant

When George Valentine answers the telephone on a rain-soaked evening in Chicago, he expects another routine case—instead, he finds himself thrust into a web of blackmail, murder, and a conspiracy that reaches from the shadowy speakeasies of the Loop into the highest echelons of the city's underworld. A mysterious voice on the line—deep, distorted, and utterly menacing—belongs to someone calling himself "The Giant," and this unseen adversary has already claimed victims. As George prowls through fog-shrouded streets and down dimly lit corridors, the pieces of an impossible puzzle begin to form. The stakes escalate with each revelation, and our quick-witted detective must stay one step ahead of a killer who operates from the shadows, commanding an army of terrified accomplices through that disembodied voice alone. It's vintage noir storytelling: gritty, suspenseful, and anchored in the real anxieties of post-war America.

*Let George Do It* emerged from the golden age of radio detective fiction, when shows like this commanded millions of listeners eager to escape into stories of moral ambiguity and urban danger. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine became iconic throughout the 1940s and early 1950s—a smart-aleck investigator with a conscience, working in a world where right and wrong blurred together like city lights through rain. The show's success lay in its commitment to genuine mystery plotting and atmospheric sound design, where every creaking door and distant siren pulled listeners deeper into the narrative.

If you cherish classic detective radio—the kind where danger feels real and solutions demand real detective work—*The Voice of the Giant* is essential listening. Tune in and discover why George Valentine became one of radio's most beloved investigators, and why this particular case still captivates audiences more than seventy years later.
