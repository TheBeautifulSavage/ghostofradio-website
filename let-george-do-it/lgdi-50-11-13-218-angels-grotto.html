# Let George Do It: Angel's Grotto

Picture this: a rain-slicked San Francisco street corner at midnight, where the neon signs bleed their colors into puddles and a desperate woman's voice crackles through George Valentine's telephone line with an urgency that makes your spine straighten. In "Angel's Grotto," our unflappable private detective finds himself entangled in a case that begins with a simple phone call and spirals into the shadowy underbelly of the city's underground gambling dens and protection rackets. What starts as a routine missing persons inquiry becomes a dangerous game of cat and mouse, where every lead pulls George deeper into a web of corruption, blackmail, and murder. The episode crackles with authentic noir atmosphere—you can almost hear the clink of whiskey glasses, the slap of wet footsteps on pavement, and the menacing undertone of men who've traded their consciences for cash.

*Let George Do It* stands as one of radio's most enduring detective programs, thriving throughout the late 1940s with Bob Bailey's perfectly calibrated performance as George Valentine—a detective who's quick-witted but never invincible, smart-mouthed but ultimately decent. The show's genius lay in its formula: Valentine wasn't a superhero solving impossibly complex crimes through deduction alone; he was a working man's private eye who stumbled onto cases through chance phone calls, then had to use his wits and fists to survive them. By 1950, when "Angel's Grotto" aired, the show had perfected its craft, delivering tightly plotted mysteries with supporting casts of memorable characters and dialogue that snapped with period authenticity.

Don't miss this opportunity to step into the shadowy world of post-war San Francisco. Tune in to *Let George Do It* and discover why audiences tuned in faithfully for nearly a decade to follow George Valentine into the city's darkest corners.
