# The Stand In

On a rain-slicked November night in 1952, George Valentine finds himself tangled in the shadowy world of theatrical deception where nothing—and no one—is quite what they seem. When a desperate actress hires the quick-witted private investigator to uncover the truth behind a mysterious stand-in, George discovers that the stage lights hide far more sinister secrets than mere costume changes. What begins as a simple case of mistaken identity spirals into a labyrinth of blackmail, jealousy, and murder, where every player has a motive and every curtain call conceals a lie. Listen as George's sharp tongue and sharper instincts navigate the treacherous backstage corridors where a stand-in's double life becomes someone's death sentence. The tension crackles through every scene—you can almost hear the cold November wind whistling through the theater's alleyways as George closes in on a killer who's learned too well how to play a part.

*Let George Do It* thrived during radio's golden age as one of the medium's most reliable sources of smart, snappy detective fiction. Bob Bailey's portrayal of George Valentine captured the essence of the postwar American gumshoe—charming enough to talk his way into danger, resourceful enough to talk his way back out. The show's writers crafted scripts that balanced genuine mystery with rapid-fire dialogue and the kind of atmospheric noir that made listeners lean closer to their sets. By 1952, when "The Stand In" aired, the show had perfected its formula: authentic detective work wrapped in authentic entertainment.

If you've never experienced the particular thrill of a George Valentine case, here's your invitation to step into the shadows. Settle in, dim the lights, and let George handle it—you're in for a mystery that will keep you guessing until the final reveal.
