By the 1940s, The Lone Ranger had become a cultural institution, proving that radio drama could explore questions of conscience alongside action and adventure. What separated the show from countless other westerns was this very quality—the recognition that a hero's greatest battles were often internal ones. Creator George W. Trendle and his talented stable of writers crafted episodes that appealed equally to children and adults, offering adventure for the former and moral reflection for the latter. The Lone Ranger represented an idealized American figure: the lone guardian of justice in a lawless land, yet constrained always by his own code of honor.
