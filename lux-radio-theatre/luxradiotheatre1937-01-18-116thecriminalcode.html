The air is thick with tension as Richard Nixon, playing the role of District Attorney James McNeil, confronts the notorious underworld figure, Joe "The Bull" Moran (played by John Ridgely). The year is 1937, and the world teeters on the brink of chaos. Economic depression has left Americans struggling to make ends meet, while abroad, the fascist regimes of Europe loom large, casting a dark shadow over the globe. As McNeil delves deeper into Moran's illicit activities, he must navigate the treacherous waters of organized crime, corruption, and deceit. The stakes are high, and the consequences of failure are dire.

As the drama unfolds, Nixon masterfully weaves a web of intrigue, drawing the listener into the world of Moran's empire. The tension builds as McNeil inches closer to unraveling the tangled threads of the criminal code. The Lux Radio Theatre's orchestra provides a haunting accompaniment, underscoring the sense of foreboding that permeates every scene.

The dramatic tension is palpable as McNeil and his team close in on Moran, culminating in a heart-stopping showdown between good and evil. Will justice prevail, or will the forces of darkness prevail? The Lux Radio Theatre's 1937 production of "The Criminal Code" is a gripping tale of intrigue, deception, and the unyielding pursuit of truth.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

As 1937 dawned, America was mired in the depths of the Great Depression. Unemployment rates soared, and the once-mighty economy teetered on the brink of collapse. The world beyond America's borders was similarly fraught with uncertainty. Nazi Germany, under Adolf Hitler, continued its aggressive expansion, while fascist Italy and Imperial Japan solidified their grip on Europe and Asia. In this climate of fear and upheaval, radio drama proved to be a beacon of escapism, offering audiences a respite from the troubles of everyday life.

Radio's popularity as a form of entertainment was at an all-time high, with millions of Americans tuning in daily to escape into worlds of fantasy and adventure. The Lux Radio Theatre, under the direction of George W. Stevens, capitalized on this trend, delivering high-quality productions that captivated listeners across the nation. The show's ability to evoke emotions, spark imaginations, and offer moral lessons resonated deeply with audiences desperate for entertainment during these trying times.

The timing of "The Criminal Code" could not have been more propitious. As Americans turned to radio for comfort, this Lux Radio Theatre production offered a thrilling narrative that tapped into the collective anxieties of its listeners. By exploring themes of corruption, deception, and justice, the show spoke directly to the concerns of an audience torn between hope and despair.
