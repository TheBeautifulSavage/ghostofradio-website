The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

"Under Two Flags," a Lux Radio Theatre production from May 24, 1937, whisks listeners away to the tumultuous world of international intrigue. The drama unfolds as James Stewart stars as John "Jack" McCadden, an American journalist who becomes embroiled in a complex web of espionage and betrayal while working for a Parisian newspaper. Jack's investigation leads him to uncover a sinister plot by his own government to overthrow the French government, all under the guise of supporting democracy. As tensions escalate, Jack finds himself torn between his loyalty to his country and his growing feelings for Colette, a beautiful and enigmatic Frenchwoman with secrets of her own.

The episode's atmosphere is tense and foreboding, evoking the dark shadows of war-torn Europe in the aftermath of World War I. The Lux Radio Theatre's signature sound design creates an immersive experience, transporting listeners to the streets of Paris as Jack navigates the treacherous landscape of international politics. Stewart's performance brings depth and nuance to his character, capturing the moral complexity of a man caught between two worlds.

As the story hurtles toward its shocking conclusion, "Under Two Flags" poses uncomfortable questions about loyalty, duty, and the nature of truth in a world where allegiances are easily bought and sold. The episode's themes resonate with listeners who, like Jack, find themselves navigating uncertain times, grappling with conflicting loyalties to country, family, and ideology.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

In 1937, America was in the midst of an increasingly globalized world. The Great Depression had left its mark on the nation, but international tensions were rising as fascist regimes began to gain traction across Europe and Asia. Radio dramas like "Under Two Flags" tapped into these anxieties, offering listeners a thrilling narrative that explored the darker corners of geopolitics. The episode's portrayal of espionage and betrayal resonated with audiences who had lived through the horrors of World War I, and were now faced with the prospect of another global conflict.

The French government was itself a focal point of international attention in 1937, as President Édouard Daladier struggled to maintain stability in the face of Nazi aggression. Radio dramas like "Under Two Flags" provided an outlet for listeners to engage with these complex issues from the safety of their living rooms, allowing them to explore the gray areas between patriotism and treason.

The allure of radio drama lay in its ability to convey the complexities of international politics through a narrative that was both entertaining and thought-provoking. Listeners tuned in each week to escape into a world of adventure and intrigue, while also engaging with pressing issues of the day. "Under Two Flags" exemplifies this unique blend of escapism and social commentary, offering listeners a captivating tale of espionage and betrayal that continues to resonate today.

WHY IT MATTERS:

As a cultural artifact, "Under Two Flags" represents a pivotal moment in the development of Lux Radio Theatre's distinctive sound. The episode's use of atmospheric sound design and Stewart's nuanced performance helped establish the Lux brand as a premier destination for adult entertainment on radio. This episode stands as a testament to the studio's innovative approach to storytelling, which combined cutting-edge production techniques with compelling narratives that explored the complexities of human experience.

Today, "Under Two Flags" remains an important work in the genre of radio drama, offering listeners a captivating tale of espionage and betrayal that continues to resonate with contemporary audiences. As we navigate our own era of global uncertainty, this episode serves as a powerful reminder of the enduring power of storytelling to engage, educate, and challenge us – all while transporting us to a bygone era of intrigue and adventure.
