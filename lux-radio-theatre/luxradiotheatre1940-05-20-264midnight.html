The Episode

EPISODE SUMMARY:

"Midnight" is a Lux Radio Theatre episode from May 20, 1940, featuring the talents of William Holden as David Morton and Mary Astor as Mrs. Morton. The story revolves around a mysterious woman who arrives at the Mortons' home on a stormy night, claiming to be the daughter of an aristocratic family. As the night wears on, David begins to suspect that she is not what she seems, and tensions rise between the two men. Mary Astor brings her signature sultry tone to her portrayal of Mrs. Morton, while William Holden delivers a nuanced performance as the increasingly skeptical husband.

The atmosphere of this episode is tense and foreboding, perfectly capturing the sense of unease that pervaded American society in the early 1940s. The sound design, courtesy of the Lux Radio Theatre team, effectively utilizes the eerie creaking of wooden floorboards and the howling wind to create an immersive experience for listeners. As the story builds towards its climax, the music swells, adding to the sense of drama and urgency.

At its core, "Midnight" is a classic tale of obsession, class, and deception – themes that resonated deeply with audiences in the 1940s. The episode's exploration of these complex ideas was both thought-provoking and entertaining, making it a staple of the Lux Radio Theatre format.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

As America teetered on the brink of war in Europe, the world seemed to be holding its collective breath. The invasion of Poland by Nazi Germany in 1939 had sent shockwaves across the globe, and tensions between the United States and Japan were escalating over the Pacific. In the midst of this chaos, radio drama became an increasingly popular form of entertainment – a way for people to escape into a world of fantasy and suspense.

The Lux Radio Theatre was at the forefront of this trend, offering listeners a unique blend of drama, mystery, and thrills that captivated audiences nightly. With shows like "Midnight", Lux Radio Theatre tapped into the deep-seated fears and anxieties of the American public – fears about war, class, and social status – to create an atmosphere of tension and suspense that was both thrilling and unsettling.

By offering a refuge from the uncertainties of everyday life, Lux Radio Theatre provided its listeners with a safe space to indulge in their darker impulses. In this way, the show spoke to the desires and fears of its audience – a desire for escapism, but also a fascination with the darker aspects of human nature.

One of the key factors that makes "Midnight" worth hearing today is its masterful use of sound design and music. The Lux Radio Theatre team was renowned for their innovative approach to sonic storytelling, and this episode is no exception. The way in which the creaking floorboards and howling wind are used to create an immersive atmosphere sets a tone that still holds up today – it's a testament to the enduring power of radio as a medium.
