In the summer of 1940, America was on edge, grappling with the aftermath of the Great Depression and the escalating tensions leading up to World War II. The Prohibition era, which had begun in 1919, was still in full swing, but its popularity was waning as attitudes towards liquor began to shift. Meanwhile, organized crime syndicates were growing bolder, exerting their influence over cities across the country.

As the United States prepared for war, the country's leaders faced a pressing concern: how to mobilize the nation and prepare for battle without losing its sense of individual freedom. The Lux Radio Theatre episode captures some of this anxiety, as characters struggle with the moral implications of participating in organized crime and the consequences of violence on their personal lives.

Despite these tensions, radio drama proved an enduring source of entertainment and escapism for Americans during this period. With the advent of electricity and mechanical recording technology, radio had become a popular medium, offering a shared experience that was both intimate and communal. As the world teetered on the brink of war, Lux Radio Theatre's dramas like "Alexanders Ragtime Band" provided an essential respite from the anxieties of everyday life.
