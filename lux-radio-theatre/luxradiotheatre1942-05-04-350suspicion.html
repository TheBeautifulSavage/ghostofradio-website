# Suspicion – May 4, 1942

Step into the drawing rooms of old money and privilege as Lux Radio Theatre presents Alfred Hitchcock's *Suspicion*, a masterwork of psychological torment that crackles with mounting dread through your radio speaker. Our protagonist, a woman of means, finds herself increasingly convinced that her charming, debonair husband harbors sinister intentions—but is her growing paranoia a reflection of his true nature, or merely the poison of her own anxieties? As the drama unfolds across a taut fifty-five minutes, listeners will experience that delicious Lux quality: stellar Hollywood performances, meticulous sound design, and the kind of intimate psychological terror that radio does better than any visual medium. Every pause between lines of dialogue becomes weighted with menace; every sound effect—a footstep on the stair, the clink of a glass—carries unbearable significance.

By 1942, Lux Radio Theatre had become America's premier dramatic program, commanding audiences of millions from coast to coast. The show's genius lay in its ambitious formula: presenting film classics and original dramas with top-tier Hollywood talent, proving that sophisticated entertainment could thrive on the airwaves. Hitchcock's influence on American culture was ascending rapidly, and this adaptation captures perfectly the master of suspense's obsession with ordinary people trapped in extraordinary circumstances, with the home itself becoming a claustrophobic stage for psychological warfare.

If you've never experienced the particular power of *Suspicion* through radio's intimate medium, this 1942 broadcast offers the perfect opportunity. Tune in and discover why families gathered around their sets for this broadcast, why critics praised its subtle performance work, and why Lux Radio Theatre remains the gold standard of dramatic radio programming.
