Picture yourself settling into an overstuffed armchair on a crisp September evening, the amber glow of your radio's dial warming the darkened room as the familiar strains of Lux Radio Theatre's theme swell through the speaker. Tonight, you're transported to the verdant valleys of Wales, where the Morgans—a proud mining family—navigate the treacherous intersection of labor strife, romantic passion, and generational conflict. This lavish production brings Richard Llewellyn's beloved novel to vivid life through the carefully modulated voices of accomplished stage and screen actors, their performances supported by a live orchestra and authentic sound effects that crackle with coal dust and yearning. As the drama unfolds, you'll hear the clash of pickaxes in the mines, the hushed intimacy of a forbidden courtship, and the weathered wisdom of a father who has given everything to the earth beneath his feet—all compressed into a taut fifty-eight minutes that will have you holding your breath.

This 1942 broadcast arrives at a poignant moment in American history, when the nation's own working men and women are sacrificing in ways both familiar and unprecedented. The class struggles and moral complexities of the Welsh mining community resonated powerfully with stateside audiences who themselves were experiencing labor's crucial role in wartime production. Lux Radio Theatre, sponsored by a luxury soap manufacturer yet commanding a devoted audience across every social class, had become America's most prestigious dramatic showcase—a weekly gift of Hollywood-caliber entertainment piped free into living rooms across the nation.

Don't miss this extraordinary evening of radio drama. Tune in and discover why millions of Americans abandoned their Sunday evening routines to experience these theatrical masterpieces, performed live, only once, never to be exactly repeated again.
