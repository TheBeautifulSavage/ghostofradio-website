Lux Radio Theatre CBS/NBC · March 13, 1944

On a crisp spring evening in 1944, millions of Americans tuned their dials to CBS for an evening of pure theatrical magic. Lux Radio Theatre presented a stunning adaptation of Oklahoma!, the revolutionary Rodgers and Hammerstein musical that had just captivated Broadway audiences the previous year. Listeners were transported to the windswept plains of Indian Territory, where the spirited Laurey and the determined Curly's romance unfolds against a backdrop of frontier ambition and genuine peril. The sound effects crackled with authenticity—the thunder of horse hooves across dusty trails, the creak of wagons, the ominous tension of a social gathering that threatens to tear a community apart. For ninety electrifying minutes, the intimate medium of radio became a window into wide-open spaces, proving that the most vivid landscapes are those painted by voice, music, and imagination.

This particular broadcast arrived at a remarkable cultural moment. While American troops fought across distant continents, Oklahoma! represented something profoundly hopeful: a distinctly American story of optimism, labor, and the promise of new frontiers. Lux Radio Theatre had already been America's premier dramatic showcase for a full decade, bringing Broadway's greatest hits and Hollywood's brightest stars directly into living rooms across the nation. The show's legendary host would introduce the evening with his characteristic warmth, while an orchestra brought Hammerstein's poetry and Rodgers' soaring melodies to life with full dramatic force—something the radio format could achieve with singular power.

If you've never experienced the enchantment of Lux Radio Theatre, this March 1944 broadcast of Oklahoma! is the perfect invitation. Settle into a comfortable chair, dim the lights, and let the golden age of radio transport you to a time when America gathered around the speaker to dream together.
