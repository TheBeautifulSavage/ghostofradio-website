Picture yourself settling into a worn armchair on a warm June evening in 1940, the amber glow of your radio dial beckoning as the familiar theme music swells through your living room. Tonight's broadcast of Mr. District Attorney plunges you into the gritty underworld of the big city, where a dangerous criminal known only as "The Ohio Kid" has evaded justice long enough. This is no ordinary case—the Kid's ruthlessness has left a trail of victims in his wake, and our determined District Attorney must outthink a cunning adversary who operates in the shadows between the law and the streets. Listen as witnesses are tracked down, evidence is carefully assembled, and the tension mounts toward a climactic confrontation that will test the very foundations of justice itself. The crackle of the airwaves carries you through dimly lit interrogation rooms and into smoke-filled offices where the fate of the guilty and innocent hangs in the balance.

Mr. District Attorney was more than mere entertainment during its remarkable thirteen-year run—it was a civics lesson for millions of Americans, celebrating the dedicated men and women who worked tirelessly in the criminal justice system. Born from the pages of actual court records and police files, each episode brought authentic legal drama directly into American homes, demystifying the courtroom and elevating the role of the district attorney as a guardian of public welfare. The show's meticulous attention to procedural detail set it apart from sensational crime dramas, offering listeners a window into how real justice was pursued and won.
