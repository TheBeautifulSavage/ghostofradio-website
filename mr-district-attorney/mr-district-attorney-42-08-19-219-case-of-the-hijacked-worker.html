Picture this: a darkened studio, the crackle of static, and then—the unmistakable voice of Jay Jostyn cutting through the night like a courthouse gavel. It's 1940, and somewhere in the sprawling corridors of the District Attorney's office, a case lands on the desk that demands justice. A worker—an honest man trying to make his living—vanishes without a trace, spirited away by criminals whose motives remain shrouded in shadow. As Mr. District Attorney himself investigates, listeners are drawn into a web of danger, deception, and desperate men willing to break the law. What begins as a simple disappearance becomes a taut game of cat-and-mouse, with our relentless prosecutor using every trick of the legal trade to outmaneuver the kidnappers and recover his man. The tension mounts with each clue, each false lead, each narrowing circle of suspects.

Mr. District Attorney stands as a towering achievement in the golden age of radio drama, bringing the machinery of American justice into living rooms across the nation at a time when faith in law and order felt paramount. The show's meticulous attention to legal procedure—its authentic courtroom language and procedural realism—set it apart from mere melodrama. Jostyn's commanding performance, paired with the show's tight scripts and atmospheric sound design, created something that felt urgently real to Depression-era and wartime audiences seeking reassurance that the system worked, that truth ultimately prevailed.

Join Mr. District Attorney as he pursues the case of the hijacked worker—a thrilling reminder of radio's power to grip the imagination and transport you directly into the halls of justice. Tune in and witness how one man's determination to uphold the law becomes a beacon against the darkness of crime.
