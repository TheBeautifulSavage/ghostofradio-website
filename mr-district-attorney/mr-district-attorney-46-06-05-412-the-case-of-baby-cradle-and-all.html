When the organ music swells and that stentorian voice intones "Mister District Attorney," listeners knew they were about to enter a world where justice hung by a thread—and this particular broadcast is no exception. In "The Case of Baby Cradle and All," an innocent infant becomes the centerpiece of a twisted extortion plot that will test our hero's moral compass and legal acumen alike. As darkness falls over the city, desperate criminals exploit the most primal of parental fears, setting in motion events that spiral from blackmail to violence. The District Attorney must navigate treacherous waters where one wrong move could mean tragedy for the family caught in the crossfire, while the clock ticks relentlessly toward the criminals' deadline. Every interrogation, every piece of evidence, every alibi comes alive through vivid sound effects—a baby's cry piercing the night, the metallic slam of cell doors, the scrape of a chair as tension coils tighter around the room.

What made Mister District Attorney such essential radio was its unflinching commitment to the actual mechanics of justice rather than sensationalism. Based on real cases and legal procedure, the show struck a chord with Depression-era and wartime audiences who craved both the reassurance of law and order and the gritty realism of how justice actually worked. By the 1940s, the series had become appointment listening, its influence shaping public perception of the legal system itself. This 1946 episode exemplifies the show's hallmark: meticulous plotting, snappy dialogue, and that distinctive balance between human compassion and professional duty.

Step into the pressurized world of mid-twentieth-century crime fighting. Tune in to "The Case of Baby Cradle and All" and discover why millions huddled around their radio sets, hanging on every word of this landmark legal drama.
