Step into the darkened projection booth of the Rialto Theatre, where flickering reels of celluloid hide a sinister secret. When a prominent film distributor is found dead beneath a cascade of burning film stock, Mr. District Attorney must navigate the labyrinthine world of Hollywood's seamier underside to expose the murderer. Is it the jealous theater owner? The blackmailed projectionist? Or perhaps someone with darker ties to the studio system itself? With only wisps of smoke and a single eyewitness account to guide him, our intrepid DA pursues justice through a maze of misdirection and carefully constructed lies. You'll hear the distinctive snap of breaking film reels, the ominous organ score swelling as suspects reveal their secrets, and the sharp crack of the district attorney's accusatory voice as he corners the guilty party in a tense confrontation.

Mr. District Attorney was America's courtroom conscience during the tumultuous 1940s, when faith in legal institutions wavered and corruption seemed to lurk in every shadow. This particular episode captures the show's golden period, when radio drama commanded the rapt attention of millions and the DA's crusade for justice represented a beacon of hope against a backdrop of wartime anxiety and social upheaval. The program's creator, Edward and Mona Uhlan, crafted mysteries that were not merely entertainment but moral instructionals—proof that the system, when wielded by honest men, could triumph over evil.
