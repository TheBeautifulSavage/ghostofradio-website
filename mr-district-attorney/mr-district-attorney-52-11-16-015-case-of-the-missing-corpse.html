# The Case of the Missing Corpse

Picture yourself huddled around the radio on a November evening in 1940, the crackle of static giving way to that familiar authoritative voice: "It's the People versus..." In "The Case of the Missing Corpse," listeners are plunged into the Manhattan District Attorney's office where the most baffling crime of the season unfolds. A body has vanished without trace from a locked morgue, and with it, the crucial evidence needed to prosecute a dangerous criminal. As Mr. District Attorney and his trusted investigator clash with clever defense attorneys and shadowy witnesses, the tension mounts with each revelation. Every clue seems to dissolve under scrutiny, every alibi crumbles—or does it? The missing corpse becomes more than a legal puzzle; it's a metaphor for justice itself, seemingly beyond reach. You'll find yourself leaning closer to the speaker, desperate to solve the mystery before the final commercial break.

This episode exemplifies why "Mr. District Attorney" became one of radio's most beloved crime dramas, running successfully from 1939 through the early 1950s. The show's creator Jay Dratler crafted scripts rooted in actual legal procedure and genuine courtroom drama, eschewing melodrama for psychological complexity. The program's popularity reflected Americans' fascination with law and order during an era of rising organized crime, offering listeners reassurance that justice—despite obstacles—would prevail. The role became iconic, particularly in later television adaptations, but it was these radio broadcasts that first captured the public imagination with their smart writing and taut performances.

This is quintessential Golden Age radio storytelling: intelligent, suspenseful, and ultimately satisfying. Whether you're a devoted fan of vintage crime dramas or discovering this classic for the first time, "The Case of the Missing Corpse" exemplifies everything that made radio's golden era unforgettable. Don't miss it.
