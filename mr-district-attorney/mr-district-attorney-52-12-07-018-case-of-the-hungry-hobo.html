Picture this: a December evening in the 1940s, the radiator hissing in the corner of your living room, the dial tuned to NBC, and the unmistakable voice of Jay Morley—the District Attorney himself—cutting through the static with grim determination. In "The Case of the Hungry Hobo," listeners encounter a tale that strips away the polished courtrooms and high-society crimes to examine something far more urgent: desperation born of Depression-era poverty. When a vagrant is found dead under circumstances that blur the line between accident and foul play, the D.A. must navigate a labyrinth of conflicting witnesses, questionable evidence, and the moral complexities that lurked beneath the surface of American society. The episode crackles with tension as the investigation unfolds, moving from railroad yards to flop houses, each scene drawn with the precise detail that made the show's writers legends in broadcasting.

What distinguished Mr. District Attorney from other crime dramas was its commitment to procedure and legal authenticity—the show consulted actual district attorneys and prosecutors to ensure its storytelling rang true. This episode particularly captures the show's social consciousness; rather than treat the hobo merely as a plot device, it examines the invisible lives of those cast aside by circumstance, giving voice to the voiceless during an era when radio had become America's window into justice itself. The series ran for over a decade because it understood that genuine drama lived not just in the verdict, but in the pursuit of truth.

Don't miss this remarkable piece of broadcasting history that proves crime drama can be both thrilling entertainment and social commentary. Tune in to experience a night in the office of the D.A., where justice was never as simple as guilt or innocence, and where the humble vagrant's death demands answers.
