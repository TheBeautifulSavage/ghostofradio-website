Step into the shadowed streets of 1940s America as Mr. District Attorney and his trusted investigator Harrington uncover a sinister web of violence centered around a notorious motorcycle club. When a brutal murder rocks the city, all evidence points to the leather-jacketed rebels who roar through the night—but is justice being served, or is an innocent rider being framed? As the investigation deepens, listeners will experience the crackling tension of courtroom cross-examinations, the gritty realism of police procedural work, and the moral complexity of a case that challenges everything the District Attorney believes about guilt and innocence. Every radio effect—the screech of tires, the slam of a gavel, the nervous breath of a witness taking the stand—pulls you deeper into a mystery where the truth lies hidden beneath layers of suspicion and prejudice.

Mr. District Attorney became an American institution by capturing the public's fascination with the machinery of justice at a moment when organized crime seemed to lurk on every corner and faith in law enforcement was both desperately needed and perpetually tested. Premiering in 1939, the show's meteoric success lay in its authentic partnership with actual district attorneys' offices, lending credibility and procedural accuracy that audiences craved. The early 1940s episodes, like this gripping case from 1940, represent the show at its zenith—balancing hard-boiled crime drama with surprising social commentary, exploring themes of class prejudice and whether the legal system truly served all citizens equally. Jay Jostyn's authoritative voice as the unnamed District Attorney became synonymous with justice itself, while the program's rapid-fire pacing and clever writing set the standard for crime dramas that would follow.
