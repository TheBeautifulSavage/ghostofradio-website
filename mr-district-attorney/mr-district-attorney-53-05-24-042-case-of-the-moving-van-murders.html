Picture yourself hunched close to your radio dial on a spring evening, the static crackling with anticipation as Mr. District Attorney's stentorian voice cuts through the darkness: "It is the duty of the District Attorney to prosecute to the limits of the law all persons accused of crime..." In this gripping 1940s episode, our fearless prosecutor finds himself navigating a labyrinth of shadowy clues surrounding a series of brutal murders connected to the city's moving and storage companies. What begins as a routine investigation spirals into a maze of blackmail, hidden identities, and desperate criminals who use the legitimate transportation industry as a front for their sinister operations. The tension mounts as our DA races against time, interrogating suspects whose stories don't quite add up, while the real killer remains concealed among them—waiting for the next opportunity to strike.

Mr. District Attorney was the gold standard of legal drama during radio's golden age, a show that gave listeners unprecedented access to the machinery of justice itself. Debuting in 1939 and running through the early 1950s, the program was celebrated for its authenticity and procedural detail, often drawing inspiration from real cases handled by New York District Attorneys. The show's format—opening with the DA's duty statement and closing with a moral reflection—established a template that would influence courtroom dramas for decades to come. By the 1940s, when this episode aired, the show had become appointment listening for millions who craved intelligent, hard-hitting crime drama that respected their intelligence.

If you're seeking authentic Golden Age radio drama—where justice hangs in the balance and clever detective work battles cunning criminality—The Case of the Moving Van Murders delivers the goods with style and substance.
