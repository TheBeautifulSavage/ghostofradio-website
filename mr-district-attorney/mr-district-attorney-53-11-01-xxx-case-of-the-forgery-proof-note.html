Settle into your favorite chair as the familiar strains of the "Mr. District Attorney" theme fill the airwaves—that authoritative march that signals justice is about to be served. In this November 1940 episode, our dogged protagonist faces a baffling case of forged documents that threatens to derail a seemingly airtight prosecution. When a seemingly "proof" note surfaces in evidence, allegedly signed by the perpetrator, the D.A. must unravel an intricate web of deception to determine which signature is genuine and which is clever fakery. With the courtroom clock ticking and reasonable doubt creeping into the jury box, listeners will find themselves pulled into the meticulous detective work that separates conviction from acquittal. The tension builds as handwriting experts clash, motives multiply, and the guilty party—whoever that may be—watches from the shadows.

"Mr. District Attorney" pioneered a distinctly American radio drama formula during the Golden Age of broadcasting: the procedural crime story told from law enforcement's perspective. Unlike the hard-boiled private eye shows that dominated the dial, this NBC/ABC series (running 1939-1952) offered listeners an inside look at the machinery of justice itself, presented with gravitas and authenticity. The show's creator drew inspiration from real case files and prosecutorial methodology, lending each episode a documentary-like credibility that audiences craved during an era when faith in institutions remained relatively intact. By the early 1940s, the program had become a cultural touchstone, celebrated for its intelligent writing and the steady-handed performance of its lead actor.

Don't miss this classic demonstration of radio drama at its finest. "Case of the Forgery Proof Note" exemplifies why millions tuned in faithfully each week to hear the district attorney proclaim: "It's the duty of the District Attorney to prosecute to the best of his ability, all violators of the law!"
