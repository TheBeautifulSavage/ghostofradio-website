When a mysterious biography lands on a publisher's desk—one that threatens to expose the carefully constructed life of wealthy industrialist Bennet Kilpatrick—Mr. Keen finds himself entangled in a web of secrets, blackmail, and dangerous deception. As the tracer of lost persons, Keen has spent years locating the missing and forgotten, but this case demands he uncover something far more elusive: the hidden truth behind a man's public facade. The tension crackles through your radio speaker as Keen pursues shadowy leads through foggy city streets and into the gilded drawing rooms of the powerful, each clue bringing him closer to a revelation that someone will stop at nothing to prevent. Who wrote the biography, and why now? What did Kilpatrick hide so desperately that murder becomes preferable to exposure? By the episode's climax, listeners are left suspended in that delicious moment of mystery—the moment before truth shatters everything.

From 1937 through the 1950s, Mr. Keen, Tracer of Lost Persons commanded loyal audiences precisely because it married accessible detective work with psychological depth. Unlike hard-boiled pulp heroes, Keen was a gentlemanly investigator whose cases hinged on understanding human nature, motive, and the fragile secrets people keep. This particular episode exemplifies the show's golden era in the 1940s, when writers crafted narratives that reflected wartime anxieties about identity, trust, and the masks we wear in society.
