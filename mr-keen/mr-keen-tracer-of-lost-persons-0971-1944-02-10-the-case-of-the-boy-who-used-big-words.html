Step into the rain-slicked streets of 1944 as Mr. Keen himself takes on one of his most perplexing cases yet—the mysterious disappearance of a boy whose vocabulary far exceeds his years. What begins as a simple missing persons report quickly unravels into a labyrinth of deception, where the youth's unusual eloquence becomes both the first clue and the deepest mystery. As Keen methodically traces the boy's movements through the city's shadowed corners and smoke-filled rooms, listeners will find themselves drawn into an atmosphere thick with suspense and moral complexity. Who would use a child's gift for language against him? And what darker forces lurk behind such calculated cruelty? The February 10th broadcast crackles with the unmistakable tension that made this series a fixture in American homes.

For nearly two decades, Mr. Keen, Tracer of Lost Persons captivated audiences with its unflinching portraits of ordinary people caught in extraordinary circumstances. Unlike the flashy detective shows of the era, Keen's cases explored the quieter tragedies of urban life—the runaways, the amnesiacs, the forgotten. In 1944, with wartime uncertainty gripping the nation, this episode's focus on a vulnerable child resonated particularly deeply. The show's success lay in its commitment to character-driven mystery and the warm, authoritative presence of its lead, making each case feel deeply human rather than sensational.

Whether you're a devoted fan of classic radio mystery or discovering Mr. Keen for the first time, this episode exemplifies why the show endured through nearly two decades on air. Tune in and let yourself be transported to an America where a single clue could unravel everything, and where Mr. Keen's patient detective work might be the only hope for a lost child.
