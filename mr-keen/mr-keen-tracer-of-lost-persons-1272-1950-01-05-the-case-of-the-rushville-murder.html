On this crisp January evening in 1950, Mr. Keen and his faithful secretary Friday face their most perplexing challenge yet: a murder in the quiet town of Rushville that defies explanation. A prominent citizen lies dead, and the evidence points in every direction at once—toward a grieving widow with secrets of her own, a business partner nursing old grudges, and a mysterious stranger who vanished the very night the crime occurred. As the investigation unfolds with meticulous precision, listeners will find themselves drawn deeper into a web of deception where nothing is quite as it seems. The sound of footsteps on rain-slicked streets, the hushed tones of frightened witnesses, and Mr. Keen's characteristic methodical questioning all build toward a revelation that will leave you breathless.

For nearly thirteen years by 1950, Mr. Keen, Tracer of Lost Persons had captivated audiences with its realistic approach to detective work, eschewing the melodramatic flourishes favored by contemporaries in favor of careful, logical investigation. This episode exemplifies the show's enduring appeal: a genuine puzzle constructed with respect for the listener's intelligence, featuring the reliable and increasingly seasoned partnership between the eponymous detective and his devoted secretary. The program's popularity across multiple networks and its longevity testified to America's fascination with procedural mystery during the postwar years—a time when order and rational solutions held particular appeal.

Whether you're a devoted follower of Mr. Keen's cases or discovering this classic detective series for the first time, "The Case of the Rushville Murder" promises an hour of genuine suspense. Settle in, dim the lights, and let your imagination transport you to a world where mysteries are solved through wit, determination, and the invaluable partnership of two seasoned investigators. The answer awaits—if you can solve it before Mr. Keen does.
