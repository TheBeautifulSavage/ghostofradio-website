When the telephone rings at Mr. Keen's modest office on this April evening in 1950, it brings news of a corpse discovered in the genteel surroundings of an exclusive country club—a place where murder seems as out of place as a gumshoe in white tie and tails. A prominent member has been found dead under circumstances that defy easy explanation, and the country club's reputation hangs in the balance. As Mr. Keen and his resourceful assistant Mike Clancy begin their investigation, listeners will find themselves navigating a world of whispered accusations, hidden rivalries, and the kind of secrets that fester beneath polished mahogany and manicured lawns. The elite members of the club become suspects, each with motive and opportunity, and what appears to be a simple case of murder transforms into a labyrinth of deception that demands Keen's legendary detective skills to unravel.

For thirteen years by 1950, Mr. Keen, Tracer of Lost Persons had captivated audiences with its formula of meticulous investigation and psychological insight. Unlike the bombastic action-adventure shows that dominated radio's golden age, Mr. Keen represented a more cerebral approach to mystery—methodical, urbane, and rooted in the everyday world of missing persons and small crimes that defined the real detective's work. Creator Charles Vanda crafted a program that valued patience and logic over gunplay, making the show an intellectual's favorite while maintaining enough dramatic tension to keep casual listeners enthralled.

If you've never experienced the measured confidence of Art Van Harvey's Mr. Keen or the quick-witted banter between Keen and Clancy, The Country Club Murder Case offers the perfect entry point into this classic detective series. Tune in and discover why millions of Americans made this show essential evening listening.
