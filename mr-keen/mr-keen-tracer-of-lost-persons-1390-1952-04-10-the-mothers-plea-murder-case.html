A desperate mother's voice crackles through the static, her words trembling with urgency as she begs Mr. Keen to find her son—a young man whose disappearance has left only questions and a growing suspicion of foul play. In this gripping 1952 episode, listeners are drawn into the fog-shrouded streets of America where the line between a missing person and a murder victim blurs with each clue uncovered. With his characteristic blend of shrewd deduction and human compassion, the legendary tracer takes the case, guided by nothing but a mother's plea and his own considerable detective instincts. The tension builds as interviews yield contradictory stories, evidence suggests the unthinkable, and Mr. Keen inches closer to a truth that may shatter more than one family.

For fifteen years by 1952, Mr. Keen, Tracer of Lost Persons had captivated millions of radio listeners with its authentic procedural approach to detective work. Unlike the more theatrical crime dramas of the era, the show grounded itself in real investigative methodology and genuine human tragedy, with each case drawing from actual missing person scenarios. By the early 1950s, the program had become a cultural fixture, its opening theme instantly recognizable to anyone who tuned in to NBC or CBS. This particular episode exemplifies the show's mature period, when writers crafted increasingly complex narratives that explored not just the mystery, but the emotional toll of secrets and lies on ordinary families.
