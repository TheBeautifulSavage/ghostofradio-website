# My Favorite Husband – "The French Lessons"

Picture yourself settling into your favorite armchair on a December evening in 1949, tuning the dial to CBS just as Lucille Ball's voice crackles through the speaker with barely contained exasperation. In "The French Lessons," Liz Cooper has taken it upon herself to become fluent in French—ostensibly for cultured reasons, but really to one-up her insufferable neighbor. What follows is a delightful collision of misplaced confidence and linguistic disaster, as her long-suffering husband George finds himself enlisted as her unwilling practice partner, subjected to phonetic butchering that grows more hilarious with each fumbled *conjugaison*. The writing sparkles with that perfect domestic comedy timing where a simple household decision spirals into romantic misunderstanding, mistaken identity, and the kind of verbal sparring that only a married couple can pull off. You'll hear the audience's warm laughter building as the situation cascades from merely awkward to absolutely farcical.

*My Favorite Husband* occupies a unique place in radio history as the experimental laboratory where Lucille Ball developed the comedic timing and physical humor that would later revolutionize television. This show, running from 1948 to 1951, gave Ball the freedom to explore her craft with a stellar writing team, creating scenarios that were simultaneously relatable and gloriously absurd. The domestic comedy format—centered on the gentle bickering and genuine affection between a married couple—resonated deeply with post-war American listeners hungry for lighthearted entertainment after the hardships of the Depression and World War II.

Step back in time and experience the quicksilver wit and impeccable comedic chemistry that made this forgotten gem essential listening for millions. "The French Lessons" showcases everything that made *My Favorite Husband* a launching pad for greatness.
