Aunt Harriet is coming to town, and pandemonium is about to break loose in this delightfully chaotic episode of My Friend Irma. Picture the scene: Jane and Irma's modest apartment transforms into a flurry of nervous energy as the girls scramble to make the place presentable for the formidable matriarch. What could possibly go wrong? Everything, of course—and listeners will find themselves roaring with laughter as our beloved scatterbrain Irma hatches increasingly absurd schemes to hide evidence of her usual shenanigans. The crackle of the studio audience's laughter provides the perfect backdrop to Marie Wilson's masterful comic timing, while the supporting cast—including the ever-patient Al and the bewildered landlord—get swept up in the whirlwind of preparation and comic misunderstanding that defines this show's irresistible charm.

Since its debut on CBS in 1947, My Friend Irma has become appointment listening for millions of American families huddled around their radio sets. The show's brilliant chemistry between Wilson's endearingly dim-witted Irma and Cathy Lewis's exasperated straight-woman Jane created a comedy template that would influence television decades later. By 1948, the program had become such a cultural phenomenon that it spawned a feature film, cementing Irma's place in the American comedy pantheon. This particular episode captures the show at its peak—confident in its formula, sharp in its writing, and utterly committed to the notion that the most ordinary domestic situations, filtered through Irma's warped logic, become comedic gold.

Don't miss this sparkling gem from radio's golden age. Tune in to hear why America simply couldn't get enough of everyone's favorite dumb blonde and the infinite variety of trouble she could manufacture from a single visit. It's comedy magic preserved in time.
