When the orchestra strikes up at the Starlight Ballroom, our beloved Irma Peterson finds herself caught between worlds—a working girl thrust into high society's glittering evening, where every step threatens to topple her carefully constructed facade of sophistication. In this October 1948 episode, listeners will delight in the familiar chaos that erupts when Irma's friend Jane tries to transform her into a debutante for the evening's charity fundraiser. But Irma wouldn't be Irma without her malapropisms, her schemes, and her uncanny ability to turn formal affairs into comedic pandemonium. As the night unfolds, expect mistaken identities, slapstick mishaps on the dance floor, and the irrepressible charm that has made this series America's most beloved domestic comedy.

My Friend Irma captured the postwar American imagination at precisely the moment when radio comedy was reaching its golden zenith. Premiering on CBS just after the war's end, the show transformed a simple premise—the exploits of two working women sharing an apartment—into a cultural phenomenon that would eventually spawn two Hollywood films and define an era. The chemistry between Marie Wilson's dippy but endearing Irma and Cathy Lewis's sardonic Jane became the template for the "dizzy dame" archetype that would influence comedy for decades. By 1948, the show was in its second year of phenomenal success, a masterclass in ensemble timing where even minor characters became beloved fixtures in millions of American living rooms.

Step back into 1948 and experience the glamour, the humor, and the perfectly timed comic genius that made My Friend Irma unmissable radio. This is comedy at its most sophisticated—sophisticated, that is, in how beautifully it captures the anxious aspirations and genuine affection between friends navigating postwar American life.
