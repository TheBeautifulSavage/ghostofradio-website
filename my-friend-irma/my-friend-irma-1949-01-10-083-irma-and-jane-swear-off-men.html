Picture it: a cramped Manhattan apartment on a winter evening in 1949, where two exasperated young women have finally reached their breaking point with the romantic entanglements that have consumed their lives. In this delightfully frantic episode, Irma Peterson and her roommate Jane decide to make a solemn pact—they're swearing off men entirely. What follows is a masterclass in comedic chaos as their noble resolution crumbles almost immediately, punctuated by Marie Wilson's infectious giggling and the rapid-fire banter that made audiences howl with laughter week after week. Listeners will revel in the misunderstandings, the scheming, and the inevitable romantic complications that ensue when two determined women discover that avoiding the opposite sex is far easier said than done.

My Friend Irma was more than just comedy—it was a cultural phenomenon that reflected the anxieties and desires of post-war America. Premiering on CBS Radio in 1947, the show captured the spirit of young, independent women navigating romance, friendship, and urban life in a rapidly changing world. Marie Wilson's portrayal of the lovable, dim-witted Irma became iconic, while her chemistry with cast-mate Cathy Lewis kept listeners tuning in night after night. By 1949, the show had become a powerhouse, eventually spawning two Hollywood films and proving that sitcom gold didn't require sophisticated plotting—just heart, impeccable timing, and characters audiences genuinely cared about.

Join Irma and Jane on their hilariously doomed quest for independence from romance. This is vintage radio comedy at its finest—where a simple premise spirals into thirty minutes of pure delight. Tune in and discover why millions of Americans made My Friend Irma appointment listening.
