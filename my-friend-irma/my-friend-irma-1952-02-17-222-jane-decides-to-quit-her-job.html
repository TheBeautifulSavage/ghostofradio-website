# My Friend Irma — February 17, 1952

Step into the Manhattan apartment of two working girls as domestic tranquility collides with career ambition in this delightful episode of America's favorite comedy. Jane, the sensible foil to Irma's scatterbrained schemes, has reached her breaking point at the office—and she's ready to walk away from it all. What begins as idle grumbling over apartment coffee transforms into genuine crisis as her best friend Irma, despite her well-meaning heart, fumbles through the role of confidante. Listeners will relish the rapid-fire banter, the perfectly-timed sound effects of creaking chairs and slamming doors, and those exquisitely awkward moments when Irma's logic turns every attempt at consolation upside down. The stakes feel genuinely personal here—this isn't just radio comedy, it's the everyday turbulence of 1950s working women navigating jobs, friendship, and the question of what they really want from life.

*My Friend Irma* became a cultural phenomenon because it captured authentic female friendship at a moment when radio was still America's living room. Created by Cy Howard and starring the incomparable Marie Wilson as Irma and Cathy Lewis as the perpetually exasperated Jane, the show balanced slapstick humor with surprisingly touching portraits of women supporting each other through life's uncertainties. In 1952, as post-war America wrestled with changing gender roles—women entering the workforce in unprecedented numbers—episodes like this resonated deeply, validating the tensions between independence and security that listeners faced daily.

Don't miss this gem of mid-century radio comedy. Tune in as Jane contemplates a life-changing decision, guided by an Irma whose heart is pure even when her advice is pure chaos. It's the kind of entertainment that made radio golden.
