# The Mysterious Traveler: Death Has A Cold Breath

As March snow falls upon a desolate mountain road, a traveling salesman's car sputters to a halt, stranding him at an isolated inn where the other guests seem far too comfortable with death. In "Death Has A Cold Breath," you'll discover that sometimes the warmth we desperately seek comes at a terrible price. The crackle of the radio fades, and suddenly you're trapped in that frigid lobby, watching shadows dance across frost-covered windows, listening to cryptic conversations that hint at something far more sinister than mere chance. Fate, it seems, collects its debts on the coldest nights.

*The Mysterious Traveler* was a stalwart of American radio from 1943 to 1952, arriving each week on the Mutual Network with tales of ordinary people confronted by extraordinary darkness. This March 1949 broadcast represents the show at its narrative height—sophisticated plotting, atmospheric sound design, and that ineffable quality that made listeners check their locks before bed. Host Maurice Tarplin's urbane introduction and the show's trademark theremin-tinged orchestrations created an experience that transcended simple entertainment; it was theater for the mind, where your imagination became the most effective special effect. In an era before television dominated the living room, *The Mysterious Traveler* reminded audiences why radio remained the supreme medium for suspense.

Tonight, settle into that worn armchair, extinguish the brightest lamps, and let your thoughts drift toward mountain inns and mysterious strangers. Press play on "Death Has A Cold Breath" and discover why, for nearly a decade, millions of Americans made appointments with *The Mysterious Traveler*—a figure who knew their darkest fears and always arrived precisely when needed most.
