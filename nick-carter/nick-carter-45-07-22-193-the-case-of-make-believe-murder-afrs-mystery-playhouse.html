When the orchestra strikes up that unmistakable Nick Carter theme on the evening of July 22nd, 1945, listeners settle in for a masterclass in misdirection and psychological suspense. In "The Case of Make Believe Murder," our intrepid detective finds himself entangled in a web of theatrical deception where the line between performance and reality becomes dangerously blurred. A wealthy patron's elaborate hoax—designed to frame an innocent rival—spirals catastrophically out of control, and what began as a cruel game of pretend transforms into genuine murder. Nick must navigate layer upon layer of lies, each performance more convincing than the last, to uncover which deception conceals the ultimate truth. The tension builds expertly throughout, punctuated by the sharp crack of gunshots and the breathless testimony of witnesses who can no longer distinguish fact from fiction.

This episode represents the golden age of detective radio drama, when Nick Carter's adventures dominated the airwaves of America. Originally a pulp magazine sensation dating back to 1886, the character found new life in broadcast form, captivating millions of radio listeners who thrilled to his methodical investigations and keen intellect. The 1943-1955 run on the Mutual network brought professional actors, sophisticated sound design, and Hollywood-quality writing to American living rooms. Episodes like "The Case of Make Believe Murder" showcase why the show remained essential evening entertainment—smart scripts that respected audience intelligence, performed with conviction by talented voice actors, and enriched by atmospheric sound effects that transformed imagination into visceral drama.

This is radio at its finest: storytelling that demands nothing but your attention and rewards it with genuine mystery and excitement. Tune in and let Nick Carter guide you through the shadows.
