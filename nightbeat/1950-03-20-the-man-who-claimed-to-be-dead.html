Nightbeat NBC · March 20, 1950

The Man Who Claimed To Be Dead

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

When Chicago private investigator Frank McNally arrives at the Palmer House Hotel on a fog-shrouded November evening, he expects a routine missing persons case. Instead, he finds himself face-to-face with a ghost—a man claiming to be deceased for three years, his corpse allegedly buried in Calvary Cemetery, yet here he stands in the flesh with a wild tale of mistaken identity and underground corruption. As Frank digs deeper into the labyrinth of forged death certificates, mob-connected undertakers, and crooked politicians, the line between the living and the dead becomes dangerously blurred. With Barton Yarborough's trademark gravelly voice cutting through the synthesized fog effects and Harry Lumsden's moody trumpet score setting the pulse of danger, "The Man Who Claimed To Be Dead" delivers the claustrophobic tension and moral ambiguity that made Nightbeat appointment listening for millions of Americans huddled around their radio sets.

Nightbeat emerged in 1950 as NBC's answer to the post-war hunger for gritty, adult crime drama—a show that rejected the comic-book heroics of earlier radio detectives in favor of authentic Chicago corruption and morally compromised characters. Produced by William Spier and steeped in the procedural realism of hard-boiled fiction, each episode tackled the city's underbelly with a documentary-like precision that earned it critical acclaim even as sponsors worried the content was too dark. This particular episode exemplifies why the show became a landmark in radio drama: it treats its absurdist premise with absolute seriousness, forcing listeners to contemplate questions of identity and justice that linger long after the final commercial break.

Tune in to experience one of Nightbeat's finest thirty minutes—a masterclass in noir atmosphere, where the impossible becomes plausible and nobody in Chicago can be trusted.
