Nightbeat NBC · May 8, 1950

The Elevator Caper

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

It's midnight in the Chicago Stockyards, and our man Nightbeat is riding an elevator to the thirty-second floor of the Hartwell Building—a journey that should take ninety seconds but might end with a bullet in his ribs. Someone's been stealing diamonds from the jewelry exchange, and the trail of clues has led our hard-boiled detective into a trap as tight as a locked steel cage, where the only way out is down. As the elevator hums upward and sweat beads on Nightbeat's brow, you'll hear the signature crackle of static, the distant wail of a Chicago police siren, and Franklyn MacCormack's narration cutting through the darkness like a switchblade—every word dripping with menace and the kind of street-smart fatalism that only the Windy City could breed.

Nightbeat arrived on NBC's airwaves in 1950 as the apotheosis of urban noir radio, arriving just as television was beginning to lure listeners away from their receivers. The show's creator, William Robson, crafted something defiantly theatrical and cinematically ambitious for the medium, transforming Chicago itself into a character as compelling as any femme fatale or corrupt cop. This was radio noir in its purest form—before the form would be largely abandoned—with sound design that rivaled anything produced before or since. "The Elevator Caper" exemplifies why devoted listeners considered Nightbeat essential listening: it's a tight, perfectly calibrated thirty minutes of suspense that proves radio drama could be as gripping as anything on the silver screen.

Pull up a chair in the dark. Light a cigarette. Nightbeat is waiting.
