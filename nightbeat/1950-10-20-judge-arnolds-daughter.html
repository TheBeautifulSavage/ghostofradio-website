# Judge Arnold's Daughter

The rain hammers against Frank McNally's windshield as he prowls the neon-soaked streets of Chicago's West Side, his cigarette glowing like a warning beacon in the November darkness. When the judge's daughter turns up dead in a warehouse on the docks—her silk dress torn, her lipstick smeared—McNally knows this isn't just another case. The judge is powerful, connected, and desperate to bury the truth. But our detective's conscience won't let him rest, not when the evidence points toward someone the judge himself wants to frame. This episode crackles with the moral ambiguity that made *Nightbeat* essential listening: a world where the law protects the powerful, where loyalty and justice collide, and where one honest man must decide how far he'll go to find the real killer.

*Nightbeat* arrived in 1950 at precisely the moment American audiences craved something grittier than the sanitized mysteries flooding their airwaves. Set in a Chicago that John Bartlow Martin himself—the show's creator and a genuine crime reporter—knew intimately, it captured the postwar city's corruption, desperation, and raw energy with documentary-like authenticity. Frank McNally wasn't a gentleman detective or a genius; he was a working cop trying to navigate systems rigged against the innocent. Judge Arnold's Daughter exemplifies why the show became a cult favorite among radio connoisseurs, praised for its hard-boiled dialogue, naturalistic sound design, and willingness to question authority.

If you've never experienced the particular thrill of *Nightbeat*—that intoxicating blend of jazz-inflected mystery and genuine moral questioning—here's your moment. Dim the lights, pour yourself something strong, and let Frank McNally guide you through Chicago's darkest corners.
