# The Glass Donkey

When Philip Marlowe accepts a seemingly routine case involving a stolen glass figurine, he finds himself threading the razor-sharp needle between two deadly worlds—a world of high society with secrets worth killing for, and the shadowy underworld that preys upon it. The missing donkey, delicate and worthless in monetary terms, becomes the key to unraveling a conspiracy of blackmail, passion, and murder that reaches far deeper than anyone anticipated. Listeners will be drawn into Los Angeles's fog-shrouded streets as Marlowe's distinctive voice—weary but determined—guides them through a labyrinth of false leads and dangerous encounters. This episode crackles with the signature tension of the finest noir: the moment when an offhand remark becomes a death sentence, when the wrong question asked to the wrong person means a .38 slug in the dark.

The 1940s saw American audiences embrace the private detective as their new folk hero, and Raymond Chandler's Philip Marlowe became the gold standard of the genre. CBS's adaptation, featuring Van Heflin's perfectly calibrated performance in the title role, captured the literary sophistication of Chandler's work while delivering the fast-paced action that radio audiences craved. "The Glass Donkey" exemplifies this balance—it's a puzzle box of a mystery wrapped in hard-boiled atmosphere, proof that detective radio drama was every bit as intelligent and artfully crafted as the novels that inspired it.

For those who appreciate the lost art of radio mystery, few things match the experience of settling in with a strong drink and a strong story. Let Philip Marlowe guide you back to a Los Angeles that exists now only in memory and celluloid—where honor still meant something, and a detective's code was worth more than gold.
