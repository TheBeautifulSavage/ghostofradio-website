# Don't Tell Me About Halloween

As the opening theme of *Quiet Please* fades into an unsettling silence, listeners in 1947 found themselves drawn into a tale that tapped into something far more disturbing than Halloween costumes and pranks. "Don't Tell Me About Halloween" presents a psychological mystery wrapped in autumnal dread—a story where what remains unspoken proves infinitely more terrifying than any monster lurking in the dark. The episode unfolds with mounting tension as a character becomes increasingly desperate to avoid any mention of the holiday, and his companions' innocent enthusiasm only deepens the sense of creeping horror. With masterful sound design and the intimate delivery that made *Quiet Please* legendary, this broadcast captures the authentic fear that comes not from jump scares, but from the slow realization that something deeply wrong has transpired.

*Quiet Please* emerged during radio's golden age as one of the medium's most audaciously experimental horror programs, pioneering the psychological thriller format that would later influence television and cinema. Where competitors relied on monsters and violence, showrunner Wyllis Cooper crafted stories rooted in human vulnerability and existential dread. The series thrived precisely because listeners couldn't see what they were imagining—their own minds supplied the terror. This particular episode exemplifies the show's genius for taking something familiar and wholesome and inverting it, making the prospect of Halloween itself feel like a harbinger of something unspeakable.

For those seeking authentic golden-age horror that respects the listener's intelligence, "Don't Tell Me About Halloween" remains unmissable. Tune in with the lights dimmed and the radio turned just loud enough—*Quiet Please* works best when you're genuinely unsettled. Some mysteries, you'll discover, are better left unresolved.
