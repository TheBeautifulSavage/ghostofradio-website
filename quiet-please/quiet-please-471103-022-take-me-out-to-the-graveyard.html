# Quiet Please: "Take Me Out To The Graveyard"

Picture this: a cool autumn evening, the kind where shadows stretch long across neglected gravestones. *Quiet Please* invites you into a tale where the boundary between the living and the dead grows dangerously thin. In this episode, an innocent proposition—a trip to the ballpark—takes a sinister turn when a man's obsession with a baseball game becomes entangled with something far more sinister lurking among the burial grounds. What begins as nostalgic Americana gradually dissolves into mounting dread, as listeners discover that some games are played not for glory, but for the very soul itself. The masterful sound design—creaking iron gates, whispered voices carried on the wind, the lonely crack of a bat echoing across empty fields—builds an atmosphere so palpable you'll find yourself holding your breath. By the episode's climax, the cheerful promise of baseball has curdled into something altogether darker.

*Quiet Please* stands as one of radio's most underrated achievements, a weekly sanctuary for those who craved psychological terror delivered through the intimate medium of sound. Hosted and largely created by Wyllis Cooper, this 1947-1949 series proved that genuine horror required neither violence nor monsters—only suggestion, timing, and the listener's own imagination. "Take Me Out To The Graveyard" exemplifies Cooper's genius for corrupting the familiar, transforming beloved American traditions into vessels for the uncanny. Each episode was crafted with surgical precision, where every footstep, every pause, every carefully modulated whisper became a tool of unease.

If you've ever wondered what truly frightened listeners huddled around their sets in the late 1940s, wonder no longer. Tune in to *Quiet Please* and discover why this forgotten gem deserves a place alongside *The Twilight Zone* in the pantheon of classic suspense. In the darkness of your home, with nothing but your radio and imagination, you'll understand why "Quiet Please" became a nightly ritual for those brave enough to listen.
