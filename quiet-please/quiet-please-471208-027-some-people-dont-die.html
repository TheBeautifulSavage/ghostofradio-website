# Some People Don't Die

Step into the shadows with this chilling installment of *Quiet Please*, where the boundary between life and death grows dangerously thin. In "Some People Don't Die," listeners will discover that mortality itself may be negotiable—a terrifying proposition explored through the story of a man who discovers he cannot escape existence, no matter how desperately he wishes to. As host Ernest Chappell's measured voice guides you through darkened rooms and whispered conversations, the sound design pulls you deeper into a nightmare of immortality. Every footstep echoes with dread, every silence screams with implication. This is *Quiet Please* at its most unsettling, a meditation on the curse of endless existence that will leave you questioning whether death is truly an ending—or merely a beginning we never wanted.

*Quiet Please* carved its legacy during radio's golden age as one of the few anthology series to prioritize genuine psychological terror over cheap theatrics. Premiering in 1947, the show distinguished itself through sophisticated writing, subtle sound design, and Chappell's hypnotic narration, creating an intimate horror experience that felt less like spectacle and more like a confession whispered in the dark. Unlike the bombastic sound effects of contemporaries, *Quiet Please* understood that the most terrifying stories unfold quietly, in the spaces between words. "Some People Don't Die" exemplifies this mastery, tackling existential dread with the precision of a scalpel rather than the bluntness of an axe.

If you've never ventured into the carefully constructed horrors of *Quiet Please*, this episode serves as the perfect entry point into a series that respected its audience's intelligence and imagination. Tune in, dim the lights, and listen closely—some stories demand our silence.
