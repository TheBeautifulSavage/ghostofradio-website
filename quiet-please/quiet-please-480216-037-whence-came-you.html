# Quiet Please: "Whence Came You"

Settle into your chair as darkness falls outside, dear listener, and prepare yourself for a journey into the unsettling unknown. In "Whence Came You," the Quiet Please ensemble crafts a tale of mysterious visitation and creeping dread that will keep you glancing nervously over your shoulder long after the final fade-out. A stranger arrives at a quiet home with an impossible familiarity, speaking of things no outsider could know, of places and moments locked away in memory. The dialogue grows increasingly tense as the host begins to wonder: who is this visitor, really? What does he want? And most chillingly—where exactly has he come from? The sound design wraps around you like fog rolling in from nowhere, each creak of a floorboard and whisper of wind serving to isolate you further in the intimate horror of the drama.

Quiet Please stands as one of radio's most sophisticated ventures into psychological terror, eschewing the monsters and mad scientists favored by its contemporaries in favor of something far more disturbing: the collapse of ordinary certainty. Broadcast between 1947 and 1949, the series proved that horror need not be loud or spectacular to be genuinely frightening. Producer-writer Wyllis Cooper understood that listeners would supply their own terrors, that suggestion outweighed spectacle. Each episode unfolds with the careful pacing of a nightmare, building an atmosphere of wrongness that seeps into the listening experience itself.

This is radio drama at its finest—a reminder of when families gathered around the set and surrendered themselves to the power of voice and sound. Don't miss "Whence Came You." Turn off the lights, adjust your dial, and listen carefully. Something is waiting to whisper to you from the static.
