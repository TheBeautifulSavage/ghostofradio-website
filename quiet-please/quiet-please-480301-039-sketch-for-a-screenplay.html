# Quiet Please: Sketch For A Screenplay

In this haunting installment from the golden age of radio drama, a struggling screenwriter discovers that art and reality blur in the most unsettling ways. As the protagonist frantically sketches scenes for what he believes will be his breakthrough film, each scene he commits to paper seems to manifest itself in increasingly sinister ways—until he can no longer distinguish between the fictional horrors he's creating and the genuine terror creeping into his own life. What begins as a tale of artistic ambition transforms into a nightmarish descent where imagination becomes indistinguishable from prophecy. The soundscape is claustrophobic and intimate, pulling listeners into the writer's deteriorating state of mind with every creak of a pen, every whisper of dread.

*Quiet Please*, which aired from 1947 to 1949, stands as one of radio's most sophisticated ventures into psychological horror. Unlike its contemporaries that relied on monsters and mayhem, this Mutual/ABC series excelled at exploring the darker corners of the human psyche, crafting stories where the real terror came from doubt, obsession, and the fragility of sanity. Created during a post-war era hungry for sophisticated entertainment, the show's writers—including some Hollywood talent—brought cinematic sensibilities to the intimate medium of radio, proving that genuine dread required only a carefully modulated voice, masterful sound design, and a story that understood how to make listeners question their own reality.

For those seeking a masterclass in atmospheric radio drama, "Sketch For A Screenplay" exemplifies everything that made *Quiet Please* essential listening. It's a reminder that the most powerful scares aren't what we see, but what we imagine—and this episode will make you imagine plenty. Tune in and discover why listeners still speak of *Quiet Please* in hushed tones, decades after its final broadcast.
