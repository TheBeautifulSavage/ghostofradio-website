# Let The Lillies Consider

In this haunting installment of Quiet Please, listeners will find themselves drawn into the suffocating quiet of a hospital room where a man lies dying, attended only by the ticking of a clock and the whispered concerns of those who love him. The episode unfolds with masterful restraint—there are no shrieking violins or crash-bang sound effects here, only the subtle rustling of bedsheets, the clinical murmur of doctors, and the protagonist's fading internal monologue as he contemplates mortality and meaning in his final hours. The title itself, drawn from Matthew 6:28, suggests a meditation on nature's indifference to human suffering, and the episode delivers on that promise with an understated dread that builds not through spectacle but through psychological precision. What begins as a simple deathbed vigil gradually reveals itself to be something far more unsettling—a exploration of what we leave behind and whether anything we do truly matters.

Quiet Please distinguished itself from its contemporary anthology competitors through its philosophy that terror lived not in the grotesque but in the quiet moments of existence. Created by Wyllis Cooper and produced with painstaking attention to sound design, the show's mere forty-two episodes (broadcast between 1947-1949) have become legendary among radio historians for their literary ambition and emotional depth. "Let The Lillies Consider" exemplifies this approach—it's a show that trusts its audience's imagination and intelligence, offering suggestion rather than exposition, silence rather than sensation. In an era when radio was increasingly dominated by action-adventure serials and broad comedies, Quiet Please remained defiantly intimate and introspective, proving that the medium's greatest strength was its ability to inhabit the listener's mind.

If you've never experienced the distinctive chill of Quiet Please, this episode is an ideal entry point—a perfect marriage of literary sensibility and audio artistry that explains why these forgotten broadcasts still captivate listeners today.
