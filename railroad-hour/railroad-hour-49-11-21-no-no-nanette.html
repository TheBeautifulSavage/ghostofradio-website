# Railroad Hour: No, No, Nanette

Step into the intimate darkness of your living room and adjust your radio dial to the frequency of pure theatrical magic. On this November evening in 1949, *The Railroad Hour* whisks you aboard a golden-age express bound for the glittering theaters of Broadway itself. Tonight's destination: Vincent Youmans' effervescent masterpiece *No, No, Nanette*, that irrepressible 1925 confection of mistaken identities, flirtation, and infectious jazz-age charm. As the orchestra swells with the unmistakable strains of "Tea for Two," you'll hear a stellar cast bring to life the romantic entanglements of a wealthy book publisher, his would-be paramours, and a spirited young woman named Nanette who threatens to upend their carefully constructed social world. The crisp dialogue crackles with wit, while the musical numbers burst with the vitality of an era when radio could transport millions into a Broadway theater without leaving home.

*The Railroad Hour* represented something revolutionary in American entertainment—a weekly celebration of the American musical theater that brought Broadway's most beloved productions to the masses. Between 1948 and 1954, the program became a cultural institution, boasting elaborate orchestrations, Hollywood-caliber talent, and a reverence for theatrical music that elevated radio drama to genuine art. Each episode was a complete musical performance, compressed and reimagined for the intimate medium of radio, introducing countless Americans to standards they might never encounter otherwise.

Don't miss this delightful journey back to the Jazz Age. Tune in and experience why *No, No, Nanette* became one of the era's most beloved comedies—a show where love conquers convention, laughter rings true, and the music never stops playing.
