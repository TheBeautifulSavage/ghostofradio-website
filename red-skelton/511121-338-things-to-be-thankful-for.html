The Red Skelton Show NBC/CBS · November 21, 1951

Things To Be Thankful For

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

As the opening theme swells through your radio speaker, Red Skelton's unmistakable voice cracks with genuine warmth—tonight, he's setting aside the pratfalls and rubber-faced comedy that made him a household name to deliver something rarer and more precious. In this Thanksgiving episode, Skelton takes listeners on a poignant journey through the small mercies of American life: a child's laughter, a soldier's letter from overseas, the simple dignity of honest work. Between tender musical interludes, Red's characters emerge—Willy Lilly and his absurd observations, Clem Kadiddlehopper with his yokel wisdom—but tonight they're all in service of genuine reflection. You'll hear the audience shift from their usual roaring laughter to something quieter, more contemplative, as Skelton reminds Depression and wartime-weary Americans what truly matters. It's comedy with a conscience, a vaudeville heart beating beneath genuine sentiment.

The Red Skelton Show arrived at radio's golden moment, when variety entertainment ruled the airwaves and a performer's ability to shift from slapstick to sincerity could make or break their career. Skelton possessed that rare gift in abundance—a clown capable of touching the heart without losing the funny bone. During the 1940s, as the nation grappled with war and uncertainty, Skelton's program became a Friday night institution, offering families a respite while never shying away from the era's deeper currents. This Thanksgiving special exemplifies why he remained beloved for decades: he understood that laughter and meaning weren't opposites, but partners in the American experience.

Don't miss this remarkable program. Settle in with the static crackle and let Red Skelton remind you why radio once united a nation every week.
