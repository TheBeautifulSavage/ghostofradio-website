Richard Diamond 49 06 26 (010) The Tom Waxman Bombing Case

· GHOST OF RADIO ·

When the phone rings in Richard Diamond's office on a sweltering June evening, it carries news that will plunge the Manhattan detective into one of his most explosive cases—literally. A prominent industrialist named Tom Waxman has survived a bombing that levels his penthouse, but the would-be assassin remains at large, and the motives are as murky as the smoke still rising from the wreckage. What unfolds is a masterclass in noir tension: Diamond navigating the shadowy intersection of high finance, personal vengeance, and organized crime, his sharp dialogue cutting through danger like a knife through silk. The taut pacing and atmospheric sound design—the crackle of flames, the ominous tick of a timer, the click of a revolver's safety—pull listeners into a world where trust is currency and death is just one misstep away.

Richard Diamond premiered in 1949 and became NBC's answer to the golden age of detective radio, eventually jumping to CBS before its 1953 finale. The show's brilliance lay in its marriage of hard-boiled dialogue (written with genuine wit, not mere pastiche) and genuinely inventive plotting. Starring Dick Powell as the wisecracking Diamond, the series captured that liminal moment when radio drama was reaching its artistic apex, knowing its days were numbered as television rose on the horizon. Episodes like "The Tom Waxman Bombing Case" showcase why devoted listeners tuned in faithfully—the writing was smart, the mystery was genuine, and the performances crackled with authenticity.

Settle into your chair and tune in for an evening of classic detective storytelling. In an age before spoilers and streaming, this is how America discovered mystery and suspense—one perfectly calibrated episode at a time.
