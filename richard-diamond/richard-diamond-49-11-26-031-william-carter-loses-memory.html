# Richard Diamond, Private Detective: "William Carter Loses Memory"

When a desperate man stumbles into Richard Diamond's office with a mind as blank as freshly fallen snow, the gumshoe detective faces his most perplexing case yet. William Carter can remember nothing—not his name, not his past, not even why someone might want him dead. What unfolds is a masterclass in radio noir suspense: Diamond must navigate a labyrinth of false identities, dangerous associates, and shadowy figures who seem far too interested in keeping Carter's memory buried. As the detective peels back layer upon layer of deception, listeners will find themselves drawn into the murky underworld where one man's forgotten past becomes the key to a deadly conspiracy. The tension crackles through your speaker as Diamond races against time, unsure whether his mysterious client is victim or perpetrator, ally or liability.

This November 1949 episode exemplifies why *Richard Diamond* captivated millions of Americans during radio's golden age. Starring David Janssen—who would later become a television icon—the show brought sophisticated, character-driven detective work to the airwaves with a style that influenced countless noir adaptations to come. Rather than relying on gunplay and melodrama alone, the program's writers crafted psychologically complex mysteries that demanded active imagination from listeners. The amnesia plot was a particular favorite of the era's mystery writers, allowing broadcasters to explore questions of identity and trust that resonated deeply with post-war audiences.

Step into Diamond's shadowy world and experience the radio drama that set the standard for detective storytelling. Pull your chair close to the speaker, dim the lights, and prepare yourself for an evening where nothing is quite what it seems and memory itself becomes a weapon.
