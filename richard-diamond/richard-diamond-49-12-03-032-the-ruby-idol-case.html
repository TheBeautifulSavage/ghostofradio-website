# The Ruby Idol Case

Step into the smoky shadows of Richard Diamond's Manhattan office on a December evening in 1949, where a mysterious client arrives clutching a photograph of a priceless ruby idol—and a story drenched in international intrigue. What begins as a simple retrieval job spirals into a tangled web of deception, murder, and double-crosses that will test Diamond's quick wit and quicker trigger finger. As the private eye peels back layers of motive and lies, listeners will find themselves navigating dimly lit docks, exclusive nightclubs, and the sinister apartments of people who'd kill to possess the stolen treasure. The tension crackles through every scene, with David Janssen's smooth, world-weary narration guiding us through a case where trust is the rarest commodity of all.

Richard Diamond Private Detective stands as one of radio's most stylish entries in the detective noir tradition, capturing the post-war American fascination with hard-boiled mystery and masculine sophistication. The show's appeal lay not just in its clever plotting but in Janssen's urbane performance—a protagonist who was equally at home with a martini and a revolver, able to talk his way out of trouble or fight his way through it with equal aplomb. Broadcast during radio's golden age, when the medium still commanded national attention, *Richard Diamond* attracted listeners hungry for sophisticated entertainment that treated them as adults capable of appreciating nuance, innuendo, and morally ambiguous characters.

If you've never experienced the golden age of radio detective fiction, "The Ruby Idol Case" is the perfect entry point—a self-contained adventure that delivers all the atmospheric thrills, clever dialogue, and narrative momentum that made the medium unforgettable. Tune in and discover why audiences huddled around their receivers, utterly captivated.
