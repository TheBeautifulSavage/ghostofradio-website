The Curse Of Shiva

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

When The Shadow whispers, "The weed of crime bears bitter fruit," listeners in 1940 knew they were about to descend into darkness itself. In this bone-chilling installment, "The Curse of Shiva," the Master of Darkness encounters something that may finally be beyond even his supernatural comprehension—an ancient Hindu curse that transforms victims into living instruments of vengeance. As wealthy collectors begin dying in impossible ways, their bodies marked with the sacred symbol of the destroyer god, The Shadow must navigate between the worlds of occult mysticism and cold criminal logic. The script crackles with authentic period paranoia: is this genuine supernatural retribution, or an elaborate murderer manipulating Eastern mysticism to commit the perfect crime? Orson Welles's iconic voice cuts through the radio static, delivering each revelation with mounting dread, while the sound design conjures temples, death rattles, and the inexplicable screams of the doomed.

By 1940, The Shadow had become radio's supreme purveyor of intelligent thrills, far removed from the pulp sensationalism of lesser crime programs. Created by writer Maxwell Grant and brought to vivid life by Welles, the show balanced genuine mystery writing with atmospheric sophistication that elevated the medium itself. "The Curse of Shiva" exemplifies this peak period—before post-war television would eventually dim radio's golden age—when millions of Americans gathered around their sets to experience serialized suspense crafted with remarkable care and artistry.

This is essential listening for anyone seeking to understand why radio drama captivated a nation. Press play, dim your lights, and let The Shadow guide you into the darkness. You'll discover why 1940s audiences couldn't wait until next week's broadcast.
