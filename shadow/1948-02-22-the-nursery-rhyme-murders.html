The Nursery Rhyme Murders

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

Step into the fog-shrouded streets of Manhattan as The Shadow pursues a madman who orchestrates murders with chilling precision—each killing arranged according to the twisted logic of children's nursery rhymes. When victims are discovered posed and positioned to mirror the gruesome lines of "Humpty Dumpty" and "Georgie Porgie," the NYPD finds itself baffled, but The Shadow knows that only a mind steeped in childhood darkness could commit such calculated horrors. Lamont Cranston must penetrate the killer's fractured psychology before the next rhyme reaches its bloody conclusion. Orson Welles's commanding voice cuts through the atmospheric sound design—creaking floorboards, the distant wail of police sirens, and that iconic laugh echoing through the night—as tension builds toward an unforgettable climax.

By 1948, The Shadow had evolved from a mere pulp magazine character into America's most sophisticated crime drama, a show that proved radio audiences craved intelligent mystery fare. This particular episode exemplifies the program's mature approach to psychology and crime, reflecting post-war anxieties about urban violence and the fractured minds it could produce. The writers crafted narratives that treated listeners as thinking adults, weaving complex plots that required genuine attention. The Shadow's popularity during this era represented something remarkable: a mass medium vehicle that refused to talk down to its audience, delivering weekly entertainment that was both thrilling and intellectually compelling.

Tune in to experience The Shadow at its finest—a masterclass in suspense, stellar writing, and the golden age of radio drama. This is mystery fiction as it was meant to be heard, played out in your imagination where fear is most vivid and the unknown lurks just beyond the reach of reason.
