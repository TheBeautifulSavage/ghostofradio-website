The Episode

In "Preview of Terror," the sixth episode of The Shadow's summer season on Mutual Broadcasting System, listeners are plunged into a nightmarish scenario where the lines between reality and illusion blur ominously. The episode opens with an eerie voiceover from Orson Welles as The Shadow: "Who knows what evil lurks in the hearts of men? Only The Shadow!" This iconic phrase sets the stage for a narrative woven intricately around a mysterious artifact known only as the "Mirror of Truth." When a series of bizarre murders start occurring in New York City, each one bearing an eerie similarity to events depicted in ancient myths and legends, Lamont Cranston (the alter ego of The Shadow) is pulled into this supernatural mystery. With his trusted confidante Margo Lane at his side, the duo races against time to uncover the identity of a shadowy figure known as "The Mask," who seems to have access to an otherworldly power that allows him to manipulate reality itself. The suspense builds as Cranston's investigation leads him into the dark underbelly of society, where corruption and deceit reign supreme. The episode masterfully balances the supernatural elements with a gritty depiction of urban crime, creating a tapestry rich in psychological depth and atmospheric tension.

Historical Context

The World of 1949

By June 1949, America was navigating the tumultuous waters of post-war reconstruction and the burgeoning Cold War era, an environment brimming with anxiety and uncertainty. The political climate was dominated by concerns over Soviet aggression and communist infiltration, as Senator Joseph McCarthy's investigations into alleged disloyalty within government institutions began to intensify. Meanwhile, the economy was experiencing a period of transition; while the post-war boom had brought unprecedented prosperity, there were also signs of inflation and labor unrest. Ordinary Americans grappled with issues ranging from housing shortages to racial tensions exacerbated by the ongoing Civil Rights movement. In this context, The Shadow offered not just an escape but also a reflection of contemporary fears through its exploration of themes like corruption, espionage, and the supernatural. Listeners were drawn to the show's ability to encapsulate both the tangible threats facing society—such as nuclear proliferation and the Korean War—and the more abstract anxieties surrounding personal freedom and government surveillance. The episode "Preview of Terror" resonated particularly well because it tapped into a cultural zeitgeist marked by paranoia and a sense of impending doom, mirroring real-world apprehensions about the unknown forces threatening to undermine American democracy.

Why Listen Today

"Preview of Terror" endures as a compelling piece of radio drama not only for its intricate plot and atmospheric tone but also for its innovative use of sound design and voice acting. The episode exemplifies how The Shadow series pushed the boundaries of audio storytelling, employing sophisticated techniques to create vivid mental images that could transport listeners into another realm entirely. Orson Welles' narration, with its deep resonance and commanding presence, serves as a cornerstone for establishing tension and ambiguity, while the interplay between Lamont Cranston and Margo Lane showcases dynamic character development and interpersonal conflict. Furthermore, the episode underscores radio drama's capacity to address complex social issues through allegory and metaphor, making it an art form capable of engaging with—and influencing—the broader cultural conversation. As a testament to its enduring appeal, "Preview of Terror" continues to captivate audiences today for its timeless exploration of morality, power dynamics, and the human psyche in crisis.
