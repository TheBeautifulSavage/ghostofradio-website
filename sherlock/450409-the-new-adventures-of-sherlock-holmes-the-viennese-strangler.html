The fog rolls thick through the London streets as a killer from the Continent brings his deadly trade to England's doorstep. In "The Viennese Strangler," listeners will experience the crackling tension of Baker Street as Holmes and Watson race against time to unmask a murderer whose methods are as methodical as they are sinister. With each strangled victim discovered in the shadowy quarters of the city, the pressure mounts—and only the world's greatest detective possesses the intellect and intuition to connect the threads before another body falls. Expect the sharp snap of dialogue, the eerie atmospheric sound design that made radio's golden age so mesmerizing, and the cat-and-mouse game between Holmes's piercing deduction and a killer who believes himself unstoppable.

This 1940s adaptation represents the pinnacle of old-time radio's commitment to bringing Arthur Conan Doyle's immortal character to vivid life for American audiences. With NBC and CBS bringing the show to listeners nationwide, "The New Adventures of Sherlock Holmes" captured the spirit of a detective who transcended his Victorian origins to speak to modern anxieties about criminal psychology and scientific detective work. The show's writers crafted original cases that felt authentic to Holmes's methodology while offering the kind of international intrigue—a Viennese killer haunting London—that reflected the era's fascination with European mystery and cosmopolitan danger. The stellar cast brought impeccable timing and gravitas to every line.

Don't miss this masterclass in radio drama. "The Viennese Strangler" awaits you in the archives, ready to transport you back to an era when the spoken word could conjure entire worlds of intrigue and suspense.
