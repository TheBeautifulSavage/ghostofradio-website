# The Driven Snow

*Stars Over Hollywood* opens tonight with a tale that cuts to the very heart of American ambition and its ruthless cost. "The Driven Snow" follows a young woman of spotless reputation who arrives in Los Angeles with nothing but her virtue and her dreams, only to discover that in the entertainment capital of the world, both are currency in a dangerous market. As the orchestra swells with strings heavy with foreboding, listeners will find themselves drawn into smoky studio offices, dimly lit hotel corridors, and the glittering yet treacherous parties where fortunes—and innocence—are lost. The protagonist's descent is neither swift nor sensational, but gradual and all the more tragic for it, each compromise smaller than the last until she barely recognizes the woman staring back from her mirror. The veteran cast delivers performances of remarkable subtlety, transforming what could have been melodrama into something far more unsettling: a portrait of how institutions and individuals alike can corrupt through proximity.

This 1948 broadcast represents *Stars Over Hollywood* at its mature best. By the late 1940s, the anthology series had perfected the art of the psychological drama, moving beyond simple tales of success and failure to explore the moral complexities that made the golden age of cinema so compelling—and so compromising. The show's writers drew heavily on genuine stories circulating through Hollywood's darker corners, lending an authenticity that made listeners squirm in their seats. CBS had positioned the program as prestige entertainment for the thinking audience, and episodes like this one justified that positioning entirely.

Tune in this evening as *Stars Over Hollywood* reminds us that not all stories in this town end with fadeouts and credits. Some truths are too uncomfortable to show on screen, but radio—intimate, invisible, demanding only our imagination—can speak them aloud.
