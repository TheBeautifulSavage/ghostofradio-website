# Studio One: Experiment Perilous

When the studio orchestra strikes up that iconic CBS chime on December 16th, 1947, listeners are transported into a world of scientific ambition gone terribly wrong. "Experiment Perilous" unfolds like a fever dream—a tale of obsession where the boundaries between genius and madness blur beneath the cold fluorescent lights of a laboratory. A brilliant researcher stands at the precipice of discovery, willing to push the limits of human endurance and ethical restraint to unlock secrets that nature jealously guards. What begins as clinical exposition soon descends into psychological terror, as supporting players voice mounting dread over their colleague's increasingly reckless methods. The sound design—those eerie ambient hums, the sharp crack of equipment, the quickening pace of dialogue—builds an atmosphere of creeping dread that reaches directly through your radio speaker and grips your imagination.

Studio One represented CBS's answer to the golden age of dramatic anthology radio, showcasing America's finest writers and actors during a pivotal moment when television was still a novelty and radio remained the nation's primary source of evening entertainment. Broadcast during the show's second and final season, "Experiment Perilous" exemplifies the program's sophisticated approach to drama—eschewing cheap thrills for genuine character study and moral complexity. The 1940s were a time when scientific progress symbolized both hope and anxiety in the American consciousness, making this episode's exploration of unchecked ambition particularly resonant with post-war audiences grappling with atomic age uncertainties.

Don't miss this haunting portrait of human limitation and scientific hubris. Tune in for Studio One's "Experiment Perilous" and discover why radio drama still captivates listeners more than seventy years later.
