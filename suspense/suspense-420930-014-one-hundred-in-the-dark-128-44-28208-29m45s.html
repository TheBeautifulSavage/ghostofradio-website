# One Hundred In The Dark

Picture this: a September evening in 1942, your radio glowing softly in the darkness as an ordinary man finds himself trapped in the most extraordinary nightmare. *One Hundred In The Dark* pulls you into a claustrophobic world where a simple wager becomes a descent into psychological terror. Our protagonist accepts a bet to spend one hundred minutes locked in complete darkness—no light, no escape, no company but his own racing thoughts. What begins as a test of nerve becomes something far more sinister as the minutes stretch like hours, and every sound, every shadow of imagination, threatens to shatter his sanity. The sound design is impeccable: the ticking of a clock marking each passing second, the creaking of walls, the whisper of his own breathing becoming unbearable. By the time those one hundred minutes near their end, you'll find yourself holding your breath alongside him, uncertain whether salvation or madness awaits.

*Suspense*, which premiered on CBS in 1942, revolutionized radio drama by proving that audiences craved intelligent, genuinely unsettling entertainment. The show's famous tagline—"tales well calculated to keep you in suspense"—was no empty promise. For two decades, the program dominated Thursday nights with stories that exploited the unique power of radio: the ability to conjure terror through suggestion rather than spectacle. With nothing but voices and effects, *Suspense* accessed something primal in listeners' imaginations, making the invisible far more frightening than anything visible could ever be.

If you've never experienced the golden age of radio drama, *One Hundred In The Dark* is the perfect entry point—a masterclass in mounting dread that proves why millions of Americans gathered around their sets each week. Tune in and discover what genuine suspense really means.
