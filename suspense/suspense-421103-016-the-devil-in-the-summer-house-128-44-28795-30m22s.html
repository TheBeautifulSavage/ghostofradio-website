# The Devil In The Summer House

On a sultry evening in the summer of 1943, CBS listeners huddled around their radios to experience one of the most deliciously unsettling tales ever broadcast: *The Devil In The Summer House*. Picture yourself in a sweltering garden retreat, where the crickets drone and the darkness presses close against the windows—then introduce something far more sinister than the heat. This thirty-minute descent into psychological terror explores the thin veil between reality and madness, as an ordinary family discovers that evil wears many faces, and sometimes it wears the face of someone you trust. The episode's brilliance lies in what listeners *don't* hear: the artful silence, the pregnant pauses, and the haunting orchestral stabs that puncture the humid night air leave more to imagination than any explicit horror ever could.

*Suspense*, which ran from 1942 to 1962, became radio's most enduring thriller anthology precisely because it understood this principle. Created by producer William S. Boyd and directed by a rotating cast of talented craftsmen, the show elevated radio drama into high art. Each week, announcer Jackson Beck's memorable opening—"Suspense!"—promised audiences an encounter with the darkest corners of human experience. The show attracted Hollywood's finest talent, from Orson Welles to Agnes Moorehead, proving that radio drama could be as sophisticated and terrifying as any cinematic production. *The Devil In The Summer House* exemplifies why *Suspense* became a cultural phenomenon, winning multiple awards and spawning imitators that never quite captured its essence.

Step into that summer house. Feel the oppressive heat, the gathering dread, the creeping certainty that something is terribly, irreversibly wrong. *The Devil In The Summer House* awaits—and once you press play, you won't want the lights turned off.
