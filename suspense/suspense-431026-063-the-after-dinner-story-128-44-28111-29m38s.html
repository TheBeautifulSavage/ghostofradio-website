# Suspense: The After Dinner Story

Picture yourself in a well-appointed dining room as the evening stretches long and shadows deepen beyond the candlelight. A gentleman settles back with his brandy, ready to regale his companions with a curious tale—one that begins innocently enough but winds inexorably toward something darker, something that will chill the blood and twist the stomach. In "The After Dinner Story," the microphone becomes your chair at that very table, and you are held captive by a narrative within a narrative, each layer peeling away to reveal something more sinister than the last. The intimate setting makes the horror all the more potent; there is nowhere to turn, nowhere to escape. As the storyteller's voice drops to a conspiratorial murmur, you begin to understand that you are not merely hearing a tale—you are witnessing a confession.

*Suspense*, which thrilled millions of Americans throughout the 1940s and 1950s, represented the gold standard of CBS radio drama. Airing live from Hollywood each week, the show pioneered techniques that would later influence television and cinema, relying entirely on sound design, dialogue, and the listener's imagination to conjure terror far more effective than any visual could achieve. The program's rotating cast of Hollywood's finest actors brought prestige and authenticity to tales of psychological torment, supernatural dread, and moral corruption. "The After Dinner Story" exemplifies the show's mastery of intimate terror—the realization that danger lurks not in dark alleys but in the comfortable spaces we thought were safe.

Tune in now and step into that candlelit room where a dangerous secret is about to be shared. *Suspense* awaits.
