Picture this: a man sits alone in the gathering darkness, nursing a drink with trembling hands. His wife Geraldine—beautiful, devoted, seemingly perfect—moves through the house like a ghost he can no longer trust. In "My Wife Geraldine," the terror isn't lurking in shadows or creeping through fog-shrouded streets; it lives under his own roof, in the knowing glance of someone he promised to love forever. As the twenty-eight-minute drama unfolds across your speaker, you'll discover that the most exquisite suspense blooms not from monsters or murderers, but from the slow, suffocating realization that the person beside you might be something far more sinister than you ever imagined. The tension mounts with each innocuous conversation, each casual touch—is paranoia consuming him, or has Geraldine's mask finally begun to slip?

Suspense, which graced CBS airwaves from 1942 to 1962, became the gold standard of psychological thriller radio, outpacing its competitors through sheer narrative ingenuity and commitment to unsettling its audience. Where other shows relied on external threats, Suspense specialized in the domestic terrors that no locked door could keep out—the betrayals of the heart, the fractures in marriages, the thin line between sanity and obsession. This 1940s episode exemplifies that philosophy perfectly, transforming an ordinary marriage into a pressure cooker of doubt and dread.

Don't miss the opportunity to experience radio drama at its finest. Tune in to "My Wife Geraldine" and discover why listeners huddled around their sets, breath held, waiting for the final devastating revelation. Sometimes the most terrifying story is the one happening next door—or next to you in bed.
