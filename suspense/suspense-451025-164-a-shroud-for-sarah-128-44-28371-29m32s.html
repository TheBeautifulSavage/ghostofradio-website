# A Shroud for Sarah

As the familiar announcer's voice crackles through the static, warning of the terrors that await in the darkness ahead, listeners are drawn into a tale of obsession and the supernatural that unfolds in the fog-shrouded streets of a small New England town. "A Shroud for Sarah" weaves a masterful narrative of a man haunted—literally—by the ghost of a woman he once loved, her spectral presence demanding justice for wrongs committed in life. The episode builds with masterful sound design: the whisper of phantom footsteps, the mournful tolling of distant bells, and the mounting dread in the protagonist's voice as he realizes that some debts can only be paid beyond the grave. What begins as psychological torment transforms into genuine supernatural horror as Sarah's shade becomes increasingly corporeal, increasingly vengeful, leaving listeners uncertain whether they're witnessing madness or genuine communion with the dead.

*Suspense*, which gripped American audiences for two decades on CBS Radio, set the gold standard for dramatic tension in the medium. With its rotating cast of Hollywood's finest talent and scripts that balanced psychological terror with genuine scares, the show proved that radio's greatest power lay not in what could be shown, but in what the listener's imagination could conjure. "A Shroud for Sarah," recorded in the mid-1940s, exemplifies this golden age of radio drama when production values and storytelling craft were at their peak. The episode's exploration of guilt, haunting, and supernatural retribution captured the anxieties of its era while remaining timelessly effective.

Tune in to experience radio drama at its finest—a chilling reminder of why audiences once huddled around their receivers, rapt in terror, as ghosts walked the American night.
