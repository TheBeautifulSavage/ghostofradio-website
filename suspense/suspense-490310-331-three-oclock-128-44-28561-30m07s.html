# Three O'Clock

As the clock tower begins its fateful chimes at the witching hour, a seemingly ordinary evening transforms into a nightmare of paranoia and dread. In "Three O'Clock," an unsuspecting protagonist finds themselves trapped in a web of circumstance where every shadow conceals a threat and every tick of the clock brings them closer to an inexorable doom. The episode masterfully builds tension through intimate sound design—the scrape of a chair, the whisper of wind through an open window, a telephone ringing in the dead of night—each acoustic detail pulling listeners deeper into an atmosphere of mounting horror. As the minutes crawl toward three o'clock, the full weight of the mystery bears down, and what begins as a simple premise spirals into a tale of psychological terror that lingers long after the final dramatic flourish.

*Suspense* stands as one of radio's most enduring achievements, running for two decades and becoming the gold standard for dramatic thriller programming. Premiering in 1942, the series built its reputation on imaginative storytelling and superb acting, attracting top talent and talented writers who understood that true terror lives in what listeners *imagine* rather than what they hear. Each episode was self-contained, allowing the show to explore an endless variety of scenarios—from supernatural hauntings to human betrayal—all grounded in the everyday world of its audience. "Three O'Clock" exemplifies the show's mastery of temporal dread, using the simple concept of time itself as a character in the drama.

Don your headphones and prepare yourself for an evening of genuine suspense. As the familiar opening theme washes over you, you'll understand why millions of Americans huddled around their radios week after week, hanging on every word. Some classics endure for good reason—and *Suspense* remains among the finest.
