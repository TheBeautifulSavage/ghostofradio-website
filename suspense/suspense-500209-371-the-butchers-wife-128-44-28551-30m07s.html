The fog rolls thick through a London street as our story opens, and with it comes a terrible suspicion that will unravel a marriage thread by thread. When a young woman discovers her husband's peculiar habits and late-night absences, she begins to piece together a horrifying possibility—one that transforms their modest butcher shop from a place of commerce into something far more sinister. With each crackling sound effect, each whispered accusation, and each revelation that lands like a cleaver on the cutting board, listeners will find themselves drawn into a psychological thriller where the most dangerous secrets are those kept behind closed doors. The brilliance of this thirty-minute episode lies not in what is explicitly shown, but in what the listener must imagine—a technique that made Suspense the master class in audio terror that kept millions huddled around their radios in the 1940s.

This episode exemplifies why Suspense became one of broadcasting's most celebrated anthology series, running from 1942 to 1962 with stories adapted from literature's darkest corners and original scripts crafted specifically for the medium. The show's genius was understanding that radio's invisible canvas allowed writers and producers to conjure terrors far more potent than any visual could achieve. Featured here is the kind of intimate domestic horror that proved Suspense wasn't content merely to startle—it aimed to unsettle, to make listeners question the trustworthiness of the familiar, to plant doubt in the safest corners of everyday life.

Tune in now to "The Butcher's Wife" and rediscover why an entire nation once sat in delicious dread, clutching armrests as disembodied voices transformed ordinary rooms into chambers of mounting dread. This is radio at its most powerful—where imagination becomes accomplice to fear.
