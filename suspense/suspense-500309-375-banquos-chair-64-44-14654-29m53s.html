# Banquo's Chair

When the curtain rises on this chilling installment of CBS Radio's *Suspense*, listeners find themselves drawn into a macabre parlor game where the line between theater and terror becomes dangerously thin. A skeptical Scotland Yard inspector accepts a audacious wager: he'll sit alone through the night in a chair allegedly haunted by the ghost of a murdered man, surrounded only by shadows and his own fraying nerves. As midnight approaches and the atmospheric sound design—creaking floorboards, distant howling wind, the relentless tick of a grandfather clock—builds to a fever pitch, the question transforms from "are ghosts real?" to "what is the inspector actually seeing?" The episode masterfully plays on the psychological vulnerability of isolation, leaving listeners breathless as reality and supernatural dread become indistinguishable.

*Suspense* earned its place as radio's premier thriller series through precisely this kind of expertly crafted psychological manipulation. Broadcasting throughout the 1940s and into the early 1960s, the show understood that radio's greatest strength was the listener's own imagination—no elaborate sets or special effects required, only expert dialogue, perfectly timed sound effects, and performances that made the invisible tangible. This particular episode exemplifies the golden age of radio drama, when millions of Americans would gather around their sets for thirty minutes of pure, unadulterated tension.

Whether you believe in ghosts or not, *Suspense* invites you into a world where certainty dissolves and fear becomes contagious. Settle into a comfortable chair—but perhaps not the haunted kind—and prepare yourself for *Banquo's Chair*, a masterclass in how radio can reach directly into the listener's mind and touch something primal. This is drama at its most compelling: the unknown made unforgettable.
