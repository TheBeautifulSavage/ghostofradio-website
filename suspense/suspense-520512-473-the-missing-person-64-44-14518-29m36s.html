# The Missing Person

Picture yourself huddled beside the radio on a spring evening in 1945, the amber glow of the dial your only comfort as an ordinary day takes a sinister turn. In "The Missing Person," a woman vanishes without a trace, and the inquiry into her disappearance unspools like a thread through a maze of shadows and half-truths. With each revelation, the walls close tighter—was she fleeing something, or was something hunting her? The cast delivers performances of mounting dread, their voices quavering with uncertainty as they navigate a mystery that grows more labyrinthine with every clue. In less than thirty minutes, you'll be swept into a vortex of suspicion, where every witness becomes a potential suspect and every alibi crumbles under scrutiny. The sound design—footsteps echoing down empty corridors, telephone conversations crackling with tension—weaves an atmosphere so thick you could cut it with a knife.

Suspense, which premiered on CBS in 1942, became the gold standard of radio thriller programming by the mid-1940s, when episodes like this one aired. The show's brilliance lay in its ability to make the mundane terrifying; rather than relying on monsters or the supernatural, it found horror in the everyday lives of ordinary people—a missing person, a chance encounter, a lie that unravels. With production values that rivaled anything in broadcast drama, Suspense commanded millions of listeners each week, proving that the invisible theater of radio could deliver shocks as potent as anything on screen.

Don't let this half-hour slip away unheard. Tune in now and discover what listeners in 1945 discovered: that sometimes the most frightening mysteries are the ones closest to home.
