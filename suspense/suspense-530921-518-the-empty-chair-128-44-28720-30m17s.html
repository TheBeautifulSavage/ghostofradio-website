Picture yourself in a dimly lit study on a fog-shrouded evening, where shadows seem to possess their own sinister intelligence. In "The Empty Chair," Suspense presents a masterwork of psychological terror that begins innocuously enough—a man, a room, a single chair—before spiraling into an abyss of paranoia and dread. As the minutes tick away, you'll find yourself questioning what's real and what exists only in the darkest corners of the human mind. The sound design is exquisite: creaking floorboards, the whisper of fabric, the terrible silence that follows a scream. By the episode's climax, listeners are left suspended in genuine uncertainty, never quite sure whether they've witnessed a ghost story, a descent into madness, or something far more unsettling. This is Suspense at its finest—not relying on cheap shocks, but on the artful manipulation of sound and suggestion to reach directly into your chest and clutch at your heart.

Throughout its remarkable twenty-year run, Suspense distinguished itself as radio's premier anthology of thrills, where each week brought a new nightmare crafted by some of broadcasting's most talented writers and directed with surgical precision. By the 1940s, when "The Empty Chair" aired, the show had perfected the delicate balance between the supernatural and the mundane, between external threat and internal torment. Suspense proved that the most terrifying horrors need never be seen—they need only be heard, and more importantly, imagined.

Don't miss this haunting episode. Tune in and discover why audiences huddled around their radios for two decades, why they checked their locks twice and left lights burning long into the night. "The Empty Chair" awaits—but will you have the courage to sit in it?
