# 'Twas The Night Before Christmas

On a winter's eve thick with malevolence, *Suspense* takes the most sacred night of the year and transforms it into something sinister. This chilling adaptation of Clement Clarke Moore's beloved poem strips away the magic and wonder, replacing it with creeping dread that coils tighter with each passing minute. As snow falls silently outside and families gather around their radio sets expecting warmth and nostalgia, listeners instead find themselves drawn into an unsettling reimagining where St. Nick becomes something far more ominous. The familiar sleigh bells grow discordant; the ho-ho-ho echoes with menace. What begins as a cozy Christmas Eve descends into psychological terror, where every shadow holds danger and trust becomes a liability. This isn't the children's tale you remember—this is *Suspense*, where tradition becomes a weapon and the stockings hung by the chimney with care harbor something altogether unexpected.

*Suspense* dominated American radio throughout the 1940s and 1950s, becoming the gold standard of dramatic thriller programming under the direction of William Spier. The show's brilliance lay in its ability to subvert familiar narratives and tap into primal anxieties lurking beneath everyday life. By choosing to darken one of America's most cherished cultural touchstones, this December 1949 episode exemplifies the show's fearless approach to storytelling—demonstrating how expertly the writers understood that true horror blooms when it invades the spaces we consider safest.

Pull up a chair beside the fire, but keep one ear alert to the darkness beyond. This is radio drama at its finest—professionally crafted, atmospherically rich, and deeply unsettling. *Suspense* awaits, and this Christmas Eve will never quite feel the same again.
