# Going, Going, Gone

When the gavel falls and the auctioneer's voice rises to a fever pitch, ordinary men become desperate. In "Going, Going, Gone," a seemingly routine estate auction becomes the crucible for human desperation, where bids transform into threats and the price of possessions spirals into the price of a life. CBS's *Suspense* masterfully traps listeners in the sweltering tension of an auction house, where every object on the block carries hidden weight—and one fateful sale threatens to expose secrets that men would kill to keep buried. As the broadcast unfolds over its tight twenty-three minutes, the walls close in, voices rise to fevered pitches, and listeners discover that sometimes what's about to be "sold to the highest bidder" is far more valuable—and dangerous—than mere money.

*Suspense* stands as one of American radio's finest achievements, running from 1942 to 1962 with an uncompromising commitment to psychological terror over cheap gimmicks. Created by William Spier, the program pioneered a sophisticated audio approach to thriller storytelling, relying on sharp dialogue, brilliant sound design, and a carefully cultivated sense of escalating dread rather than monsters or jump scares. This 1950s episode exemplifies the show's golden period, when it commanded millions of listeners every week, and its inventive writers mined everyday scenarios—an auction, a business deal, a chance encounter—for their hidden capacity for horror. The program became a training ground for Hollywood's finest talents and influenced everything from noir cinema to modern television drama.

Tune in to *Suspense* and discover why critics hailed it as "radio's finest achievement." In "Going, Going, Gone," the bidding opens at a price no one should have to pay.
