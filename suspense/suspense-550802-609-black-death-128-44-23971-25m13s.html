When the lights dim and that iconic theremin wail pierces the darkness, you know you're about to enter a world where death itself becomes a character in the drama. In this chilling installment of Suspense, the specter of plague—mankind's oldest terror—returns to haunt the living with devastating intimacy. As the narrative unfolds across twenty-five minutes of pure atmospheric tension, listeners are drawn into a claustrophobic nightmare where invisible doom creeps through every shadow, every cough, every shared breath. The sound design conjures medieval pestilence reborn in modern contexts: the tolling of bells, whispered diagnoses, and the creeping dread of contagion spreading through an unsuspecting population. This is Suspense at its most primal, exploiting not monsters or madmen, but the very real horror of disease and the fragile veneer of civilization stripped away by collective panic.

Throughout its remarkable twenty-year run beginning in 1942, CBS's Suspense established itself as the gold standard of American thriller radio—a program where top-tier talent, sophisticated sound engineering, and psychological horror converged nightly. The series thrived by grounding supernatural and sensational plots in recognizable human vulnerability, transforming everyday situations into nightmares through expert pacing and the intimate medium of radio itself. "Black Death" exemplifies this approach, wielding historical dread as a vehicle for exploring contemporary anxieties that audiences in the 1940s understood all too well.

This is required listening for anyone seeking to understand how golden-age radio built suspense without visual tricks—only voices, sound, and the terrifying power of suggestion. Settle in, dim your own lights, and discover why millions of listeners made Suspense an appointment with fear. Your pulse will thank you for the workout.
