When the lights dim and that unmistakable Suspense theme pierces the evening air, listeners know they're about to enter a world where trust becomes treachery and friendship masks something far more sinister. In "A Friend To Alexander," the drama unfolds with the deliberate pacing that made this series legendary—a seemingly innocent relationship between two men begins to curdle with dread as the true nature of one man's intentions becomes horrifyingly clear. The sound design crackles with menace: footsteps in shadowed rooms, the ominous pause before a revelation, voices trembling with barely concealed anxiety. By the final moments, what began as a cordial encounter transforms into something deeply unsettling, leaving listeners to contemplate the danger lurking beneath the surface of ordinary human connection.

Suspense aired during radio's golden age, when the intimate nature of the medium—voices speaking directly into the listener's ear—created an unparalleled power to unsettle and terrify. The show, which ran for twenty years on CBS, became America's premier thriller program by refusing cheap tricks in favor of psychological depth. Each episode was meticulously crafted by writers and directors who understood that true horror emerges from character and circumstance rather than monsters or mayhem. "A Friend To Alexander" exemplifies this philosophy, drawing its tension from the universal human fear: not knowing who you can truly trust.

This nearly thirty-minute gem represents Suspense at its finest—a masterclass in building dread through dialogue and performance. Whether you're a devoted fan of classic radio or a newcomer curious about why millions once gathered around their sets each week, this episode is an essential listen.
