# Suspense: "Daisy Chain"

As the familiar shadow-dripping theme cuts through the static, you settle into your chair for an evening of delicious terror. "Daisy Chain" draws you immediately into a web of innocent-seeming connections—a chain of small favors, casual conversations, and chance encounters that tighten inexorably around an unsuspecting victim. The episode unfolds with the relentless logic of a nightmare, where each link in the chain seems harmless in isolation, yet together they forge shackles of inescapable doom. The sound design envelops you in ordinary spaces—a coffee shop, a street corner, a telephone line—that become sinister precisely because nothing about them seems sinister at all. You'll find yourself wondering who is orchestrating this invisible machinery, and whether the protagonist can break free before the final loop closes.

*Suspense*, which debuted in 1942, became CBS Radio's crown jewel of theatrical horror, running for two decades and spawning some of broadcasting's most unforgettable moments. Created by William Spier and featuring the incomparable voice talent of the era, the show pioneered a distinctly American gothic sensibility—less concerned with monsters and the supernatural than with the terror lurking in everyday life. Each episode leveraged radio's unique power to inhabit the listener's imagination, using silence, whispered dialogue, and dissonant sound effects to unsettle in ways that visual media could never match. Episodes like "Daisy Chain" exemplify the show's mastery: a story of psychological entrapment that proves more chilling than any ghost story.
