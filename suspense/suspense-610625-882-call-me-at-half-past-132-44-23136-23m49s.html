# Call Me At Half Past

When the telephone rings at precisely half past the hour, no one suspects it will herald a descent into psychological terror. *Suspense* draws you into an ordinary American home where a simple arrangement—a mundane agreement to check in by phone—becomes the thread holding together a life balanced on the knife's edge of madness. As minutes tick away and the appointed call time approaches, dread accumulates with agonizing inevitability. What should be a reassuring ritual transforms into an instrument of torture, each silent moment between rings a small eternity of doubt and fear. The sound design of this 1940s masterpiece uses the very technology meant to connect us—the mechanical ring, the heavy receiver, the hollow silence of an empty line—to isolate and unsettle. By the episode's conclusion, you'll understand that sometimes the most terrifying threats aren't monsters in shadows, but the fragile constructs of trust and sanity we build in the dark.

*Suspense* ran for two decades as CBS's gold standard of dramatic terror, and episodes like this demonstrate why. Premiering in 1942, the show pioneered the art of psychological horror, featuring stories that lingered in listeners' minds long after the final organ chord faded. Where other programs relied on cheap scares and obvious villains, *Suspense* excavated the horrors buried within human nature itself—jealousy, paranoia, desperation, and obsession. The show's stellar cast and meticulous sound design created an intimacy that radio uniquely provided; in your living room, through your speaker, the terror felt personal and immediate.

Don't let half past the hour pass without experiencing this chilling installment. Tune in, turn down the lights, and discover why *Suspense* earned its place as radio's most celebrated thriller series. Some calls, after all, are far too important to miss.
