When the telephone rings late at night, who answers? In this chilling installment of CBS's Suspense, a desperate plea crackles through the darkness—"Please believe me"—and with those four words, a listener's comfortable evening transforms into a labyrinth of doubt and dread. A voice on the line claims to be someone the protagonist knows intimately, yet something feels terribly wrong. Is this person truly who they claim to be, or has something sinister replaced them? As the conversation unfolds over twenty-five minutes of mounting tension, the line between trust and terror blurs. The episode masterfully exploits the intimate nature of radio itself—the disembodied voice, the inability to see one's conversation partner—turning a simple phone call into a gateway to psychological horror. Every pause carries weight, every inflection of the voice becomes a clue in this intimate nightmare.

Suspense earned its reputation as radio's most dependable purveyor of terror during its two-decade run, delivering stories that proved the imagination far more potent than any visual effect. Broadcasting from 1942 to 1962, the program became synonymous with the golden age of radio drama, when millions would gather around their sets seeking thrills and chills. Each episode was crafted with meticulous attention to sound design—creaking doors, distant screams, the subtle suggestion of danger—all designed to unsettle and captivate an audience that trusted the medium's power to transport them into darkly compelling worlds.

If you crave an evening of genuine suspense, where mystery and menace bloom from the simple act of listening, tune in to "Please Believe Me." This is radio drama at its finest—no special effects, no cheap tricks, just expert storytelling that proves sometimes the most terrifying things are those we cannot see.
