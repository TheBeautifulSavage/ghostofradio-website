As the CBS orchestra swells and that unmistakable announcer's voice cuts through the static—"Suspense!"—listeners are pulled into a shadowy world where ordinary people confront extraordinary darkness. In "With Murder In Mind," the tension builds from the very first moment: a mind preoccupied with a single, terrible thought. Someone is planning murder, and as the minutes tick away in that claustrophobic radio drama, you'll find yourself trapped in the killer's psychology, listening to motivations unfold and circumstances collide toward an inevitable, violent conclusion. Your imagination becomes the true stage, conjuring horrors far more personal and terrifying than anything seen on screen.

For twenty years, Suspense reigned as CBS's crown jewel of terror, the show that proved radio could deliver genuine chills to millions of Americans gathered around their living room sets. Born in 1942 during the golden age of dramatic broadcasting, the program became legendary for its sophisticated storytelling, stellar cast of Hollywood actors, and producer-director William Spier's masterful control of pacing and suspense. Each week, the show explored not monsters or supernatural forces, but the darkness lurking within human nature itself—the capacity for betrayal, greed, and murder that lives in the most respectable citizens. Episodes like "With Murder In Mind" showcased why the show's influence on American entertainment remains undiminished decades later.
