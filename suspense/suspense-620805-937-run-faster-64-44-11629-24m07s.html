# Suspense: Run Faster

When the studio lights dimmed and that iconic theremin wail pierced the airwaves on August 5th, 1962, CBS listeners settled in for a masterclass in mounting dread. "Run Faster" unfolds like a nightmare made manifest—a ordinary person thrust into extraordinary peril, pursued by forces both seen and unseen. As the title suggests, this episode transforms the simple act of running into an obsession, a desperate flight that becomes increasingly surreal and terrifying. The sound design crackles with footsteps, heavy breathing, and the relentless percussion of a hunter closing in. By the twenty-four-minute mark, listeners find themselves as breathless as the protagonist, caught in the inexorable logic of fear itself. This is Suspense at its finest: not relying on supernatural gimmicks, but on the primal human terror of being hunted.

For two decades, Suspense commanded the Thursday night prime-time slot with psychological thrillers that transcended the medium's limitations through sheer narrative craft and stellar voice acting. In an era before television's visual spectacle, the show understood that terror lives in the mind's eye—in what listeners *imagine* lurking in the shadows. "Run Faster" exemplifies the show's mature approach to horror, where the real enemy is often the human capacity for cruelty or obsession. These episodes were recorded live before studio audiences, adding an electric spontaneity that polished scripts could never quite capture. By 1962, Suspense had become an American institution, proving that radio drama's golden age wasn't fading—it was evolving into something more sophisticated and unsettling.

Tune in to experience why generations of listeners kept their hands poised over the radio dial. In the dark of evening, with only your imagination for company, discover why "Run Faster" remains one of Suspense's most unforgettable journeys into terror.
