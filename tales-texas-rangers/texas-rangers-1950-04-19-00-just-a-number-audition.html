# Texas Rangers: "Just A Number" (April 19, 1950)

When you tune into this April evening broadcast, you'll find yourself in the dust-choked streets of a sleepy Texas town where a young drifter's identity becomes his death sentence. "Just A Number" opens with the unmistakable crackle of gunfire and the keen wail of sirens—the signature sound that millions of Americans knew meant trouble was brewing in the Lone Star State. As Ranger Captain McNally and his steady partner navigate the case, they uncover a web of mistaken identity and cold-blooded vengeance that culminates in a breathtaking climax. The episode's brilliance lies in its moral complexity: is a man responsible for crimes committed under another's name? The sound design is superb—footsteps echoing down empty boardwalks, the metallic click of handcuffs, and that distinctly 1950s radio orchestration that turns an ordinary crime drama into pure American gothic.

This audition episode represents something crucial in broadcasting history. *Tales of the Texas Rangers* emerged at the golden age of radio drama's twilight, arriving just as television began its inevitable ascent. Yet NBC and the show's producers created something that refused to be overshadowed—authentic, gritty crime stories grounded in real ranger folklore and contemporary police procedure. The series would go on to captivate audiences for two seasons with its commitment to realism and moral seriousness, proving that radio drama still had vital stories to tell.

Settle in with the static and let your imagination populate this unfolding mystery. "Just A Number" is radio drama at its finest—compelling, expertly crafted, and utterly immersive. This is what America listened to on spring evenings when the world felt both smaller and stranger than ever before.
