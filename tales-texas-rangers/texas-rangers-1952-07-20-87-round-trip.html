# Texas Rangers: Round Trip

When the dust settles on a dusty Texas back road, a traveling salesman lies dead—his wallet untouched, his sample case rifled. Rangers Jace Pearson and Clay Morgan find themselves caught in a web of deception that stretches across two states and back again, following a trail of aliases and false identities that leads them to question everything they thought they knew about their prime suspect. In "Round Trip," the Rangers must race against time and their own assumptions to separate fact from fiction, discovering that the most dangerous criminals are often those who know how to hide in plain sight. The tension crackles throughout this taut seventy-minute episode as the investigation moves from railroad depots to roadside diners, each stop revealing new complications and dangerous twists that test the Rangers' legendary detective skills.

*Tales of the Texas Rangers* occupied a unique position in early 1950s radio drama—it was one of the few westerns grounded explicitly in contemporary crime-solving rather than frontier gunslinging. Drawing its authority from actual Texas Ranger case files, the show's creators crafted stories of modern law enforcement where procedure and persistence mattered as much as a quick draw. By 1952, the program had cultivated a devoted audience who appreciated the procedural realism mixed with compelling human drama. This particular episode exemplifies what made the series special: intelligent storytelling that respected both the Rangers' methodology and the listener's intelligence.
