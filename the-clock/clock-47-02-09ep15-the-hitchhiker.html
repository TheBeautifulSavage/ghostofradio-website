# The Clock: "The Hitchhiker"

A lone motorist picks up a drifter on a rain-slicked midnight highway, and within minutes, the driver's certainty about reality begins to unravel. Is his passenger merely an opportunistic vagrant, or something far more sinister? As the miles accumulate and the conversation grows increasingly unsettling, listeners will find themselves gripping the edge of their seats, wondering along with the protagonist whether he's encountered a dangerous criminal, a supernatural entity, or simply a mirror reflecting his own hidden guilt. The Clock's sound design creates an oppressive atmosphere—the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of the car's engine, the whisper of tires on wet pavement, dissonant string arrangements that creep under the skin—turning a simple ride into an existential nightmare.

*The Clock* occupied a distinctive niche in radio's golden age, premiering on NBC in 1946 when audiences hungered for sophisticated psychological drama in compact, episodic form. Rather than relying on recurring characters, each fifteen-minute installment presented a self-contained mystery that explored the darker corners of ordinary life—the uncanny lurking beneath suburban normalcy. "The Hitchhiker" exemplifies the show's mastery of claustrophobic tension; confined to a car with nowhere to escape, the driver (and by extension, the listener) experiences mounting dread as conventional explanations collapse. The episode's ambiguous conclusion—never quite confirming what really happened—became the show's trademark, leaving audiences debating the truth long after the final musical sting.

Turn your dial to *The Clock* and discover why discerning radio enthusiasts still consider it essential listening nearly eighty years later. This particular episode proves why the show earned its devoted following: it transforms the familiar into the frightening, and makes strangers on the road seem infinitely more dangerous than any monster lurking in the shadows.
