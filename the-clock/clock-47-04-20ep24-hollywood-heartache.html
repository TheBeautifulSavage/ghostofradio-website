When the clock strikes in the Hollywood hills, it marks not just the passage of time but the unraveling of dreams. In "Hollywood Heartache," listeners are transported to the glittering yet treacherous world of 1940s Tinseltown, where a starlet's disappearance sets off a chain of revelations that no one saw coming. As our narrator guides us through dimly-lit soundstages and the opulent homes of studio executives, we discover that fame's golden promise often conceals darker bargains. The episode crackles with tension—snappy dialogue, the distant wail of police sirens, and a jazz-inflected score that captures the desperation of those chasing stardom. By the climax, listeners will find themselves entangled in a mystery where appearance and reality prove as malleable as celluloid itself.

The Clock, which aired from 1946 to 1948 on NBC, stands as a masterclass in the anthology mystery format, and "Hollywood Heartache" exemplifies why the show became a must-listen for radio enthusiasts. With each episode running approximately 30 minutes, the series demonstrated how tight writing and stellar voice acting could craft fully realized worlds in the span of a commercial break. The show's unique conceit—that every mystery culminates precisely when the clock chimes—created a trademark tension that kept audiences riveted. The Hollywood episode, in particular, captured postwar America's simultaneous fascination and disillusionment with the film industry, making it as relevant to contemporary listeners as it was in April 1947.

Don't miss this gem of American radio drama. Tune in to The Clock as it reveals the hidden cost of Hollywood's glamour—a reminder that some prices can never be repaid in fame or fortune.
