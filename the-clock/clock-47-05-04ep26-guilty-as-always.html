The Clock NBC · May 4, 1947

Clock 47 05 04ep26 Guilty As Always

· GHOST OF RADIO ·
0:00 --:--

When the chimes of the Clock strike midnight on this tense spring evening, listeners are drawn into a web of circumstantial evidence and moral ambiguity that will keep them riveted to their sets. A man accused of a crime he swears he didn't commit finds himself cornered by facts that seem damning, witnesses that seem credible, and a past that seems to condemn him. As the clock's methodical ticking punctuates each revelation, the tension mounts—is justice about to be served, or is an innocent man about to pay for crimes he never committed? The skilled cast delivers dialogue with the kind of naturalistic urgency that only live radio could achieve, their voices conveying the desperation of someone watching their life crumble under the weight of suspicion.

The Clock was NBC's answer to the puzzle-mystery craze of the mid-1940s, and this particular episode exemplifies why the show developed such a devoted following. Rather than rely on exotic locales or superhero antics, The Clock grounded its stories in the ordinary world—small towns, city apartments, everyday people facing extraordinary moral dilemmas. By 1947, when this episode aired, radio audiences had grown increasingly sophisticated, demanding mysteries that challenged them intellectually. "Guilty As Always" delivers exactly that: a narrative constructed with clockwork precision, where every detail matters and nothing is quite what it seems.

Settle in for twenty-five minutes of pure dramatic tension as The Clock takes you back to an era when radio could make your imagination do all the heavy lifting. This is storytelling at its finest—no laugh tracks, no commercials breaking the mood, just voices in the darkness and the inexorable ticking of justice's mechanism.
