When the clock strikes midnight on October 16th, 1947, listeners are drawn into a shadowy world where a simple domestic life unravels with terrifying speed. "Hazel" presents the chilling tale of a woman whose comfortable routine becomes a cage of suspicion and fear. As the clock ticks relentlessly forward—that signature sound effect that defined the program—we follow Hazel through an ordinary evening that transforms into something far more sinister. The mystery unfolds with the deliberate pacing characteristic of The Clock, building tension through overheard conversations, meaningful silences, and the creeping realization that nothing is quite as it seems in her own home. By the episode's conclusion, listeners will have witnessed a masterclass in dramatic irony, where the audience knows far more than poor Hazel herself.

The Clock occupied a unique space in radio's golden age, premiering on NBC in 1946 and quickly establishing itself as one of the finest mystery anthology programs of its era. Unlike shows that relied on supernatural elements or spectacular crimes, The Clock found its power in the intimate domestic sphere—where secrets hide behind closed doors and danger wears a familiar face. The program's meticulous sound design and narrative structure influenced countless subsequent mystery shows. "Hazel" exemplifies what made The Clock essential listening: the recognition that ordinary people in ordinary circumstances can find themselves caught in extraordinary moral and physical danger.

Don't miss this gripping episode from radio's greatest mystery anthology. Tune in as The Clock reminds us that time—and fate—wait for no one. Experience the tension, the revelations, and the haunting conclusion that have made this 1947 broadcast a lost gem of American radio drama.
