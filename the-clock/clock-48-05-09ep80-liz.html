# The Clock - "Liz" (May 9, 1948)

When the great clock tower of the city strikes midnight, mystery stirs in the shadows. In this taut episode, listeners will follow the desperate case of a woman named Liz—a figure caught between desperation and deception, where every tick of that omniscient timepiece marks another moment closer to her reckoning. What begins as an ordinary night unfolds into a labyrinth of secrets, false identities, and the kind of moral ambiguity that made *The Clock* essential listening for millions of Americans. The tension builds like a spring wound too tight, with sound effects that crackle through your living room speaker: footsteps echoing down rain-slicked streets, a telephone ringing unanswered, whispered conversations in dimly lit diners. By the episode's climax, you'll find yourself wondering whether Liz is victim or perpetrator—or perhaps both.

*The Clock* occupied a unique place in radio's golden age, arriving in 1946 just as America was settling into peacetime and grappling with noir-tinged anxieties about urban life, identity, and justice. Where other anthology shows trafficked in the supernatural or the sensational, *The Clock* grounded its dramas in plausible, often unsettling human conflict. Each episode unfolds across a single night, held together by the city's great clock—that impassive witness to everything humanity does in the dark. The show's writers crafted stories that reflected postwar America's moral complexity, exploring themes of displacement, desperation, and the thin line between innocence and guilt.

This is radio at its most hypnotic—a story you won't be able to turn away from, even as the hour grows late and the clock continues its relentless ticking. Tune in and discover why listeners couldn't resist answering the call of *The Clock*.
