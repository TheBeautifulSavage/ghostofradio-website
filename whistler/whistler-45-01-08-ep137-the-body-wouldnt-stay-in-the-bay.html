# The Body Wouldn't Stay in the Bay

When you tune in to *The Whistler* this evening, prepare yourself for a tale of murder most stubborn—where a killer's carefully laid plans unravel not through detective work, but through the simple refusal of a corpse to stay hidden. The fog rolls thick over the bay as our protagonist discovers that disposing of evidence is far more complicated than the crime itself. With each passing hour, the body drifts back to shore, each discovery pulling our killer deeper into a web of desperation and fear. The Whistler's haunting theme cuts through the darkness, accompanied by his cryptic laugh, as he guides us through a labyrinth of deception where nature itself becomes the unwitting accomplice to justice. This is noir at its finest—intimate, claustrophobic, and deliciously tense.

*The Whistler* captured the hearts and anxieties of 1940s America during its thirteen-year run on CBS, offering listeners an escape into shadow-filled narratives of crime, fate, and moral reckoning. Unlike the boisterous detective shows of the era, *The Whistler* was subtle and psychological, relying on masterful sound design and understated performances to generate genuine dread. The show's mysterious narrator—the Whistler himself—seemed to exist outside the story, observing human weakness with an almost supernatural detachment. Episode 137, preserved from January 1945, represents the show at its peak, when post-war audiences craved stories about hidden crimes and the inevitable consequences of transgression.

Don't miss this haunting journey into the criminal mind. Adjust your dial to the frequency of *The Whistler*, where fate always has the final say.
