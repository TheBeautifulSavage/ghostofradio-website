# The Whistler: "Backlash"

In the fog-shrouded streets of a city where trust is currency and betrayal the going rate, a man's past comes calling with a vengeance. "Backlash" pulls listeners into a web of blackmail and desperation where every shadow hides a secret, and every confession conceals a deeper lie. As the mysterious Whistler's eerie signature tune fades into the night, we find ourselves trapped alongside a desperate protagonist who thought he'd buried his sins—only to discover that some ghosts refuse to stay buried. The tension mounts with each revelation, each accusation, each moment of terrible recognition. By the time this sordid affair reaches its inevitable conclusion, you'll understand why The Whistler became America's most riveting late-night companion.

The Whistler occupied a unique space in radio's golden age, arriving in 1942 when wartime anxieties made noir's cynical worldview feel almost comforting in its honesty. Unlike more theatrical mystery programs, The Whistler trafficked in psychological realism and moral ambiguity—plots driven not by elaborate murders but by the corrosive weight of human weakness. Each episode's narrator, that cryptic Whistler himself, served as a modern Greek chorus, appearing at the episode's end to comment on the moral wreckage that had preceded him. This particular broadcast from 1947 exemplifies the show's mastery: tight writing, stellar voice acting, and sound design that conjured entire locations from nothing but clever acoustics and a few well-placed effects.

Don't miss "Backlash"—turn down the lights, lean close to your speaker, and let The Whistler remind you why radio once held Americans spellbound. Some thrills simply cannot be improved upon by picture or screen.
