HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

It was the late 1950s, a time of great change and uncertainty in America. The Cold War was at its height, with tensions between the US and Soviet Union running high. Many Americans feared that the world was on the brink of nuclear war, and the threat of communism loomed large over the horizon. Meanwhile, the European theater was still reeling from the devastation of World War II, and the rebuilding process was slow and difficult.

In this context, "Lulu" taps into the collective fears and anxieties of its time. The story's themes of obsession, deception, and betrayal are classic tale of psychological suspense, echoing the fears of a world on the edge. By exploring these darker aspects of human nature, the episode offers a unique perspective on the post-war era, one that is both thought-provoking and unsettling.

Radio drama was particularly well-suited to this time period, as it offered a cheap but effective means of escapism for audiences looking to forget their troubles. With its ability to transport listeners to another world, radio became an ideal medium for tackling complex themes and emotions in a way that film or theater could not. As such, "Lulu" represents the perfect example of how radio drama can engage and captivate audiences, even today.
